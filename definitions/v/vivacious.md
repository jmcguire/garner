# vivacious

vivacious; **voracious**. Both words date from the 1600s. *Vivacious* describes someone who is animated, brisk, lively, and sprightly—full of vivacity \<a vivacious dancer\>. *Voracious* describes a person or animal that is ravenous and greedy in eating—or else someone extremely eager in some particular pursuit \<a voracious appetite\> \<a voracious consumer\>. A *voracious reader* is one who reads widely and ardently, with a seemingly insatiable appetite for the written word. It's such a common collocation as to be a set phrase.

In a stupendous example of word-swapping, *vivacious* infrequently displaces *voracious* in this phrase, the result being the malapropism \**vivacious reader* (rather hard to picture without an exaggerated oral delivery). Especially common in obituaries, it often appears elsewhere as well. One doubts the accuracy of this statement made by a college student in a news release: "I'm an overachiever in language arts. I was always a *vivacious reader* \[read *voracious reader*\]." In any event, although the error can be found in older texts, it has been much on the rise during the 21st century—e.g.:

 - "Olga is very studious. She is a *vivacious* \[read *voracious*\] reader." John D. Loscher, *The Bolsheviks* 225 (2009).

 - "She said students had transformed from nonreaders to *vivacious* \[read *voracious*\] readers." Meredith Clisby, "Program Assists Literacy Rates," *Canberra Times*, 6 Sept. 2011, at A27.

 - "As a *vivacious* \[read *voracious*\] reader, she will skim through many novels for inspiration, reading the last page first, until she checks out one or two for home reading." Jeremy Bauer-Wolf, "Carroll Libraries Promoting Creative Writing Programs for Teens," *Advocate Eldersburg & Sykesville* (Md.), 19 Nov. 2013.
