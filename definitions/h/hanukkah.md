# Hanukkah

Hanukkah; **Chanukah**; **Hanukah**. The spelling of the Jewish Festival of Lights—an eight-day celebration that falls in November or December—has long been troublesome. Today, though, *Hanukkah* is much more common in AmE and BrE print sources than the next-most-common variants, *Chanukah* and *Hanukah*. It also generates almost three times as many hits on internet search engines as *Chanukah*, and ten times as many as *Hanukah*. The festival's name has been spelled many other ways as well. It's a matter of tricky transliteration: how to represent some Hebrew sounds that do not correspond to any letter or combination of letters in the Latin alphabet.

The beginning sound is especially troublesome, being not quite an /h/ sound nor a /ch/ sound but what phoneticians call a *voiceless fricative*. \(*Chutzpah* begins with the same sound.\) The sound is represented in phonetics by a symbol resembling a connected *k* and *h*. The Yiddish linguist Max Weinreich \(1894–1969\) transliterated the word as *khanike*, but added that in English the spelling should be *Hanukkah*.

That spelling certainly predominates in AmE. It is also becoming more common in BrE, which has traditionally preferred the *Ch-* variant.

Current ratio in print \(*Hanukkah* vs. *Chanukah* vs. *Hanukah*\): 12:3:1
