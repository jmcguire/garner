# headquarters

headquarters. Since the 1800s this noun has commonly taken a plural verb, as in the first two examples listed below. But the singular predominates when the reference is to a building, as in the third example, or to authority \(as opposed to a place\), as in the fourth example:

• "Heather Cook \[is the\] manager of public relations for SurfControl, whose U.S. *headquarters are* in Scotts Valley, Calif." Morgan Lee, "General Services to Add Internet Filters," *Albuquerque J.*, 11 Dec. 2000, at 4.

• "The company's world *headquarters are* in Ramat-Gan, Israel." "Technology," *Atlanta J.-Const.*, 18 Jan. 2001, at C2.

• "The Louisiana Lottery Corp.'s new state *headquarters is* still on the drawing board, but it's already turning heads." Scott Dyer, "Lottery Building Bends with Downtown Decor," *Advocate* \(Baton Rouge\), 17 Dec. 2000, at B1.

• "Its *headquarters is* in Tide Point." Stacey Hirsh, "High-Techs Learn Some Old Rules," *Baltimore Sun*, 21 Jan. 2001, at P5.

Of course, if you're torn by this dilemma, you can usually recast the sentence <the company has its headquarters in St. Louis>.
