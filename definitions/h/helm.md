# helm

helm, vb. As a verb, *helm* was originally an 18th-century nautical term meaning "to steer." It has been borrowed by the entertainment industry in the metaphorical sense "to direct or produce \(a film, play, album, etc.\)." This extended sense, now entrenched in showbiz talk, is likely to strike many readers as newfangled and catchpenny—e.g.:

• "The multiple-director system works sometimes, as witness Zucker/Abrahams/Zucker *helming* the hits ‘Airplane\!' and ‘Ruthless People.' " "The Movies: Blank Checks," *L.A. Times* \(Mag.\), 27 May 1990, at 22.

• "Shadowy friend Steve Albini will be *helming* Schneider's second solo effort, and has tentatively assembled bands to alternately back the quirky vocalist." Kieran Grant, "Shadowy Doings," *Toronto Sun*, 26 Aug. 1995, Entertainment §, at 38.

• " ‘La Promese,' a study of immigration *helmed* by brothers Luc and Jean-Pierre Dardenne, won the Golden Spike award for best film." John Holland, "Euro Indies Give Fest a Big Boost," *Variety*, 4–10 Nov. 1996, at 20.
