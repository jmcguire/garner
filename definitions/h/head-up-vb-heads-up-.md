# head up, vb.; heads-up, n. & adj

head up, vb.; heads-up, n. & adj. A. *Head up*, vb. This phrasal verb has been voguish in business and academic jargon since the 1970s—e.g.:

• "Nigel Wilson, a transit expert at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, will *head up* the panel." Doug Hanchett, "MBTA on Board for Review of Silver Line Buses," *Boston Herald*, 22 Oct. 2000, at 9.

• "At Rocco, Ranelin will *head up* a superb band that includes greats John Heard on bass and Roy McCurdy on drums and rising pianist Danny Grissett." Zan Stewart, "Valley Life Ticket to Slide," *L.A. Times*, 8 Dec. 2000, at B5.

• "Reddy, of the school of computer science, *heads up* something called the Aura project." Steve Hamm, "Net Culture," *BusinessWeek*, 11 Dec. 2000, at 102.

**B. *Heads-up*, n. & adj.** As a noun, this casualism has been a voguish substitute for "warning" since the 1980s—e.g.: "[Louis W.] Uccellini said this new forecasting method should . . .give local governments a *heads-up* to get out their snow plows and call in their snow-removal crews." Scott Burke, "Better Forecasts in the Future?," *Capital* \(Annapolis\), 17 Nov. 2002, at A1. As a phrasal adjective, it is informal for "alert"—e.g.: "Franco Wakhisi tied it at 1–1 in the 73rd minute with a *heads-up* play." Will Parrish, "Eagles Meet Coastal for BSC Title, NCAA Berth," *Herald* \(Rock Hill, S.C.\), 17 Nov. 2002, at D3.
