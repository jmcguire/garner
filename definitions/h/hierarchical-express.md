# Hierarchical Expressions

Hierarchical Expressions. However egalitarian we may think we are, certain expressions are still considered appropriate only from superior to subordinate or vice versa. They connote, even if they don't exactly denote, that a hierarchy exists between speaker and listener. English is not, to be sure, a language that teems with expressions peculiar to either men or women. Such languages do exist: in some, there is a terminological distinction in how even such basic ideas as *hello*, *goodbye*, and *thank you* are said by men and by women. In these languages, many terms are thought to be idiomatically proper only to one sex.

But the subject here is not gender at all: it is the entirely distinct idea of social rank. Some things are considered appropriate only from one rank to another, without reciprocity. Easy examples come in the form of self-abasement. *My pleasure, boss\!* \(AmE\) and *Pleasure, Guv'nor\!* \(BrE\) are often used in the service industries. Throughout the U.S., direct references to a customer as *boss* surged into vogue in the second decade of the 21st century among hotel doormen, bellhops, and skycaps—primarily people who derive a significant part of their income from gratuities. Acknowledge the higher rank of the person being served, the logic seemed to run, and you're likely to receive a larger tip. But the same phenomenon could also be seen with people not customarily given tips, such as convenience-store clerks.

Analogous situations of recognizing superior rank arise more overtly in courts \(*Your Honor*\), in embassies \(*Your Excellency*\), in executive agencies \(*Yes, Madam Secretary* \[old-fashioned AmE\], *Yes, Minister* \[BrE\]\), and in the seat of government \(*Yes, Mr. President* \[AmE\], *Yes, Prime Minister* \[BrE\]\). Traditional etiquette books brim with protocols for "forms of address" in communicating with people holding high positions.

The more interesting everyday examples relate to situational awareness of one's linguistic place. Normally these arise in relationships such as those of parent/child, adult/minor, teacher/pupil, and employer/employee. In each of those pairings, it might be acceptable for the first \(superior\) in the dichotomy to say to the second \(subordinate\), *I'm proud of you* or *I'm disappointed in you*. Only in exceptional circumstances could the second say such a thing to the first without seeming somehow insubordinate. Likewise, imperatives are more usual with the first \(*Have a seat*, *Turn to page 49*, *Go ahead*, etc.\). To the extent that any such idea might be conveyed from a lower-ranking person to a higher, it would be couched as a polite request or even a question \(*Would you like to have a seat?* *If you would, please look at page 49.* *Would you like to go ahead?* etc.\). All sorts of things can be said by the higher-ranking person to the lower—e.g.:

 - At ease \(in the military\).

 - Don't delay.

 - Get it done pronto.

 - Get to it.

 - I'm counting on you.

 - Make it so.

 - Shape up.

Sometimes there are much subtler distinctions. The verb *befriend*, for example, has traditionally connoted not just "to make friends with" but "to give favor, aid, or benefit to"—meaning that the higher-ranking befriends the lower-ranking, not vice versa. Or equals can befriend each other, but a lower-ranking person cannot idiomatically or appropriately be said to "befriend" a higher-ranking person.

Of course, it's possible for the higher-ranking person to subvert expectations in self-mockery or similar jocularity, as by saying to a subordinate *At your service*, *How may I be of service?* or *Right away\!* The existence of such examples doesn't undercut the principle; rather, the humor works only *because* of the principle.
