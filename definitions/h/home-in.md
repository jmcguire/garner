# home in

home in, not *\*hone in*, is the traditional and still preferred phrase. In the early 20th century, the metaphor referred to what homing pigeons do; by the early 20th century, it referred also to what aircraft and missiles do.

And by the late 20th century, some writers had begun mistaking the phrase by using the wrong verb, *hone* (= to sharpen) instead of *home*—e.g.:

 - "While Mr. Bradley *honed* \[read *homed*\] in on healthcare, Mr. Gore scolded his opponent as lacking a comprehensive education plan and for supporting a school voucher program when he was in the Senate." Richard L. Berke, "Bradley, in Debate with Gore, Goes on the Attack," *N.Y. Times*, 18 Dec. 1999, at A12.

 - "The knowledge gained from these conferences informs the intervention and helps the instructional team *hone* \[read *home*\] in on the specific strategies that will help the students move forward." Carol Collier Kuhlthan et al., *Guided Inquiry: Learning in the 21st Century* 120 (2007).

 - "British and American agents began to *hone in* \[read *home in*\] on his location and activities." Heribert von Feilitzsch, *The Secret War on the United States in 1915* 209 (2015).

In modern print sources—both AmE and BrE—the collocation *homing in on the ~* predominates over *\*honing in on the ~* by a 2-to-1 margin. For more on these competing phrases, see p. xii.
