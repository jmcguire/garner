# hegemony

hegemony /hi-**jem**-ә-nee/ is a fundamentally political term \("political dominance; the leadership or predominant authority of one state of a confederacy, union, or region over the others"\) that has been imported into commercial and nonpolitical contexts. E.g.:

• "Influential men began to expand the old-fashioned and physiologically specific definition of masculinity into an overwhelming concept implying social and \(especially important at Harvard\) intellectual *hegemony*." Susan Jacoby, "What Makes a Real Man?," *Newsday* \(N.Y.\), 15 Dec. 1996, at C36.

• "Clearly, the new peace process on all three tracks—Syrian, Palestinian and moderate Arab states—is shaping to play out as part of the wider struggle between Iran and the U.S. for regional *hegemony*." Leslie Susser, "An Ambush on the Way to Peace," *Jerusalem Rep.*, 20 Aug. 2007, at 14.

The word verges on being a vogue term.

The corresponding adjective is *hegemonic* /hej-i-**mon**-ik/—e.g.: "As history shows, *hegemonic* empires almost automatically elicit universal resistance, which is why all such aspirants eventually have exhausted themselves." Christopher Layne & Benjamin Schwarz, "U.S. Can Become Less of a Target," *Times Union* \(Albany\), 18 Nov. 2002, at A7.
