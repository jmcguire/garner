# hijack

hijack. Vehicles and airplanes are *hijacked*, not people. E.g.: " ‘It's horrifying, because it's like a kidnapping,' said Greg Britt, a 34-year-old language instructor from Atlanta who was *hijacked* \[read *abducted* or *held up*\] in a cab at knife point last year, then ordered to make 12 separate withdrawals from automatic-teller machines—six before midnight and six after the new day began, when he was able to withdraw more." Steve Fainaru, "Mexico's Risky Ride: The Hazards of Hailing a Cab in Capital," *Boston Globe*, 6 Feb. 1997, at A1. Cf. **skyjack**.

The word is often misspelled *\*highjack*—e.g.:

 - "Buildings are bombed and planes are *highjacked* \[read *hijacked*\]." Anita G. Nicholls, "Jerry Falwell Isn't This Nation's Problem," *Richmond Times-Dispatch*, 28 May 1997, at A8.

 - "The need for such work became clear to Karadja in December 1994, during the *highjacking* \[read *hijacking*\] of an airplane in which her daughter and sister were on board." Scott Peterson, "Amid Algeria's Massacres," *Christian Science Monitor*, 9 Jan. 1998, Int'l §, at 1.

## Language-Change Index

 - *\*highjack* for *hijack*: Stage 1
 - Current ratio in print \(*hijack* vs. *\*highjack*\): 29:1
