# honoree

honoree; **honorand**. In the 1800s, these two forms sprang up, both denoting a person who receives an honor. Both words are acceptably formed. Until 2014, the *OED* recorded only *honorand*, which predominates in BrE but appears only occasionally in AmE—e.g.: "But as President Harry S. Truman's newly appointed secretary of state, Marshall was one of 12 *honorands* at the following year's Commencement." John T. Bethell, "The Ultimate Commencement Address," *Harv. Mag.*, May–June 1997, at 36. In AmE today, *honorand* is no better than a needless variant because *honoree* has taken the field—e.g.:

 - "Among the *honorees* in 10 categories are Cambodian sculptor Chum Sambath, conductor David Wilson and Wade Hobgood, dean of the College of the Arts at Cal State Long Beach." Steven Linan, "Morning Report," *L.A. Times*, 14 Oct. 1997, at F2.

 - "The evening, hosted by the Arkansas Game and Fish Foundation, began with the Governor's Reception for the *honorees* and their families." Cary Jenkins, "100 Years on Record," *Ark. Democrat-Gaz.*, 30 Aug. 2015, at 40.

Current ratio in print \(*honoree* vs. *honorand* in World Englishes\): 1.6:1
