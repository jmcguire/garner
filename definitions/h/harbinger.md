# harbinger

harbinger /**hahr**-bin-jәr/ \(= a sign that something will soon happen\), a noun introduced in the 1100s, had more than 25 spellings in Middle English. But since the 1600s, the settled spelling has been *harbinger*. Although none of the old spellings put an *r* after the *b*, since the 1950s writers have often mistakenly written \**harbringer*—perhaps from a false association with *bring*. Meanwhile, the term is often mispronounced /**hahr**-bing-әr/ or even /**hahr**-bring-әr/—losing the hard /j/ sound of the final syllable.

Since the 1600s, the term has also been used as a verb—a synonym of *presage*. That usage has long been standard. But the misspellings and mispronunciations of the word cannot be said to harbinger a heightened degree of punctilio among speakers and writers generally.

 - Current ratio in print \(*harbinger* vs. \**harbringer*\): 1,905:1
