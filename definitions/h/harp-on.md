# harp on

harp on \(= to talk or write about persistently and tediously\) is frequently miswritten *\*hark on*, especially in BrE. Whereas *harp on* usually takes a direct object <kept harping on his foibles>, the erroneous phrasing *\*hark on* often occurs in the collocation *\*hark on about* \(as in the second and third examples below\)—e.g.:

• "It's about stitching the club into the fabric of the community for years to come, rather than *harking on* \[read *harping on*\] times past." Michael Gannon, "The Dons of a New Era," *Daily Herald* \(Glasgow\), 18 Apr. 2013, at 30.

• "He's at a different club now and no one will move forward if they keep *harking on about* \[read *harping on*\] the past." Ian Rush, "Five Things Rodgers Must Do to Turn Season Around," *Irish Independent*, 13 Dec. 2014, at 75.

• "You're probably sick of hearing about them, to be fair. But Conor O'Brien is worth *harking on about* \[read *harping on*\]." "More from the Village Folk," *Daily Mirror*, 17 Apr. 2015, at 12.
