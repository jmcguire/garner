# bequest, n

bequest, n.; \***bequeathal**; **\*bequeathment**. *Bequest* = \(1\) the act of bequeathing; or \(2\) personal property \(usu. other than money\) disposed of in a will. As a classic example of word-swapping, *bequest* is sometimes confused with *behest*. See **behest**.

*\*Bequeathal* and *\*bequeathment* are needless variants of sense 1 of *bequest*—e.g.:

 - "What is known about Lillian Thomas Pratt is that she changed the face of the Virginia Museum, with a *bequeathal* \[read *bequest*\] that placed the commonwealth on the artistic map of the world." Sibella C. Giorello, "Egg Money: Lillian Thomas Pratt Gathered Some of the Finest Faberge Pieces," *Richmond Times-Dispatch*, 18 Aug. 1996, at J1.

 - "Martha \[Boyle\] receives this *bequeathment* \[read *bequest*\] as her family prepares to leave for its annual trip to Cape Cod—and one of the oceans that Olive never saw." Claire Martin, "Realizing How Many Lives One Person Can Touch," *Denver Post*, 19 Oct. 2003, Books §, at EE2.

 - "Last updated in 1993, before the U.S. political lexicon had acquired ‘soccer moms' \(1996\), ‘fuzzy math' \(2000\) and ‘Swift Boat spot' \(2004\), the book's newest version includes rich linguistic *bequeathals* \[read *bequests*\] from both the Clinton and second Bush White Houses." Katie Baker, "How to Sound Presidential," *Newsweek*, 24 Mar. 2008, at 17.
