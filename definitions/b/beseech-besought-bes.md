# beseech > besought > besought

beseech > besought > besought; **beseech > *\**beseeched > \*beseeched**. The traditional past tense and past participle is *besought*. The simple past is the more common usage—e.g.:

 - "She tried to comfort me, *besought* me to come to breakfast." Lincoln Steffens, "Even Santa Claus Can't Put a Pony into a Stocking," *Star Trib.* \(Minneapolis\), 25 Dec. 1997, at E1.

 - " ‘Let history treat me kindly,' California Democrat Maxine Waters, a deep-seated opponent of impeachment, *besought* shortly after the first article was approved." Craig Gilbert, "Historic Votes Send House 3 Articles of Impeachment," *Milwaukee J. Sentinel*, 12 Dec. 1998, at 1.

 - "One evening, my sister *besought* a blessing on Jimmie Morton, the red-headed boy in the corner house who had a brown puppy and chewed bubble gum." John Gould, "Praying for Mother to Stay," *Christian Science Monitor*, 15 Jan. 1999, at 23.

The form *besought* will surprise some readers, who might have thought the past form to be *\*beseeched*, which the *OED* labels "now regarded as incorrect." It is a fairly common solecism—e.g.:

 - "The presiding officer *beseeched* \[read *besought*\] speakers to avoid ‘personally abusive' comments about the president of the United States." David M. Shribman, "Debate Shows the Roundup of Resentments," *Boston Globe*, 19 Dec. 1998, at A12.

 - "McClelland frequently *beseeched* \[read *besought*\] the society to accept the design that he obsessively continued to modify." Mary Kay Ricks, "Washington Monuments That Never Quite Made It," *Wash. Post*, 13 Jan. 1999, at H3.

 - "Coats had a powerful backer in former U.S. Rep. Louis Stokes, who *beseeched* \[read *besought*\] the Democratic Party to open countywide offices to black politicians." James Ewinger & Joe Hallett, "Mason's Biggest Foe in Prosecutor Bid May Be Memories," *Plain Dealer* \(Cleveland\), 15 Jan. 1999, at B1.
