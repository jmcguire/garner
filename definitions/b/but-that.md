# but that

but that; **but what**. These peculiar idioms, used in expressions of doubt, may strike the modern reader as quaint or affected. *But that*, the more literary of the two, is used today most commonly in negative constructions—e.g.: "I do not doubt *but that* you are disappointed." Most readers would find the *but* in that sentence to be superfluous and would translate the sentence to read, "I do not doubt *that* you are disappointed." *But what* is used in the same way, but it's rarer and more colloquial—e.g.: "I don't know *but what* it might be the best thing for my own special interest, which is bicycling." Don Harvey, "A Changing Cycle in Traffic Patterns," *L.A. Times*, 18 Aug. 1996, at B6.
