# burnish

burnish (= \[1\] to polish to a shine, esp. by rubbing; or \[2\] to improve \[a reputation, an image, etc.\] through hard work), a French loanword dating from the 1300s, appears in various literal phrases (*burnished armor*, *burnished copper*, *burnished gold*, *burnished steel*, etc.) and figurative ones (*burnishing her résumé*, *burnishing his credentials, burnishing their image*). Occasionally the word appears in a malapropism caused by word-swapping with *burn*. The error occurs most often when writers intend any of several set phrases such as *to burn into (one's) brain*, *heart*, *memory*, *mind,* etc., but for some reason resist using the verb *burn*—e.g.:

 - "These are trifles, you realize, compared to the one law that is now *burnished* \[read *burned*\] into your brain." Ora Mackey, "The Family That Goes Parasailing Together," *L.A. Times*, 21 Oct. 1993, at 23.

 - "The Tuesday morning that the Twin Towers came down will remain *burnished* \[read *burned*\] into the hearts and minds of the nation." Ron Barnett, "Challenger Memories Still Bring Pain 25 Years Later," *Greenville News* (S.C.), 28 Jan. 2011, News §.

 - "Save it forever, *burnish* \[read *burn*\] their names in your memory, and plot out the pettiest revenge you can." Roxanne Gay, "The Sting of the Careless Email Chain," *N.Y. Times*, 21 Nov. 2021, at 4.
