# \*but nevertheless

\*but nevertheless. This redundancy has been fairly common in the language since the late 1600s. But it is a redundancy nevertheless. Usually you can drop either word. *Nevertheless* is typically the one to drop—e.g.: "Val Kilmer probably agrees, *but nevertheless* \[read *but*\] he's committed to an attempt to regain control." Stephen Hunter, "Stars Reveal a Normal Side," *Baltimore Sun*, 4 Apr. 1997, at E1.

If special emphasis is needed, either *but . . . still* or a construction with *though* or *although* is the better choice—e.g.: "In Hanover, conditions are not as bad *but are nevertheless* \[read *but are still*\] considered taxing." Alexander Reid, "Wanted: New Police Stations," *Boston Globe*, 2 Apr. 1995, South Weekly §, at 1. Cf. **\*but yet**.
