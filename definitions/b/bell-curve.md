# bell curve

bell curve (= a graph line representing the usual statistical distribution of frequency—so called because it resembles the profile of a church bell \[flared at the bottom\]) dates only from the early 1940s. Earlier terms were *bell-shaped curve* (1900) and *normal curve* (1894). By far the most frequently occurring of these terms is *normal curve*, but it is used in a great variety of contexts \<the normal curve of the earth's surface\> \<the pitcher threw his normal curve\>. *Bell-shaped curve* was more common than *bell curve* until the mid-1990s, when the shorter phrase leapt ahead—probably as a result of the notoriety received by the 1994 book *The Bell Curve: Intelligence and Class Structure in American Life*, by Richard J. Herrnstein and Charles Murray.
