# banister

banister \(= a handrail supported by vertical posts running usu. along a stairway\), a noun dating in English from the 1600s, was originally a corruption of *baluster* \(= a vertical post supporting a handrail\). In the early 1800s, *banister* was decried by some as "improper" and "vulgar," but as the *OED* notes, it had by then "already taken literary rank and . . . acquired general acceptance." By then, it had moved to Stage 5 in the Language-Change Index. Meanwhile, it underwent differentiation so that it denoted something quite distinct from *baluster*—not merely a single post but the handrail running atop and connecting all the posts. *Balustrade*, meanwhile, has come to denote the balusters and the banister collectively. \(See **baluster** second paragraph.)

In the 1600s and early 1700s, the predominant spelling was \**bannister*. During the 1700s, the two spellings \(*banister* and \**bannister*\) competed. Not until the 1820s did *banister* become the standard spelling across World Englishes.

 - Current ratio in print \(*banister* vs. \**bannister*\): 5:1
