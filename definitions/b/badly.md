# badly

badly.

**A. And *bad*.** With a linking verb such as *is*, *feels*, *seems*, or *tastes*, the predicate adjective *bad* is required, not the adverbial complement *badly*—e.g.:

 - "But the punishment hardly seems to fit the ‘crime' as presented, and both teacher and students come off looking *badly* \[read *bad*\]." Eric Ries, "Rip-Snortin' Romp," *Techniques*, 1 Feb. 2000, at 76.

 - "A good rule of thumb is: If a mushroom looks, smells, or tastes *badly* \[read *bad*\], do not eat it." Art Judd, "Mushroom Madness," *Santa Fe New Mexican*, 21 June 2001, at C1.

See **feel \(c\)**.

Language-Change Index

*badly* after a linking verb <\*feel badly> <\*looked badly after the loss>: Stage 2
Current ratio in print \(*felt bad* vs. *\*felt badly*\): 13:1

**B. In the Sense "very greatly" or "very much."** This use, as in *badly in need*, was formerly criticized. Having become established in the early 20th century, it is perfectly idiomatic in AmE—e.g.: "Democrats demanded concessions in a bill tightening immigration laws, another measure Republicans want *badly*." Dave Skidmore, "Congress Fights Budget Demons," *Chattanooga Times*, 28 Sept. 1996, at A4. But cf. **awful**.

Stick to *badly* \(not *bad*\) in this adverbial sense. You might expect a professional athlete to say, for example, " ‘We need him *bad*, we need him real *bad*.' " Joe Donnelly, "Ecstasy, Agony," *Newsday* \(N.Y.\), 17 Dec. 1993, at 247 \(quoting Herb Williams, a basketball player\). But an announcement about a local symphony function should probably be more refined: "If you can meet these criteria, then the people who put on the Symphony Style Show need you *bad* \[read *badly*\]." Douglas Imbrogno, "In Search of a Fashionable Dog," *Charleston Gaz.* \(W. Va.\), 8 Sept. 1994, at D1.

See adjectives \(e\).

Language-Change Index

1. *badly* for *very much* <badly in need>: Stage 5

2. *bad* for *badly* <we need some paper bad>: Stage 2
Current ratio in print \(*need it badly* vs. *\*need it bad*\): 1.4:1
