# barque

barque /bahrk/ \(= a sailing ship usu. having three to five masts\), a French loanword dating in English from the 1400s, has from the beginning competed closely with the alternative spelling *bark*. Etymologically, according to the *OED*, the word was probably identical to *barge*. It is also related to *embark* and *disembark*, both dating from the 1500s.

In both AmE and BrE, the phrase *masted barque* has been more common than *masted bark* since about 1900. Likewise with *sailing barques* over *sailing barks*.

Given the several unrelated senses for *bark* \(= \[1\] the outer covering of a tree; \[2\] the yelp of a dog; \[3\] any similar sound, as in human speech; and \[4\] a chocolate or similar candy baked in sheets and then broken into pieces\), it would be advantageous to the language to use *barque* for ship-related senses. But the two spellings coexist in these uses, and many dictionaries \(including the *OED* and Merriam-Webster products\) list *bark* as the main headword for ship-related senses. The word's orthographic history is untidy. So make your choice and be consistent. Cf. **debarkation** & **embarkation**.
