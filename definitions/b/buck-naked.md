# buck naked

buck naked; **\*butt naked**. Both phrases mean "completely undressed." The original and standard expression, dating from the 1910s, is *buck naked*—e.g.:

• "With Lucille Ball flair she torches houses and locks herself out of the house *buck naked*." Tom Cunneff et al., "My Favorite Housewife," *People*, 24 Jan. 2005, at 38.

• "The current centerfolds, *buck naked* though they may be, communicate almost no suggestion of anything. In Playboy pinups, one is not looking for the note of the divine that one finds in the Venuses of ancient statuary, let alone for the pathos of Rembrandt's nudes." Joan Acocella, "The Girls Next Door," *New Yorker*, 20 Mar. 2006, at 144.

The origin of *buck naked* is a matter of speculation, and many theories are just folk etymology. \(See etymology \(d\).\) The most prevalent guess is that *buck* derives from Old English terms for male deer, goats, and some other animals. In northern Europe, the skin of bare buttocks, presumably, was considered pale and smooth like buckskin. Another theory is that *buck* refers to the racist term for a Black man—especially in reference to enslaved men, who were stripped naked for potential buyers to inspect them before an auction. The real answer is probably lost to time.

The form *buck naked* has traditionally been much more common in edited publications than *\*butt naked*. The more graphic version does appear, usually in reported quotations but sometimes not—e.g.:

• "Burnett and CBS aren't stupid. They needed people to start buzzing about Survivor again, as they did when some fat guy named Richard Hatch won $1 million after walking around *butt-naked* \[read *buck-naked*\] for most of the show." Kevin D. Thompson, " ‘Survivor' to Play a Sensitive Game," *Palm Beach Post*, 10 Sept. 2006, TV Post §, at 3.

• "He indeed is out there, as in *butt-naked* \[read *buck-naked*\] out there, standing with his fishing pole . . . \[in\] full exposure to the sun." Steven Slosberg, "What's This Fisherman's Angle, Fresh Catch or Bare-Bottomed Attention?," *Westerly Sun* \(R.I.\), 15 Aug. 2021, at C1.

Outside edited publications, though, the more graphic *\*butt naked* has outranged the standard phrase in popular usage. As in the title of the last-cited example, *butt naked* is more fundamentally evocative.
