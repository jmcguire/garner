# biographee

biographee \(= the subject of a biography\), though a seeming neologism, actually dates from 1812, but it's still a relative newcomer compared to *biographer* \(1644\). Of course, many English words ending in *-graphy*, such as *biography*, *calligraphy*, and *cartography*, have agent nouns ending in *-er* even though there is no corresponding verb. But the *-ee* noun *biographee* is anomalous, just as \**calligraphee* and \**cartographee* \(seemingly impossible forms\) would be. \(See -ee \(a\).) Still, the word can be useful, especially in book reviews—e.g.:

• "The *biographees'* years of birth and death, places of birth, parentage and/or Welsh ancestors are indeed valuable genealogical data." Lloyd Bockstruck, "The Raisin: More than a Shriveled Grape," *Dallas Morning News*, 21 Oct. 1995, at C10.

• "But it's clear that the roles of biographer and *biographee* have been correctly assigned." Desson Howe, " ‘Basquiat': Spray-Painted Valentine," *Wash. Post*, 16 Aug. 1996, at N31.

• "There is none of that grotesque gap between style of biographer and style of *biographee* that can plague the form." Nicholas Lezard, "Sifting Fact and Fiction in Updike's Life and Work," *Evening Standard*, 24 Apr. 2014, at 44.
