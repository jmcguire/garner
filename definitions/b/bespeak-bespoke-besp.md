# bespeak > bespoke > bespoken

bespeak > bespoke > bespoken. So inflected (but see the exception below). This Old English verb, dating from the 800s, today means (1) "to engage, reserve, or claim beforehand" <that table is bespoken>; (2) "to request" <I must bespeak a favor>; (3) "to signify, indicate, or suggest" <living conditions that bespeak hoarding>; or (4) "to foretell or foreshadow" <the early results bespeak a victory>. In these senses, the inflected forms hold true <she bespoke a favor> <his firm handshake had bespoken confidence> <his eagle on the first hole bespoke an excellent round of golf>.

There's an important exception. For something custom-made (clothing, software, etc.), *bespoke* is the past-participial adjective <a bespoke suit> <a bespoke computer system>. It also describes a person or firm engaged in custom-made articles <a bespoke clothier>. This usage dates back to the early 1600s.
