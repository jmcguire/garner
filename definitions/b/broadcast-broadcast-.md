# broadcast > broadcast > broadcast

broadcast > broadcast > broadcast. So inflected, like the verb *cast* itself. In his book *Words and Rules* \(2000\), Steven Pinker condemned Theodore Bernstein, the estimable author of *The Careful Writer*, for his 1965 preference for the past-tense *broadcast* over *\*broadcasted*. Pinker said that this preference "had long been a losing battle," and quoted H.L. Mencken in support of *\*broadcasted*. Back in 1936, Mencken said that *\*broadcasted* "is what one commonly hears." Yet with only five minutes' work on the westlaw database, it was possible in 2000 to show that in modern print, the ratio staggeringly favors *broadcast* \(19,805 sources\) and not *broadcasted* \(86\) as a past-tense form. \(More recently, Google ngrams show the ratio in World Englishes to be still quite overwhelming.\) Bernstein was right in 1965, and Pinker should not have relied on a 1936 quotation as his sole evidence against Bernstein—a *New York Times* editor with his ear firmly to the ground—whom he accused of "hectoring people into sticking with the irregular."
