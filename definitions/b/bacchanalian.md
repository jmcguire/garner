# bacchanalian

bacchanalian /bahk-ә-**nay**-lee-әn/ (= of, relating to, or involving a party with uncontrolled revelry, excessive consumption of alcohol, and usu. sex), a Latin loanword dating in English from the 1500s, is the usual adjective. *Bacchanal*, *bacchic*, and *bacchant* are variants. All derive from *Bacchus*, the Greek god of wine.

The term *bacchanal* is also a noun denoting (1) a worshiper of Bacchus, (2) a drunken reveler, (3) a Roman festival in honor of Bacchus, (4) a song or dance in honor of Bacchus, or (5) a drunken party or orgy. Senses 1 and 2 of *bacchanal* are shared by the noun *bacchant* (or, in its feminine form, *bacchante*). Sense 3 of *bacchanal* is shared with *bacchanalia* (often capitalized).

*Bacchanalia* (the Roman festival in honor of Bacchus) is a Latin plural traditionally construed in English with a plural verb \<the bacchanalia were riotous\>. But during the 20th century, it came to be treated as a mass noun that takes a singular verb \<the bacchanalia was riotous\>.

Given the frenzied orgies and disorderly merry-making involved with these words, one can perhaps understand their fuzzy boundaries. In bacchanalian events, boundaries are often disregarded.

 - Current ratio in print (*bacchanalian orgies* vs. *bacchanal orgies*): 16:1
 - Current ratio in print (*bacchanalia was* vs. *bacchanalia were* \[case-insensitive search\]): 1:1
