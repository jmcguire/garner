# \*cause . . . is due to

\*cause . . . is due to. This prolix phrasing emerged in English in the late 1860s, reached its peak of popularity about 1910, and has since tapered off in print. But the redundancy still appears with some frequency—e.g.: "*The cause of crime is largely due to* \[read *Crime results largely from* or *Crime is largely due to*\] the loss of individual character." Cal Thomas, "Morals, Not Money, Cure Crime," *Cincinnati Enquirer*, 9 Aug. 1994, at A6. See **due to**.
