# critique

critique, n. & v.t. Until recently, this word was almost always a noun. But in the late 1900s \(beginning especially in the 1980s\), the verb became quite common as a neutral equivalent to the word *criticize*, which has always had negative connotations. In fact, though, the verb *critique* dates from the mid-1700s.

The verb is conjugated *critiqued* and *critiquing*. Some writers mistakenly write *\*critiqing* \(perhaps from the influence of *cataloging* \[see **catalogue**])—e.g.:

 - "The sometimes animated *critiqing* \[read *critiquing*\] . . . is intended only to help a 24-year-old quarterback get better." "Holmgren: Favre Not Perfect," *Capital Times* \(Madison\), 25 Oct. 1993, at B3.

 - "While lacking any sexually graphic details, Ruth gets downright dirty while describing, and unavoidably *critiqing* \[read *critiquing*\], her meals with Andrews." Braden Keil, "Terminal Gets Some Dim Sum," *N.Y. Post*, 11 Apr. 2001, at 39.
