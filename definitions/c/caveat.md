# caveat

caveat \(L. "let him beware"\) means, in ordinary speech and writing, merely "a warning"—e.g.:

 - "One *caveat*: Don't keep topiaries out of a sunny spot for too long." Martha Stewart, "Make an Ornamental Statement—Shape a Topiary," *Dayton Daily News*, 17 Oct. 1996, Homelife §, at 7.

 - "This reportedly would help about 10 percent of women. However, there are a few *caveats*. No drinking. And there's a risk of low blood pressure and fainting." Ann McFeatters, "Your Government at Work," *Ark. Democrat-Gaz.*, 22 Aug. 2015, at 16.

The word derives from the Latin phrase *caveat emptor* \(= let the buyer beware\), which is widely used—e.g.: "The old home-buying legal rule used to be ‘*caveat emptor\!*' \(let the buyer beware\). But in 1984 a California Court of Appeal was the first to hold a home seller and real estate broker liable for damages to the buyer for failure to disclose unstable soils which caused severe home damage." Robert Bruss, "Disclosure, Insurance Help Sellers Avoid ‘Bad-House' Lawsuits," *Chicago Trib.*, 7 Dec. 1996, New Homes §, at 2.

The traditional pronunciation of the word /**kay**-vee-aht/ is far less common today than /**kav**-ee-aht/.
