# curb

curb; **kerb**. *Curb* is the AmE spelling; *kerb* is a BrE variant. And BrE makes an interesting distinction: the physical sense of a raised stone or concrete edge for a road or path is a *kerb*, while anything that metaphorically halts or checks is a *curb*. That's because the figurative use derives not from *curbstones*—or *kerbstones*—but from a type of bit used to check an unruly horse. E.g.:

 - "The chances are you would stumble before even stepping off the *kerb*." "Computers That Run Themselves," *Economist*, 21 Sept. 2002, Tech. Q. §, at 26.

 - "In Germany, the government wants to encourage more part-time working and remove *curbs* on self-employment." Tony Major, "Germany Drags Europe Down," *Fin. Times*, 27 Feb. 2002, at 4.

So in BrE, the verb *curb* is common, but *kerb* rare. AmE uses *curb* in all senses.
