# Count Nouns and Mass Nouns, Essay

Count Nouns and Mass Nouns. Count nouns are those that denote types of individual objects that can be counted \(*crane*, *party*, *minivan*, *child*\). They have plural forms \(*cranes*, *parties*, *minivans*, *children*\) and can occur with numerals and similar determiners \(*three cranes*, *several parties*, *two minivans*, *three children*\). Mass \(noncount\) nouns denote concrete or abstract stuff that cannot be enumerated \(*wine*, *insurance*, *courage*, *mud, crockery*\) and generally aren't used in the plural. It is meaning rather than form that determines the count/mass distinction: many nouns with more than one sense can be both count <he gave several talks> and mass <talk is cheap>. Almost any count noun can be coerced into denoting a kind of stuff <this car smells of dog>, and almost any mass noun denoting some kind of stuff can be used to denote a variety of that stuff <fine wines> <different muds on the two suspects' shoes>. On neologistic plurals for mass nouns, see plurals \(i\).
