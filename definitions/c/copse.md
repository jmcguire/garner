# copse

copse; **coppice**. In the mid-1700s, Samuel Johnson defined *coppice* as "low woods cut at stated times for fuel; a place overrun with brushwood." (*A Dictionary of the English Language* \[1755\].) In the etymological note, he added: "It is often written *copse*." In other words, *coppice* (a Latinized spelling) and *copse* (an anglicized phonetic spelling arrived at through the linguistic process known as syncope) are the same word. Both spellings date from the 1500s, and modern desktop dictionaries define the term as "a thicket, grove, or growth of small trees" (*W11*) or "a thicket of small trees or shrubs" (*WNWCD*).

But the two leading desktop dictionaries disagree about which spelling predominates: *W11* puts the main entry under *coppice*, while *WNWCD* puts it under *copse*—each with a cross-reference to the alternative spelling. So which lexicographic team has it right? Merriam-Webster or Webster's New World? The latter. *Copse* greatly outnumbers *coppice* in print today. And so we debouch from the linguistic thicket. Cf. **bosk**.

Finally, a tip for scatologically minded word-lovers: many of the most weirdly cognate and thoroughly obscene, cloacal, and stercoraceous words in the English language appear in unabridged dictionaries between *coppice* and *copse*.

 - Current ratio in print (*copse* vs. *coppice*): 4:1
