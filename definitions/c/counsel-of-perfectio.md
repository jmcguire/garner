# counsel of perfection

counsel of perfection = (1) orig., advice of Jesus on ways to strive for perfection, given in Matthew 19; (2) consequently, the virtues of obedience, chastity, and charity appearing in the vows of monastic orders (also called *evangelical counsels*—so pluralized); or (3) a noble but unattainable ideal.

While the phrase itself is not especially common in general use, sense 3 is the most widespread today—e.g.:

 - "A professor at my seminary, Jacob Rader Marcus, once said, ‘It may well be that we cannot love our neighbor as we love ourselves—that is a *counsel of perfection*—but the least we can do is to tolerate him and his differences.'" James A. Gibson, "Muslims Grievously Offended by Caricatures of Mohammed Are Justified," *Pitt. Post-Gaz.*, 12 Feb. 2006, at J1.

 - "Prime Minister Stephen Harper is quite right that the solution to the Syrian refugee crisis lies in the end of the Syrian civil war. But that's a *counsel of perfection* that takes us nowhere." "Syria, Russia and the Assads," *The Globe & Mail*, 19 Sept. 2015, Editorial §, at F9.

The phrase is furthest from perfection when it merely denotes casual advice—e.g.: "In Cantonese Roasted Duck ($14.95), the skin had been rendered almost free of fat, while remaining richly flavored. In a *counsel of perfection*, some of the meat was a little dry but tender and revealing solid depth of flavor." John Batchelor, "This Experience Is Worth the Wait," *News & Record* (Greensboro), 17 Aug. 2006, at GT27. One wonders at the writer's intention there.

For more on this type of phrase (borrowed from literature), see literary allusion.
