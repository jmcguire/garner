# center around

center around. Beginning in the 20th century, usage critics took issue with this phrasing. The disapprovers invoked logic \(and sometimes geometry\) in arguing that something can *center on* or *revolve around* something else but cannot *center around* it, given that a center is a single point. A few defenders pointed out that a city center isn't a single point, and there's nothing wrong with saying a historic neighborhood *centers around* a church—which certainly sounds idiomatic. Even if there were something illogical about the wording, the idiom grew in frequency after its first recorded use in 1893: "the plot invariably centres round the love intrigue of persons in the middle or upper classes of life" \(*OED*\). The *OED* contains no explicit note about the phrasing.

The literature on this point begins with a mention in 1931. \(Lascelles Abercrombie, "Colloquial Language in Literature," 2 *S.P.E. Tracts* 1181, 1182 \[1931\].\) Since then a great profusion of disapproval has appeared, of which the following is only a small sampling:

 - **1935:** "*Centre round.* Here, in my judgment, is another offence which is increasing and ought to be abolished. . . . If I am wrong it would be correct to say that the stars centred round the sun. But do they?" A.P. Herbert, *What a Word\!* 92 \(1935\).

 - **1943:** "The widely used colloquialism *center around* is seen upon analysis to be ridiculous. The word *around* contradicts the idea of *center*. Say *center in* not *center around*." John B. Opdycke, *Don't Say It* 162 \(1943\).

 - **1956:** "The verbs *centre round* and, less frequently used, *centre around* and *centre about*, are bad formations. To ‘centre' means ‘place in a centre,' ‘mark with a centre,' or, figuratively, ‘be concentrated in.' One thing can go ‘round,' or center ‘in' or ‘on' or ‘at' another thing, but . . . how can anything *centre round* something else that already has a centre?" V.H. Collins, *Right Word, Wrong Word* 25 \(1956\).

 - **1957:** "*Centre round* is threatening to establish itself as a legitimate idiom; but it represents an odd confusion of the \(centre\) point and the circumference. True, ‘*centre on*' does not bear logical analysis, but it is the better idiom, and is therefore to be preferred by careful writers." G.H. Vallins, *Better English* 79–80 \(4th ed. 1957\).

 - **1965:** "The verb *center* means to be collected or gathered to a point. Therefore, one may use *center on, center in,* or *center at,* but should not use *center around*. If one has a particular fancy for the word *around*, he should precede it with *revolve, rotate, cluster,* or some similar word." Theodore M. Bernstein, *The Careful Writer* 98 \(1965\).

 - **1966:** "Quasi-metaphorical language derived from Euclidean geometry ought not to flout Euclidean sense, yet the geometrically senseless expression *center around* \(or *about*\) is more frequently met than the tolerable images *center in, on,* or *at*." Wilson Follett, *MAU* at 98 \(1966\).

 - **1974:** "*Center around, Center about*. Common expressions, but objected to by many as illogical." Glenn Leggett, C. David Mead & William Charvat, *Prentice-Hall Handbook for Writers* 368 \(6th ed. 1974\).

 - **1980:** "It is physically impossible to *center around*. Make it *center on*." John B. Bremner, *Words on Words* 93 \(1980\).

 - **2002:** "*Center* indicates a point, and a point cannot encircle anything. Make it *center on* or *revolve around*." Bill Bryson, *Bryson's Dictionary of Troublesome Words* 36 \(2002\).

 - **2004:** "Things have their *centre* at a particular point and not *around* it; therefore Oedipus's tragedy should *centre on* his unfortunate relationship with his parents." James Cochrane, *Between You and I: A Little Book of Bad English* 24 \(2004\).

What about the other side of the argument? There are few apologists. One is Merriam-Webster: "*Center around* is a standard idiom, as commentators increasingly concede, even when they prefer another combination. You can use it freely, if you want to." *Merriam-Webster's Concise Dictionary of English Usage* 164 \(2002\). No evidence is cited there for the increasing concessions.

So overwhelming are the criticisms that many more than those just quoted could be plentifully supplied. Hence it is a misstatement to say, as one did in 1980, that "the opinions are evenly divided." Roy H. Copperud, *American Usage and Style: The Consensus* 67 \(1980\). The main "defenders" are the Merriam-Webster and Random House dictionaries—and, by inference based on silence, the Oxford dictionaries.

If we add into the analysis the empirical fact that *centering on* is much more common in print than *centering around* \(doubtless because of all the stylebook recommendations\!\), it's hard to justify a conclusion that *centering around* is unobjectionable. In editorial practice, it has long been objected to. But is the gap closing in modern usage? No. Since 1960, *centering around* has been in retreat in comparison with *centering on*.

The recommendation here is to understand that although *centering around* may one day be standard, that day hasn't arrived. If you're an editor, you're justified in wanting to change it to *centering on*. But to call it an outright error is to exaggerate. It is common in everyday speech but not yet unimpeachable in what purports to be polished writing. The criticisms haven't worn off.
