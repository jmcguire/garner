# complimentary

complimentary; **complementary**. This pair of adjectives is even more susceptible to confusion than the corresponding nouns. When a mistake occurs, *complementary* \(= \[1\] serving to complete; or \[2\] fitting together well\) usually displaces *complimentary* \(= \[1\] expressing admiration; or \[2\] given for free\)—e.g.:

 - "\[William M.\] Finkelstein knows his parents can handle it—and then some. ‘If they catch wind of somebody saying some less than *complementary* \[read *complimentary*\] remarks about the show, they hunt them down,' he said, laughing." Deborah Seibel, "Creator Draws on His Past to Conduct ‘Civil Wars,' " *Chicago Trib.*, 15 Mar. 1992, TV Week §, at 3.

 - "Casino host Sam Boyd, son of Boyd Gaming Chairman Bill Boyd, keeps the players comfortable with *complementary* \[read *complimentary*\] rooms and other amenities as the game stretches from hours into days." Jeff Simpson, "Big Games Bring In Big Money, Big Names," *Las Vegas Rev.-J.*, 13 Apr. 2003, at E1.

 - "I'm sending them my best wishes, along with some *complementary* \[read *complimentary*\] copies of a lovely travel magazine." Betsy Bitner, "Living Life of Luxury with Half a Tank of Gas," *Times Union* \(Albany\), 16 May 2021, at D2.

The set phrase for the sign-off in a letter is *complimentary close*.

## Language-Change Index

*complementary* misused for *complimentary*: Stage 1
Current ratio in print \(*complimentary close* vs. *\*complementary close*\): 42:1
