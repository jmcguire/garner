# carol, vb

carol, vb., meaning "to sing joyfully, and esp. to sing traditional Christmas songs," makes *caroled* and *caroling* in AmE, *carolled* and *carolling* in BrE. The AmE spellings did not become established until the early 1930s. The *OED* notes that "the derivative forms in -*ed*, -*ing*, -*er* are now most commonly spelt (in England) with *ll* (*carolled*, etc.) though for no good reason." That statement was true of *caroller* in BrE only for the period from about 1820 to 1890 and from 1915 to 1935; otherwise, the two spellings (*caroler* and *caroller*) competed closely in BrE. Today, *caroler* is predominant in AmE, *caroller* (no surprise) in BrE. But one erudite British correspondent assures me: "We don't really have *carolers* except when we watch schmaltzy American afternoon films. We say *carol-singers*."

 - Current ratio in print (*caroler* vs. *caroller* in World Englishes): 2.2:1
