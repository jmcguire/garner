# cornucopia

cornucopia (= a horn-shaped container filled with fruit and flowers, intended to represent a time of bounty and prosperity) has always formed the plural *cornucopias* in English. An antiquated Latinate form of the singular, *cornucopiae* (the *-ae* ending marks not the plural but the genitive—the "of" in "horn of plenty") recurs as a minor variant, taking the English plural *cornucopiaes*. Predictably, some writers have mistaken the *-ae* as a plural ending—e.g.: "One never really needs an excuse to visit the British Museum. Britain's cultural *cornucopiae* \[read *cornucopias*\] are legion, fads in them come and go, but the BM stands alone, exotica's most familiar seat—venue for foreign deputation or school trip, ambling wayfarer or lovers' tiff, native of Bloomsbury and the world." "Float of Wonder," *Times* (London), 17 Dec. 2001, at 15.

The Latin plural is *cornua copiae*.
