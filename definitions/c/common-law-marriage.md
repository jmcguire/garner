# common-law marriage

common-law marriage —a term dating from the 1600s but not in common use till the late 1800s—has one meaning in AmE and another in BrE. In American law, the phrase generally denotes an agreement to be married, followed by cohabitation and a public recognition of the marriage. Common-law marriages are valid in seven states, including California and Texas. Others have abolished the institution, as New York did in 1932.

In the states where this type of marriage is allowed, the term is increasingly being displaced by the term *informal marriage*.

In England, *common-law marriage* now often refers to a marriage celebrated according to a common-law form in a place where the local forms of marriage cannot be used \(e.g., a desert island\) or are morally unacceptable to the parties \(e.g., because of religious differences\), or where no cleric is available. Additionally—and more commonly in BrE—the phrase refers to an illicit union of some duration. As Sir Robert Megarry wrote, with a Johnsonian flair, "The so-called *common-law marriage*, little known in England save as a polite verbal cloak for fornication or adultery of the less ephemeral type, has a respectable ancestry in America." *A Second Miscellany-at-Law* 210 \(1973\).
