# compare with

compare with; **compare to**. Most traditionally, to *compare* something *with* something else is to place them side by side, noting differences and similarities <let us compare his goals with his actual accomplishments>. *To compare* something *to* something else is to emphasize similarities or likenesses <he compared her eyes to limpid pools>. From the 1600s to 1980, *compare with* was the more usual phrasing; since the late 20th century, *compare to* has become somewhat more common.

The phrase *compare and contrast* is an entirely forgivable English teacher's tautology. True, in comparing two things \(one thing *with* another\) one notes both similarities and differences, but students are likely to need the reminder that they must contrast the two things they're comparing.
