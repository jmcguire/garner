# crematorium

crematorium; **crematory**, n. Dating in English from about 1880, *crematorium* denotes a place or building where remains of the dead are incinerated. For a long time it was less common in AmE than the variant noun *crematory* (still listed as the main term in many dictionaries), but *crematorium* became predominant by the 1940s. *Crematorium* was always the most common form in BrE. The terms are distinct from *cinerarium*, which denotes a place for keeping the ashes of the dead.

Some use *crematory* to denote the furnace instead of the place or building that houses it, but the more usual (and unambiguous) phrasing is *crematory furnace* for such a need. That phrase also shows that *crematory* today is typically an adjective, not a noun.

The predominant plural of *crematorium* has always been *crematoria* by a fairly wide margin. But when, in April 2021, makeshift crematoria were established in India to deal with overwhelming Covid-19 mortalities, most news organizations called them *crematoriums*. My Twitter poll of 30 April 2021 resulted in 54% of the votes in favor of the plural *crematoriums*.
