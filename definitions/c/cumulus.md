# cumulus

cumulus. Pl. *cumuli*. \(See plurals \(b\).) Although the corresponding adjective is *cumulous*—and although the noun *cumulus* can stand on its own—the standard phrasing is *cumulus cloud*. E.g.:

• "The type of clouds that cross Wisconsin changes seasonally, and you find more *cumulus clouds* this time of the year." John Malan, "Today Is Likely to Be More Typical of a Day in June," *Milwaukee J. Sentinel*, 5 June 1996, Sports §, at 8.

• "Above, a ceiling fan turns lazily, barely disturbing the *cumulus clouds* of smoke." Linda Federman, "Music for Folks at an Irish Pub," *Wash. Times*, 14 Dec. 1996, at B1.

The phrase *cumulous clouds*, though not strictly wrong, is certainly unusual—e.g.: "South of town, puffy white *cumulous* \[read, preferably, *cumulus*\] clouds began to explode upwards." Rob Schneider, "Some Students Do All Their Studies in the Classroom," *Indianapolis Star*, 16 June 1996, at A1.

Current ratio in print \(*cumulus clouds* vs. *cumulous clouds*\): 11:1
