# *cum* 

*cum*  \(/kәm/ or /koom/\) is a Latin preposition meaning "with." It appears in various latinisms, such as *cum grano salis* \(= with a grain of salt\), *cum laude* \(= with praise\), and *cum notis variorum* \(= with notes of various people\). In English, it bears that same sense, or sometimes "along with being; and." It's usually hyphenated before and after—e.g.:

 - "Such changes have done nothing to tame the fundamental irreverence, particularly among the young, for the high Confucian-*cum*-communist culture of the mainland." Andrew Higgins, "Shuffling the Cards of Identity," *Guardian*, 28 June 1996, at 14.

 - "Or splurge and join the Explorers Club for a conference-*cum*-cruise on the last weekend of May." J.A. Lobbia, "Summer in the City," *Village Voice*, 3 June 1997, at 2.

 - "They designed and built an art gallery-*cum*-house." "War Photographer and Fearless Picture Editor of the Observer" \(obit.\), *Irish Times*, 31 July 2021, at 14.

But some writers mistakenly use *come* this way, as Fritz Spiegl notes: "A film critic in the *Sunday Times* recently described the character of Thomas Jefferson in a new film as ‘a sort of founding father *come* speak-your-weight machine.' " Spiegl, "Usage and Abusage," *Daily Telegraph*, 8 July 1995, at 32.

Unrelated to the Latin word is the pornographer's use of *cum* either as a noun \(meaning "semen"\) or as a verb \(meaning "to experience an orgasm"\). This is simply a respelling of *come*, which has borne sexual meanings from the beginning of the 1600s. This new use of the word, which became exceedingly common in the 1980s and 1990s and has remained so, has probably forever tinged the word *cum* with sexual associations.
