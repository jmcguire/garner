# come down the pike

come down the pike \(= to appear on the scene\), originally an AmE colloquial phrase dating from about 1900, uses the word *pike* in the sense of a road or tollway. The noun *pike* is often used in phrases such as *the best ever to come down the pike*, with some such superlative at the outset.

Once in use, *come down the pike* caught on quickly. Soon, however, some people misunderstood what they heard and, through word-swapping, created the malapropism *come down the pipe*—as if the metaphor had to do with plumbing instead of travel. E.g.:

 - "You're the luckiest guy who ever came down the *pipe* \[read *pike*\]." Louis Tracy, *The Black* *Cat* 298 \(1925\).

 - "The Soviet Union has created . . . some of the biggest appetites that ever came down the *pipe* \[read *pike*\]." *Hard Lessons: The Mine Mill Union in Canadian Labour Movement* 156 \(Dieter K. Buse et al. eds., 1995\) \(quoting or misquoting Ray Stevenson\).

 - "Jim Travers was one of the very best Canadian journalists ever to come down the *pipe* \[read *pike*\]." Dan Turner, "Anonymity Breeds Ugliness Online," *Ottawa Citizen*, 11 Mar. 2011, at A13.

Confusing matters still further is that we have the vaguely similar phrase *in the pipeline* \(= \[1\] in development or production; or \[2\] nearing completion and readiness\).

Although most instances of the phrase *come down the pipe* are indeed malapropisms, some of them are correct literal uses <if the waste will not come down the pipe on its own, plumbing tools may become necessary>. The ratio-in-print figures below must be seen with that caveat.

Current ratio in print \(*to come down the pike* vs. *to come down the pipe*\): 11:1
