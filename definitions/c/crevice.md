# crevice

crevice; **crevasse**. Etymologically identical, these words have a divide between them. *Crevice* (= a narrow crack or break, as in the ground, a sidewalk, or a wall), dating in English from the 1300s, was borrowed from the OF *crevace*. The word had taken on the anglicized pronunciation /**krev**-is/ even in those early days. The fissure it denotes is a small one—e.g.: "They are curious animals, nosing into cracks and *crevices* with their pointy snouts." Nancy J. Smeltzer, "Hedgehogs Curling Up in More Homes," *Columbus Dispatch*, 15 Dec. 1994, at C1.

*Crevasse* /kri-**vas**/ (= a large split or rupture, as in a levee, glacier, or embankment), dating in English from the early 1800s, was reimported from Modern French. First, Louisianians used the term for breaches in levees of the Mississippi River. Shortly after, Alpine climbers in Switzerland used the term for chasms in glaciers. In both uses, the word denotes a much greater split than *crevice* does. E.g.: "The 48-year-old climber would rappel down the deepest *crevasse*, haul himself up the deepest slope and slide into a sleeping bag to warm a victim of hypothermia." Mike Tharp, "Traveler's Death Still a Mystery," *Rocky Mountain News* (Denver), 5 Dec. 1994, at A26.

In sum, if *crevice* can spell small trouble, *crevasse* can spell big trouble.
