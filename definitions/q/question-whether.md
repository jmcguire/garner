# question whether

question whether; **question of whether**; **\*question as to whether**. The first has a long literary lineage: it properly uses in apposition to the noun *question* the noun clause beginning with *whether* <we should address the question whether you'll attend>. Some writers use *of* between *question* and *whether* in a way almost analogous to *the city of Boston* or *the name of Reynolds*. This minor prolixity—using *question of whether* instead of *question whether*—may strike traditionalists as less than refined. But it has become exceedingly common: it was equal in frequency to the tighter phrasing in books published in 2008, and by 2019 it had overtaken the phrasing without *of.* The phrasing *question of whether* shows no signs of retreating. Although it has achieved Stage 5 on the Language-Change Index, many editors will continue to prefer *question whether*. See **whether \(b\), \(c\).**

But \**question as to whether* raises a different question. Yes, it's ungainly, but it also features illogic, as Fowler pointed out in 1926: you're trying to say what the question *is*, not what it's *about*. \(*FMEU1* at 479.\) The prepositional *as to* traditionally introduces what something concerns, not its very essence. See **as to \(a\)**.

*question of whether* for *question whether:* Stage 5
