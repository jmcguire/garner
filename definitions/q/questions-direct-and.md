# Questions, Direct and Indirect 

Questions, Direct and Indirect . A. The Difference in Punctuating. A direct question—one posed explicitly—ends with a question mark. E.g.:

• "How can demand be controlled and stimulated?" Helen E. Haines, *Living with Books* 39 \(2d ed. 1950\).

• "There are \[other cultures that\] seem to have largely succeeded in taming or repressing \[envy\]. What causes such differences? Is it perhaps the varying frequency of certain types of personality and character?" Helmut Schoeck, *Envy: A Theory of Social Behavior* 10 \(1970\).

• "Does he or she have pockets deep enough to finance future rounds?" Roberta Reynes, "Venturing Out for Rapid Growth," *Nation's Bus.*, 1 Nov. 1997, at 54.

But an indirect question—one posed at a distance from the actual asking—ends with a period. E.g.:

• "In Clarksdale, Miss., a motel desk clerk diplomatically inquired what we were doing in that city, having noted we were from Florida." Red Marston, "Every Place Has a Story to Tell," *St. Petersburg Times* \(Fla.\), 25 Nov. 1997, at G16.

• "I wondered what he was thinking." Dori Butler & Sheila Kitzinger, "They Saw It All," *Mothering*, 22 Dec. 1997, at 58.

• "The minister was asked whether needing a Covid pass was a ‘real possibility over the coming days.' " Ruth Mosalski, "Welsh Government's Plan to Stop Soaring Coronavirus Rates," *Daily Post* \(North Wales\), 25 Oct. 2021, at 2.

Writers sometimes err by putting a question mark after an indirect question, especially one beginning with *I wonder*—e.g.:

• "Come to think of it, I wonder if the weapon in question is one he confiscated from the OU football dorm?" Jim Donaldson, "A Tale of a Gunslinger and a Smoking Gun," *Providence J.-Bull*., 6 Aug. 1997, at D1. \[Replace question mark with full stop.\]

• "I wonder whether the NAACP would have considered it proper if the Ku Klux Klan had similarly paid off the plaintiffs in Brown v. Board of Education?" "Lost Opportunity," *Fresno Bee*, 27 Nov. 1997, at B14. \[Replace question mark with full stop.\]

• "Chuggs was asked whether he thought Rachel would pick Brad?" "Love Island: Canny Lad Brad McClelland Survives Eviction Drama," *Northumberland Gaz.* \(U.K.\), 5 July 2021, Human Interest §. \[Replace question mark with full stop.\]

In those examples, as noted, the question mark should be a period. This phenomenon can occur in the most surprising places—e.g.: "We may wonder what \(if anything\) should replace the sentence as a basis for dividing a spoken discourse into maximal grammatically analysable \(parsable\) units?" Douglas Biber et al., *Longman Grammar of Spoken and Written English* 1039 \(1999\). \[Replace question mark with full stop.\]

**B. The Syntactic Difference.** For direct questions, there must be an auxiliary verb \(the first auxiliary if there is one, and otherwise *do*\) before the subject in the main clause <may I leave now?> <does the statute apply?> <will she want to go?>. But once the question is made indirect, so that the question appears in a subordinate clause, the inversion does not take place <people are asking whether they can leave now> <the courts have not decided whether the statute applies> <the question is whether she wants to go>.

For many decades now in nonstandard speech—and to some extent in writing—people have been stating indirect questions with the inverted style of direct questions. They'll say *People are asking can they leave now* or *The courts have not decided does the statute apply*. This syntactic habit might be regarded at best as a casualism. In writing, as in recorded speech, these statements might be rendered *People are asking, "Can they leave now?"* and *The courts have not decided, "Does the statute apply?"* In speech, however, the pause indicated by the comma is likely to be absent.

Consider two examples. First: " ‘You have *to ask what would we* have to give up.' " Alan Tovey, "West on Red Alert as China Steals March on Hypersonics," *Sunday Telegraph*, 24 Oct. 2021, at 8 \(quoting Justin Bronk, fellow at a military think tank\). Conventionally, one would expect there to see *You have to ask what we would have to give up.* Or perhaps *You have to ask, "What would we have to give up?"* Second: "\[It\] begs the *question of how do you* actually play while in the grip of a hallucinogenic trip?" Neil MacCormick, "Mescaline, Meditation, Miles Davis and Me," *Daily Telegraph*, 20 Oct. 2021, at 25. One would expect there to see *It begs the question how you actually play while in the grip of a hallucinogenic trip.* Or perhaps *It begs the question, "How do you actually play while in the grip of a hallucinogenic trip?"* On other issues in this last example, see both **beg the question** & **question whether**.

The mixing of direct and indirect questions raises questions not only about the syntax of standard english but also about standard punctuation. Are we to use a question mark or a period? In this nonstandard construction—stating an indirect question with the inverted syntax of a direct question—seemingly most writers include the question mark. But there's really no uniformity. E.g.:

• "Many are *asking can* \[read *asking whether*\] Black-and-Brown-owned banks compete in a digital economy?" Stacy M. Brown, "Can Black-Owned Banks Compete?," *Wash. Informer*, 10 June 2021, at 14. \[Replace the question mark with a period.\]

• "Mr. Biden was *asked what would be the ‘fair share'* \[read *asked what the ‘fair share' would be*\] of the tax burden that the rich and/or corporations should pay." Jeff Murdock, "Joe Biden Said He Doesn't Think Corporate Tax Rate Increase Will Be in Spending Bill," *Wash. Times*, 22 Oct. 2021, Politics §. \(On the *and/or* in that example, see **and/or**.\)

• "Parsons was later *asked how would the GPA* \[read *asked how the GPA would*\] deal with this." Seán Moran, "Promise to Reform Flawed Option B," *Irish Times*, 25 Oct. 2021, at 7.

The trend toward this small syntactic anomaly may be fueled, in some constructions, by the sense that *whether* signals formality. That is, *asked whether she can* is more formal than *asked if she can*, which is more formal than *asked can she*. If the middle wording there is casual \(see **if \(a\)**), the last wording is at-the-beach casual. The recommendations here are to (1) observe the traditional syntactic rules for inverting only direct questions, not indirect ones; (2) in reporting the speech of someone who inverts an indirect question, set it off with a comma and quotation marks, as if quoting a direct question; and (3) avoid the problem of deciding whether a question mark or a period follows the hybrid form by not using it. But if you ignore no. 3, use a period. Full stop.
