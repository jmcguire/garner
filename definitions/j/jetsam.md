# jetsam

jetsam \(= a ship's cargo or equipment that, in time of distress, is tossed overboard and either sinks or washes ashore\), a corruption of *jettison* dating from the 1500s, has always been predominantly so spelled. \**Jetsom* is a little-used variant—e.g.: "A box contains the flotsam and *jetsom* \[read *jetsam*\] she collects to make impressions in the sand." Larry Maddry, "Artist Finds a Home for Her Creative Spirit via the Sand of Virginia," *Virginian-Pilot* \(Norfolk\), 25 Oct. 1995, at E1. The word is pronounced /**jet**-sәm/.

It's almost impossible for anyone to mention *flotsam* without coupling it with the word *jetsam*. \(See irreversible binomials.\) *Flotsam* is literally cargo that floats on the water's surface after a shipwreck or, by extension, unimportant material.

Current ratio in print \(*jetsam* vs. \**jetsom*\): 227:1
