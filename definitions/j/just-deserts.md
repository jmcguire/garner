# just deserts

just deserts \(= the treatment one truly deserves\)—the second word being pronounced /di-**zәrts**/—is often misapprehended because of its pronunciation. *Desert* /di-**zәrt**/ here has meant "one's due reward, whether good or evil," since the 1300s. But because the homophone *dessert* is so much better known, and perhaps because people often think of *dessert* as a reward at the end of a meal, the correct spelling *just deserts* is often misrendered *\*just desserts*. Sometimes, of course, that's a playful pun, as in the biography of Martha Stewart titled *Just Desserts* \(2003\). But often it's sloppiness or ignorance—e.g.:

 - "Chortle all you want about that awful Linda Tripp getting her *just desserts* \[read *just deserts*\]." Rod Dreher, "Leave This Whistle-Blower Alone," *N.Y. Post*, 16 Dec. 1999, at 18.

 - "It was only a matter of time before the Calgary Hitmen would enjoy their *just desserts* \[read *just deserts*\]." Rita Mingo, "Keeping Faith Pays Off for Hitmen Club," *Calgary Herald*, 19 Dec. 2009, at E5.

 - "Seeing such a popular team member finally get her *just desserts* \[read *just deserts*\] has had a galvanising effect." Alex Spink, "A Kip of Tea Pays Off for Hero Holly," *Daily Mirror*, 6 Aug. 2021, at 51.

The U.S. Supreme Court has an unblemished record: in 21 uses of *just deserts* since 1946, not once has it misspelled the second word.

The opposite error—misusing *deserts* for *desserts*—is so rare as to suggest a typographical error. E.g.: "Fragrant apple butter spice case, like many *deserts* \[read *desserts*\], has extra appeal when served still warm from the oven." Carol Kress, "Roseburg Then and Now," in *Classroom 15* 60 \(Peter Laufer ed., 2021\).
