# jealousy

jealousy; **envy**. Careful users of the language tend to distinguish between these terms. *Jealousy* typically involves emotional rivalry, usually \(though not exclusively\) regarding the sexual instinct. It is especially apt when denoting the fear of losing an existing relationship to a challenger, real or perceived. *Envy*, by contrast, denotes a usually resentful contemplation of a more fortunate person; it involves the desire to have something that someone else has. In its more benign manifestations, *envy* can denote admiration—as someone good-naturedly declares, "I envy you your \[books, cars, house, etc.\]." While *jealousy* denotes an attitude about what you yourself possess, *envy* denotes an attitude about what someone else possesses.

Admittedly, many people apply *jealous* and *jealousy* to situations in which *envious* and *envy* might fit better. These uses might appropriately be considered casualisms as opposed to errors. They're well enough established that we have phrases such as *professional jealousy* \(where *professional envy* isn't quite a plausible choice\)—e.g.: "Much of this criticism was fuelled by the *professional jealousy* of less talented colleagues who resented Grant's success." Neil McKay, "Reluctant Soldier, Failure in Civilian Life, General Who Led Union to Victory," *Belfast News Letter*, 11 Apr. 2022, at 10. In that sentence, *envy* might replace *professional jealousy*, though perhaps with a loss of force.
