# grind \> ground \> ground

grind \> ground \> ground. So inflected. But the erroneous form *grinded* is gaining ground—e.g.:

 - "The ancient constitutional process of impeachment *grinded* \[read *ground*\] into operation again yesterday for the first time in 24 years." David M. Shribman, "A Titanic Jousting Act," *Pitt. Post-Gaz.*, 20 Nov. 1998, at A17.

 - "The Giants' return units have all but *grinded* \[read *ground*\] to a halt." Neil Best, "Giants vs. Broncos," *Newsday* (N.Y.), 13 Dec. 1998, at C12.

 - "Masahiro Tanaka (9–6), coming off a complete-game victory last Saturday in Toronto, *grinded* \[read *ground*\] through six innings." Erik Boland, "Yanks Go Down Swinging to Tribe," *Newsday* (N.Y.), 22 Aug. 2015, at A34.

See irregular verbs.

In one context, however, *grinded* is perfectly legitimate (though lamentable, in the eyes of high-school chaperons everywhere): a style of dancing known as *grinding*, involving a female's pressing her buttocks against her partner's groin (through clothing), or sometimes a direct rubbing of groins or of her breasts, often with vigorous perfrication. (Call it part of the coarsening of our culture.) Someone who has engaged in such dancing would say that he or she *grinded*, not *ground*. *O tempora*\! *O mores*\!
