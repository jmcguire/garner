# government

government.

**A. Pronunciation.** *Government* is preferably pronounced with the /n/ sound distinctly in the second syllable: /**gәv**-әrn-mәnt/. According to the pronunciation expert Charles Harrington Elster, it is "one of a number of words that careful speakers are particularly diligent about pronouncing precisely. . . . How you say it is an indication of whether you are an average educated speaker or a cultivated one." *BBBM* at 229. Robert W. Burchfield, the *OED* chief editor and BBC consultant, said that *government* belongs "to a small group of words that \[give\] a maximum of offence to listeners if pronounced in a garbled manner, with the first *n* silent." *FMEU3* at 339.

**B. And *administration*; *regime*; *dictatorship*; *junta*.** Have you ever noticed that one's characterization of a particular group in power often involves editorializing? We refer to the *administration* of a particular American president <the Eisenhower administration> <the Clinton administration>. We tend to refer to the *government* of a British prime minister <the Churchill government> <the Blair government>. Although it's not impossible to say *Eisenhower government*, that phrasing is exceedingly rare compared to *Eisenhower administration*. With other major powers outside the English-speaking world, the references are variable <the Xi Jinping administration *or* government *or* regime>.

There's a tendency to use *regime* for ruling powers that are not well regarded, especially authoritarian ones <the Nazi regime> <the communist regime> <the Soviet regime>. Some writers disapprove of using *regime* with the name of a particular rule, as in *the Castro regime in Cuba*. Why *Castro regime* but *Obama administration*? The answer must lie in value judgments. Castro sympathizers might well write *Castro administration* but *Obama regime*. In the 1980s, the Voice of America style guide took the position that it's incorrect to use *regime* as a pejorative suggesting illegitimacy or the illegal assumption of power.

The *AP Stylebook* has long taken the position that it's incorrect to write *Assad regime* or *North Korean regime* because the word *regime* should denote a political system, not a particular government. "The word *regime*," the book says, "should be used only in general terms." Under this guidance, it's proper to say *democratic regime* or *totalitarian regime*, but not *Gaddafi regime* or *Khomeini regime*. But this prescription has never reflected actual usage.

The phrase *regime change*, dating from the 1920s, rather clearly uses the term (attributively) in reference to a particular foreign government that is viewed as antagonistic or inclined to state terrorism. The *OED* defines *regime change* as the removal of a hostile government, especially by military force. Hence it would be quite wrong, despite the violent upheaval involved, to refer to the transition from the Trump administration to the Biden administration as a *regime change*.

From a reporting point of view, the Voice of America and AP style guidance does make some sense: the choice of term betrays an editorial slant that may be viewed as tendentious. The Voice of America style manual also noted, rightly, that neither *government* nor *parliament* necessarily carries with it the idea that those in power have been democratically elected.

If it's a single authoritarian ruler, the word is often *dictatorship* <the Franco dictatorship> <the Somoza dictatorship>. If it's an authoritarian ruling committee or council, the word is typically *junta* <the Myanmar junta> <the Argentine junta>. You might argue that these terms, too, involve value judgments. Yet the counterargument that they denote objectively observable phenomena is a compelling one.

As for the choice between *regime* and *regimen*, see **regime**.
