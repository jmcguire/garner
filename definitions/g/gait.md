# gait

gait (= a manner of walking, trotting, etc.) is sometimes confused with its homophone *gate*—e.g.:

 - "Eddie squinted at the ball through thick black-framed glasses, his spindly legs and stooped shoulders giving him an awkward *gate* \[read *gait*\]." Hilary Waldman, "They Were Family," *Hartford Courant*, 24 Nov. 1991, at 14.

 - "He was moving with the feeble *gate* \[read *gait*\] of an old man." Greg Logan, "Hampton Jitney Running on Empty," *Newsday* (N.Y.), 9 Dec. 1994, Sports §, at A118.

 - "American Creams, which were not recognized as a breed until 1950, are medium-size draft horses, weighing about 1,600 to 1,800 pounds. . . . Their *gate* \[read *gait*\] is smooth and easy, and their temperament is amiable and trustworthy." Suzanne Hively, "American Cream Is Featured Draft at Lake Farmpark Horse Show," *Plain Dealer* (Cleveland), 16 May 2002, at E3.

See word-swapping.
