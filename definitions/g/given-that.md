# given \(that\)

given \(that\). At the head of a statement \(most commonly\), *given* is an age-old shorthand way of saying "assuming as a fact" or "with \[the thing stated\] supplied as a basis for reasoning." If what follows *given* is a mere phrase \(without a verb\), *given* appears alone—e.g.: "*Given* her high position, she does have opposition." Larry Whitham, "Episcopalians Brace for Divisive Sessions," *Wash. Times*, 13 July 1997, at A4. But if a clause follows \(with a verb\), a *that* should accompany *given*—e.g.: "*Given that* he was a medical student at Brown before turning to professional hockey, Bill McKay might be expected to be a quick study." Peter Barrouquere, "McKay Learns How to Wing It," *Times-Picayune* \(New Orleans\), 26 Nov. 1997, at C3. \(Cf. **provided that**.\) Typically, though, *because* or *since* might replace *given that* with a gain in conciseness and power.

For more on wrongly omitted *that*s, see **that \(d\)**.
