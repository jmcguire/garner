# glitter

glitter; **glister**; **glisten**. *Glitter*, dating from the 1300s, derives from the Middle English *gliteren*. It has four senses: \(1\) to sparkle; to reflect many small flashes of light <the sequins glittered>; \(2\) to flash positive emotions <when praised, the children glittered>; \(3\) to be spectacularly attractive <at the party, the couple glittered>; and \(4\) to be superficially attractive or exciting; to flash <those arguments may glitter, but there's little substance to them>.

*Glister* is related to *glitter*, having arisen from the same root, with the same meaning, and at the same time. It is now an archaic form of *glitter* \(in sense 1\), but it appears in the set phrase *all that glisters is not gold* \(from Shakespeare's *Merchant of Venice* 2.7.65 \[1600\]\).

*Glisten* is the oldest term; the OED cites its use in a poem written about 1000. To *glisten* is to "produce a lustrous shine or sparkle like light on water" <the wood's polish is so brilliant it glistens> <moonlight glistening on snow>. In modern usage, *glisten* most often occurs in allusions to moisture in some form <her eyes glistened with unshed tears>.
