# gladiolus

gladiolus —/gla-dee-**oh**-lәs/ or \(pretentiously\) /glә-**dī**-ә-lәs/—is the singular form. The plural form is predominantly *gladioli* /gla-dee-**oh**-lee/ or /-lī/. See plurals \(b\).

But because *gladiolus* is sometimes wrongly taken to be a plural, especially in speech, the mistaken back-formation *\*gladiola* has emerged. It is an arrant mistake that should be uprooted. E.g.:

 - "A *gladiola* \[read *gladiolus*\] among a patch of violets, her spirit shines all the more brightly against the backdrop of her restrictive society." Mary McCarty, "The Real Little Women," *Dayton Daily News*, 1 Jan. 1995, at C1.

 - "Minutes later they crowd into Celsa Garcia's house, delivering their figures of Joseph and Mary to a makeshift corner altar that Ms. Garcia, 65, has draped with garlands of *gladiolas* \[read *gladioli*\] and pine." Sam Dillon, "Tehuixtla Journal," *N.Y. Times*, 25 Dec. 1996, at A4.

 - "Anyone at the fair at 11 a.m. Thursday, Sept. 3, can join the new *gladiola* \[read *gladiolus*\] competition in the Horticulture Building. Fairgoers can try to make a *gladiola* \[read *gladiolus*\] arrangement in 10 minutes." Debra J. Groom, "State Fair Gears Up," *Advance-News* \(Ogdensburg, N.Y.\), 23 Aug. 2015, at 7.

You might be tempted to say that in a phrase such as \**bouquet of gladiolus*, the term *gladiolus* is being used less as a plural than as a collective or mass noun. But the argument doesn't go far. Nobody would refer to a \**bouquet of sunflower* or \**bouquet of tulip*—even though a *bouquet of lavender* is possible \(*lavender* not generally being thought of as each flower on its own stalk\). The phrase \**bouquet of gladiolus* is neither idiomatic nor logical.
