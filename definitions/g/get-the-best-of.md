# get the best of

get the best of; **get the better of**. Eric Partridge suggested a narrow distinction between these verb phrases: "Properly, the former applies to swindling and other trickery, whereas the latter has no offensive connotation and simply = to overcome, to defeat" \(*U&A* at 135\). Yet the *OED* records no such distinction, and today the phrases are used synonymously to mean "to gain the advantage over" or "to prevail against." The older phrase, *get the better of*, dates from the early 1600s and occurs with somewhat greater frequency in modern print sources. *Get the best of* emerged about two decades later and now appears most often in AmE.

 - Current ratio in print \(*get the better of* vs. *get the best of *\): 1.9:1
