# garden path

garden path; **primrose path**. The two phrases have similar but not quite the same meanings.

*Garden path*, the phrase that appears in print more often, is usually literal—e.g.: "Ipe is also available in interlocking tiles that require no nails or finish. Using a jigsaw to cut rounded edges or odd corners, they can be placed on the surfaces of decks, courtyards, balconies, patios, porches, hot tub and pool surrounds, *garden paths*, garages and saunas." Stacy Downs, "Brazilian Walnut Hardwood Prized for Its Good Looks," *Chicago Trib.*, 29 Sept. 2006, Your Place §, at 16. But it may also be used figuratively to denote an intentional misleading—e.g.: "Blair has been under pressure at home from critics who contend that he let U.S. President Bush take him down the *garden path* in Iraq." Editorial, "Horror in London," *Tulsa World*, 8 July 2005, at A14.

*Primose path*, on the other hand, is almost always allusive. It traditionally denotes a profligate or debauched lifestyle destined for ruin—e.g.: " ‘The man is said to be a well-to-do merchant of Memphis, but he followed the *primrose path* too far in Los Angeles,' the newspaper said." "Man on Bender Seeks to Go Straight," *L.A. Times*, 24 Sept. 2006, News §, at A3. The phrase was used twice by Shakespeare, in *Hamlet* \(1.3.45–51\) and *Macbeth* \(2.3.17–18\).

It would be helpful to distinguish the two phrases. But unfortunately, *primrose path* is now being used more and more often in the same way as *garden path*, to denote being misled, as here: "Regardless of form—oil, gas, coal or nuclear—the Green movement is blocking efforts to harness our accustomed energy sources while leading us down the *primrose path* \[read *garden path*\] of so-called ‘renewable energy.' " Steven Milloy, "Color Energy Woes Green," *Wash. Times*, 7 June 2005, at A16.
