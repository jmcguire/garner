# girlie

girlie; **girly**; **girlish**. First having appeared in the late 1800s, *girlie* (= \[1\] of or like a girl; or \[2\] featuring naked or scantily clad women) has traditionally been the predominant spelling in AmE and BrE alike. Since about 1990, however, *girly* surged in English-language print sources. Today it closely rivals *girlie*.

But that story is incomplete. In fact, what has happened is that *girly* has become strongly associated with the innocent sense 1 \<girly girl\> \<girly stuff\> \<girly things\>, while *girlie* has become associated with sex-related sense 2 \<girlie magazines\> \<girlie bars\> \<girlie show\>. Hence the two spellings are undergoing differentiation—a development to be encouraged.

*Girlish* (= of or like a girl; involving girlhood) is most often paired with the nouns *beauty*, *face*, *fancy*, *figure*, *heart*, *laughter*, and *voice*. It often carries positive connotations of innocence and artlessness \<girlish charm\>—though it can also tend in the other direction \<girlish emotions\>. In this way, the term doesn't quite mirror *boyish*, which is almost invariably positive \<boyish enthusiasm\> \<boyish grin\>. This fact doubtless tells us something about cultural views; any discussion of it belongs in a book on sociology, not English usage.
