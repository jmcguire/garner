# gambade

gambade; **gambado**. These synonymous doublets have come into English through French \(*gambade*\) and Spanish \(*gambado*\). They denote \(1\) the spring of a horse, or \(2\) an antic-filled dance; a caper or gambol. *Gambade* /gam-**bayd**/ is slightly older in English and has always been slightly predominant in AmE and BrE alike. Although *gambado* /gam-**bay**-doh/ has shared the two senses listed above, it has undergone differentiation, so that in its primary sense today it denotes a horse rider's legging. The standard plural is *gambadoes*, not *\*gambados*. See plurals \(d\).

 - Current ratio in print \(*gambadoes* vs. *\*gambados*\): 1.6:1
