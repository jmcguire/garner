# peruse

peruse \(= to read with great care\), long regarded as pompous and stilted in business correspondence, shouldn't be used merely as a fancy substitute for *read.* It is pronounced /pә-**rooz**/; the corresponding noun is *perusal* /pә-**rooz**-әl/.

Some writers misuse the word as if it meant "to read quickly" or "scan" \(in the colloquial sense of that word\)—e.g.:

 - "While both teams anxiously milled about in front of their dugouts and restless fans voiced their opinions, Thornton hurriedly *perused* \[read *read* or *scanned*\] the rule book." "One Giant Step for Merchants," *Lewiston Morning Trib.* \(Idaho\), 5 Aug. 1997, at B1.

 - "It is now a bit awkward so I do as I'm told and very briefly *peruse* \[read *look over*\] the authors, seeing Dickens, Kipling, Steinbeck, Günter Grass, Don DeLillo, Virginia Woolf." Helena de Bertondano, "I Hear Obama Did Read the Book," *Sunday Telegraph*, 26 Sept. 2010, Features §.

This slipshod extension has become common enough to be listed in some dictionaries. But since it's the opposite of the word's traditional meaning, that usage is best shunned.

Even worse is the "scan" sense that has nothing to do with reading—e.g.:

 - "After quickly *perusing* \[read *examining*\] the stacks of tile, I identified one that looked fine." Kate Snyder, "Just Choose H," *Advocate-Messenger* \(Ky.\), 15 Mar. 2019, Features §.

 - "She quickly *perused* \[read *scanned*\] the girl's body for other injuries." Ed Mullins, "Let's Be Real the City Is in Freefall," *Daily Mail* \(U.K.\), 14 May 2021.
