# protuberance

protuberance \(= something that sticks out; a knob or bump\), dating in English from the early 1600s, has the corresponding adjective *protuberant* and verb *protuberate*. The root is the Latin *tuber* \(meaning "to swell"\). The better-known verb *protrude* \(= to stick out from something\), dating from the 1500s, derives from a different Latin root \(meaning "to thrust or push forward"\). Its less-well-known cognates are *protrusive* \(adj.\) and *protrusion* \(n.\). Since the 1700s, writers and speakers of English have been confused by the fact that a *protuberance* is something that *protrudes*. So they've often written the mistaken form \**protruberance*, labeled nonstandard in the *OED*. The misspelling is so subtle that it doesn't exactly stick out—e.g.:

 - "Yayoi Kusama . . . became notorious in the 1960s for covering surfaces with phalluses. In the catalogue is a photograph of a room filled with furniture bristling with such *protruberances* \[read *protuberances*\], which, seemingly, are cast in plaster." Vivien Raynor, "An Exhibition Emphasizing the Feminist Role in Politics," *N.Y. Times*, 7 May 1995, § 13, at 24.

 - "One of the main characters has an enormous boil on his right buttock. The director was utterly obsessed with this *protruberance* \[read *protuberance*\], which—in the film's most noxious scene—is squeezed." Geoffrey MacNab, "Clouds over Cannes," *Independent*, 23 May 2008, at 10.

 - "It is fun explaining to other mystified people why Kim's enormous *protruberances* \[read *protuberances*\] have earned a vast global following even though she doesn't appear to have any . . . um, talent." Paul Lewis, "Faceless Blogs Another Hurdle for Jenner," *New Zealand Herald*, 7 June 2015, at A40.

 - "Geologically speaking, Sigiriya is what is known as a monadnock: a volcanic *protruberance* \[read *protuberance*\] harder than the surrounding rock that eroded over eons." Catherine Bush, "In the Heart of the Old Country," *Globe & Mail* \(Toronto\), 1 Apr. 2017, at T6.

As you might gather from those instances, *protuberance* is a formal word often used in polite or mock-polite discussions of awkward subjects.
