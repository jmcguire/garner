# plenitude

plenitude. So spelled. The word is derived from the Latin *plenus* "full"—the etymon also for *plenary*. Unfortunately, through confusion with the word *plenty*, the misspelling *\*plentitude* \(which first appeared in the 1700s\) has become common—e.g.:

• "In its scant 181 pages \(including a well-organized index\), the reader will find a *plentitude* \[read *plenitude*\] of good shopping theory." Georgia Brown, "Gonzo Shopper," *Wash. Times*, 2 Jan. 1997, at C11.

• "On one hand, the new image of feminine pleasure played into fantasies of sexual *plentitude* \[read *plenitude*\] that have long characterized pornography." Kristen Hatch, "The Sweeter the Kitten the Sharper the Claws," in *Bad: Infamy, Darkness, Evil, and Slime on Screen* 143, 150 \(Murray Pomerance ed., 2004\).

• "Tissot . . . interviewed rabbis and Bedouins in order to understand Christ in the *plentitude* \[read *plenitude*\] of his reality." James Gardner, "So What Was the Fuss All About?," *Wall Street J.*, 17 Dec. 2009, at D6.

Of course, the phrase *a plenitude of* can very often be shortened to a simple *much* or *many*.
