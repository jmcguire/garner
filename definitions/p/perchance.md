# perchance

perchance, now an archaism for *perhaps*, enjoyed its heyday in the 1600s. Today, it's mostly found in mockery or self-mockery—e.g.:

 - "Because his offensive skills run to zero, Dennis Rodman baits whomever he can into rutting with him, *perchance* to draw the technical \[foul\], *perchance* better to forward his image that night on SportsCenter." Thomas Stinson, "Murder and Mayhem? No, It's the NBA," *Atlanta J.-Const.*, 13 May 1995, at F7.

 - "The composition of the crowd began to change as exultant figures of the left and centre came to partake of the excellent champagne, and *perchance* to gloat." Rian Malan, "Election '97: One Last Spin Precedes a New Messiah," *Independent*, 4 May 1997, at 18.

 - "People didn't know those shops were there but peeked in the window and wandered in, *perchance* to buy." Charles Andrew, "Closure Doesn't Solve Problems," *Santa Monica Daily Press*, 11 Aug. 2021, at 6.
