# Phatic Language

Phatic Language is communication used for polite social interaction rather than to elicit or convey information. It's the smallest of small talk—often about the weather. The inventor of the term, Bronislaw Malinowski \(1884–1942\), explained that in its most primitive form, it overcomes taciturnity, which can be seen as hostile, and smooths the way into and out of interactions. It helps us, he wrote in 1923, "get over the strange and unpleasant tension which men feel when facing each other in silence." Malinowski, "The Problem of Meaning in Primitive Languages," Supp. I to C.K. Ogden & I.A. Richards, *The Meaning of Meaning* 314 \(1923; 8th ed. of 1946 reprinted in 1989\).

Phatic words and phrases are most often liminal: they begin and end communications, whether in speech or in writing. *Hello* and *goodbye* are archetypal conversational examples. In letters, it's *Dear* and *Yours sincerely*, or whatever other salutation and complimentary close you might use.

Some Elizabethan examples: *How fares your Grace? / How do you do, sir? / Why, how now, ho\! / How does thy Honor? / How fares my gracious Sir? / How now, Sirrah? / How do you, man?* Those greetings are all from Shakespeare. Only one is conceivable in today's speech: *How do you do, sir?* It's formal but still natural-sounding.

Most people understand that it's not literal: only the bore complains that people say *How are you?* without really wanting to hear the true answer. But some phrases can be either phatic or substantive, such as *Let's do lunch soon\!* Perhaps only a certain type of person uses that as phatic language—meant nonliterally and therefore insincerely. But then the interlocutor responds, *Yes, let's set a date\! Do you have your calendar handy?* And now they're planning—one more enthusiastically than the other.

Phatic language changes from one generation to the next. It can be almost as ephemeral as slang. Sometimes it gets transformed. *How do you do?* was changed to *Howdy\!*—a word now associated with the South. In fact, though, the phrase *Howdy, neighbor*, according to the *OED*, was recorded in Cornwall way back in 1600.

For those greeted with *How do you do?* the proper answer, according to Judith Martin \(aka Miss Manners\), is the same question in response. In her book *Miss Manners' Basic Training: The Right Thing to Say*, she calls it "the original answer-a-question-with-a-question."

It was long considered gauche to say, upon being introduced, *Glad to meet you\!* or *Nice meeting you\!* or even *Pleased to make your acquaintance\!* Martin says that it's "impossible to know in advance if there will be pleasure in the association."

Phatic language often proclaims social status and self-identification. \(See class distinctions.\) In Middle America in the 1970s, this type of exchange was typical among college-educated speakers:

"How's it going?"

"Great. How's it going with you?"

"Fine. Thank you."

Or:

"How are you?"

"Fine. Thank you. And you?"

"I'm doing well, thank you."

Things changed. By 2020, college-educated people were using utterances that those of the 1970s would find objectionable on grammatical grounds. People started using *good* in place of *well*—adverbially. Hence today this kind of exchange became common in some circles:

"How are you doing?"

"I'm doing good. And you?"

"I'm doing good, too. Thanks for asking."

It used to be that *doing good* in the world meant promoting the common good: voluntarism, public service, and philanthropy. Old-timers wouldn't *say* they were doing good \(an immodest claim, in their view\); they'd prefer to have it said about them. But they saw *good* in the phrase *do good* as a noun, certainly not as an adverb.

The other notable aspect of that exchange is *Thanks for asking*, usually uttered with great earnestness. It suggests that this isn't a phatic exchange at all but one meant to be taken literally. Your interlocutor is so kind to want to know how you're doing\!

Many other phatic exchanges are current today. More shocking in the old-timers' view would be this natter, commonplace by 2020:

"How are you doing?"

"I'm good. You?"

"I'm good."

Here, from a traditionalist's viewpoint, the speaker is claiming to be "good." Quite a claim. Good as in virtuous? Living the good life?

Isn't it funny, though, that traditionalists object to *I'm good* but consider *I'm fine* to be entirely appropriate—as well as *I'm doing fine*? That's just the way language works: it's a socially stipulated set of norms, and the social stipulations can vary from group to group.

Some people answer the question *How are you?* by responding *I'm well*. That leads to another set of problems. *Well* can be both the adverb corresponding to *good* \(a good golfer plays well\) and an adjective that is the antonym of *unwell*. To be well is to be healthy—not sick. So to the traditionalist, to say *I'm well* is to say you're not sick.

As for terminating an exchange, how did Shakespeare's characters say goodbye? Consider these sweet and not-so-sweet partings: *Madam and mistress, a thousand good morrows. / Farewell, good Pompey. / Adieu, good master. / Good day and happiness, dear Rosalind. / Good morrow, coz. / Fare you well, dear mistress. / A good digestion to you all. / Away, away\! / Goodnight. I'll be your fool no more.*

Shakespeare seems not to have used *Have a good day\!* In the 1980s, this phrase suddenly spread through the nation, often accompanied by a yellow smiley face. Edwin Newman, the author and broadcaster, satirized its ubiquity. \(See Newman, *I Must Say* 11–13 \(1988\).\) But today in America, it's a stock example of well-accepted phatic language.

What may surprise you is that *Have a good day\!* appeared often in Middle English \(1100–1499\)—without, of course, the smiley face. It's not so very different from variations used through the centuries, such as *Good day to you\!* How gratifying, in a way, to know that we continue a linguistic tradition dating back so many centuries. But change *good* to *nice* and suddenly you arrive at an Americanism traceable only to the 1950s: *Have a nice day\!* Americans do like the nondescript adjective *nice*. See **nice**.

By the way, I hope your day is going well.
