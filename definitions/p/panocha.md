# panocha

panocha /pa-**noh**-chә/ \(= \[1\] a coarse brown sugar orig. manufactured in Mexico; or \[2\] a fudge-like confection or pudding made with brown sugar, butter, milk or cream, and nuts\), a Spanish loanword dating in English from the mid-1800s, is recorded in the dictionaries under various spellings but has been predominantly so written since 1920. Among the variants are *penuche* /pә-**noo**-chee/, \**penuchi*, and \**penoche*.

In Latin-American Spanish, *panocha* has become a taboo word for the female pudendum—doubtless from the literal Spanish meaning of a type of bread with a slit for stuffing. Although most dictionaries don't warn of this, you'd be ill-advised to go into a store in the southwestern U.S. to ask for panocha.

Current ratio in print \(*panocha* vs. *penuche* vs. \**penoche* vs. \**penuchi*\): 75:51:1.3:1
