# purposive

purposive; **purposeful**. H.W. Fowler and the original (Victorian) *OED* editors objected to *purposive* as an ill-formed hybrid. Today, however, it is usefully distinguished in one sense from *purposeful* (= \[1\] having a purpose; or \[2\] full of determination). Although *W11* records under *purposive* the sense "serving or effecting a useful function though not as a result of planning or design," this sense is obscure and difficult to recognize. The primary sense is "of, relating to, or involving judicial interpretation that emphasizes not the wording of a legal instrument but instead its broad purposes." The corresponding noun is *purposivism*, which denotes the controversial doctrine that a judge-interpreter should derive meaning not from the words of the statute but from its social, economic, and political objectives.

In most other uses, *purposive* is a needless variant of *purposeful*—e.g.: "The raw materials for John Wayne the persona were Wayne's imposing physique and an economical, *purposive* \[read *purposeful*\] body language, which combined with the famous Wayne voice . . . to convey an air of inevitability and control." Michiko Kakutani, "The Making of a Myth Who Rode into the Sunset," *N.Y. Times*, 25 Feb. 1997, at C14.
