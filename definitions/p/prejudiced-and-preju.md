# Prejudiced and Prejudicial Terms

Prejudiced and Prejudicial Terms. Language both reflects and shapes perceptions. That makes it a natural battleground for those who seek to reform society in the name of social justice—or even just to encourage individual people to be more civilized. It's an ever-present struggle.

While linguistic traditionalists wish to preserve the language handed down to them, with perhaps incremental changes, linguistic progressives wish to eradicate built-in biases at once. The attendant issues transcend the obvious issues of racial and ethnic slurs \(see race-related terms), putdowns of gay and transgender people, and other patently discriminatory expressions. Metaphorical uses of language, in particular, can express invidious biases against people with disabilities <lame arguments> <blind followers> <deaf to reason>—and against all things black <a black mark on your record> <black sheep> <blackening your reputation>.

Even the word-stock, the lexis of English, can reflect and reinforce discrimination. Why should the language have dozens of words available to denote unpleasant \(or perhaps merely assertive\) women—even quasi-literary words such as *amazon*, *battle-axe*, *fishwife*, *harridan*, *hellcat*, *scold*, *she-devil*, *shrew*, *termagant*, *virago*, and *Xantippe*—but no comparable array for unpleasant men? Why should there be some 800 synonyms to denote female prostitutes—all of which are available for the boorish disparagement of women generally—but only a few comparable terms for men? You can doubtless think of possible answers, but none that might assuage those who, understandably, object both to the imbalance and more generally to the use of callous derision.

Is it possible to avoid offending everyone? No. \(See political correctness.) But it's surely desirable to avoid giving gratuitous offense that might be reasonably and predictably taken—offense that mars the message.

This is nothing new. In 1989, the University of Missouri-Columbia School of Journalism issued a pamphlet titled *Dictionary of Cautionary Words and Phrases*. True, the title itself was a misnomer, given that *cautionary* is essentially a synonym of *admonitory*. The words and phrases themselves weren't cautionary but instead merited the exercise of caution. So it should have been *A Cautionary Dictionary of \(Objectionable\) Words and Phrases*. If we examine the body of the text itself, and the words it cautions readers about, the admonitions seem largely reasonable in the third decade of the 21st century.

There were a dozen primary categories of objectionable terms, namely, those that:

• reinforce invidious stereotypes for women \(*airhead*, *ball and chain*, *bimbo*, *chick*, *dingbat*, *ditz*, *shrew*, *wench*\);

• comment on a woman's body \(*buxom*, *cheesecake*, *full-figured*, *statuesque*, *voluptuous*\);

• comment on a woman's sexuality \(*frigid*, *nympho*, *slut*, *whore*\);

• reinforce invidious stereotypes for minorities \(*bandido*, *boy* \[in reference to an adult\], *Chinaman*, *Chink*, *colored*, *coolie*, *coon*, *frito bandito*, *gook*, *greaser*, *Guido*, *Injun*, *Jap*, *Julio*, *Kike*, *raghead*, *Uncle Tom*\);

• associate foods with particular races \(*fried chicken*, *watermelon*\);

• denote racial mixtures \(*Amerasian*, *halfie*, *mulatto*, *octoroon*, *quadroon*\);

• denigrate a person's religious beliefs \(*Bible-thumper*, *Cafeteria Christian*, *Holy Roller*, *Hymie*, *Jesus freak*, *Yid*\);

• belittle senior citizens \(*blue-haired*, *codger*, *coot*, *geezer*, *senile*\);

• characterize people with disabilities \(*crip*, *crippled*, *dumb*, *pegleg*, *retarded*, *spastic*, *vegetable*\);

• perpetuate stereotypes of men as sexual conquerers \(*Don Juan*, *lady killer*, *Latin lover*, *stallion*, *stud*\);

• invoke nationality or race in insulting ways \(*Dutch treat*, *gyp*, *Indian giver*, *Jew down*\);

• insult LGBTQ people \(*dyke*, *faggot*, *fairy*, *fruit*, *queer*\); and

• denote gender gratuitously in a way traditionally regarded—by many—as demeaning \(*comedienne*, *executrix*, *poetess*, *prosecutrix*, *starlet*, *waitress*\).

Also on the list were various terms denoting racial minorities that are "X on the outside and white on the inside": *banana* or *Twinkie* \(Asian\), *coconut* \(brown\), and *Oreo* or *Hostess Cupcake* \(Black\).

The committee that assembled the list understood the sensational nature of committing such a list to paper: "The list is not intended to be a guide for the bigot but to alert journalists that certain words and phrases are simply unacceptable. The list is but a starting point. As social attitudes change and definitions are refined, the list must be updated." \(P. 1.\) Note that the admonitions were intended for *journalists*, not necessarily everybody else: journalists have traditionally been enjoined to aspire to a kind of omniscient objectivity. But there are lessons here for everyone.

Because fashions change, some of the 1989 cautions don't endure well. Writers were told not to use "Asian, Hispanic, black, or gay community." Why? *Community* "implies a monolithic culture in which people act, think, and vote in the same way." True enough. That's a danger. But various groups of people have specifically adopted the word *community*, perhaps for the political capital that such an implication may have. Writers were cautioned to write *Black residents in a northside neighborhood*, which certainly seems sharper. But *community* is hardly objectionable in the way that the other terms are.

The advice to avoid dysphemisms for people is simply common sense. Far from conveying some intended thought, they are certain to reflect poorly on the speaker or writer.

You can well imagine how someone harboring prejudices might use words with unfortunate sound associations—words and phrases with no etymological connection but, in a given context, convey a questionable intent. Among these are *blackguard*, *chicanery*, *chink in the armor*, and *niggardly*. Protest all you like that *niggardly* is a perfectly innocent adjective with no connection to the N-word. That may be true etymologically, but using it is asking for trouble. And your efforts can't possibly benefit you as a writer. It won't behoove you to administer a lesson in etymology after you've been misunderstood. See **niggardly**.

Is all this really a matter of English usage? Yes. Usage equates to custom—linguistic custom. You must be aware of linguistic taboos and near-taboos, whatever the language you're working in. They change over time. If the goal is credibility—not the much lower standard of intelligibility—then you must gauge the likely effect of the words you use.
