# piggyback

piggyback, adv. & n., a compound dating back to the 1500s, was *pickback*, *pick-pack*, *pickaback*, *pick-a-back*, or any of similar variants. By the late 1800s, *pickaback* had evolved, through folk etymology, to *piggyback*. \(See etymology \(d\).\) But *piggyback* wasn't the predominant form until the mid-1950s. It may well have been helped along by the 1934 Oscar-winning movie *It Happened One Night*, in which the stars, Claudette Colbert and Clark Gable, trade lines in one scene using the distinctly pronounced *piggyback* ten times. Today it vastly predominates over the earlier spellings.

Although the adverbial sense, usual today, is traditionally personal and physical \("on someone's shoulders or back"\) <carried his small daughter piggyback through the carnival>, it has extended senses. The word can refer to, for example, the carrying of aircraft <the space shuttle rode piggyback on a jumbo jet>. But since the 1950s, the term has been most commonly employed as a figurative verb meaning either "to capitalize on another's success" <computer games that piggyback on the popularity of cartoon characters> or "to use someone else's advantage for oneself" <piggybacking on a neighbor's internet connection> <an advocate who piggybacks onto another's argument>.

The pedantic objection that pigs don't willingly carry anything on their backs is the literalism of a tedious prig.
