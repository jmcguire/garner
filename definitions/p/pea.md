# pea

pea. This word has an interesting history. It evolved from *pease*, which in Old and Middle English served as both singular and plural to denote either the round green seed that is cooked and eaten as a vegetable or the plant on which these seeds grow. About 1600, the back-formation *pea* arose through the misperception that *pease*, with its sibilant ending, was a plural. Hence, in Modern English *pea* has had two plurals: the irregular *pease* \(which thrived especially from about 1750 to 1850\) and the regular form *peas*. Today *pease* occurs mostly in the set phrase *pease porridge* \(BrE *pease pudding*\). Yet most dictionaries continue to list it as a variant plural.

The cognate word denoting a pea pod—namely, *peascod* /**peez**-kahd/ or \**peasecod*, dating from the 1200s \(Middle English\)—has always been predominantly written *peascod* in Modern English.
