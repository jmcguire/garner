# platypus

platypus. Pl. *platypuses*. Like *octopus* \(pl. *octopuses*—not *\*octopi*\), *platypus* derives from Greek. Hence the faux-Latin plural *\*platypi*, founded on an error, should be avoided. As one writer warns: "Don't expect platypus spotting to be a close-up, cuddly affair. Platypuses \(never platypi\) are shy and elusive." Sue Neales, "In Pursuit of the Platypus," *The Age*, 2 Nov. 1996, at 3. Although H.W. Fowler and other respected usage commentators have always promoted the native-grown plural, writers sometimes go astray—e.g.:

 - "How do you even start to invent a fractured, empty soul for your generation while there are kangaroos and duckbilled *platypi* \[read *platypuses*\] boinging past your window?" Bruno Maddox, "Australia: Not That Boring," *N.Y. Times*, 23 Feb. 1997, § 7, at 11.

 - "*Platypi* \[read *Platypuses*\] were dissected in an attempt to show that they were fakes. We now know that *platypi* \[read *platypuses*\] are indeed real animals and can explain their odd characteristics in the light of what we now know about the unique evolution of the animal life of the Australian land mass." Ian Marsh, *Theory and Practice in Sociology* 16 \(2014\).

 - "The filtered water enters a large mature wetland, which is home to eels, galaxids, trout, and *platypi* \[read *platypuses*\]." Frances Bender, "Huon Proud to Claim Meadowbank as Our Salmon Hatchery," *Mercury* \(Hobart, Aus.\), 13 Aug. 2021.

Even if the first example was intended to be jocular, the humor would not have been diminished by the doubly alliterative -*puses* form.

It may be a lost cause in Australia, where various sports teams are called the "Platypi." Perhaps we can understand that athletes would be reluctant to call themselves the "Platypuses."

## Language-Change Index

 - *\*platypi* for *platypuses*: Stage 1
 - Current ratio in print \(*platypuses* vs. *\*platypi*\): 18:1
