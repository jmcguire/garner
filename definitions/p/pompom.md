# pompom

pompom; **pompon**. The decorative tuft of strands used by cheerleaders was originally called by its French name, *pompon*, which in English dates from the 1750s. \(The cheerleading sense arose in the 1920s.\) This spelling is still quite common, even preferred in some current dictionaries. E.g.: "In case Red Bone wasn't enough, the Chinese organizers brought in a red-clad cheerleading squad which danced to Latin beat music waving little fans instead of *pompons* during breaks." Peter Fimrite, "Jumpin' Cyclists Are Smash in China," *S.F. Chron.*, 22 Aug. 2008, at D8.

But usage has shifted toward *pompom* in recent years because so many people misheard the word, and because of the linguistic tendency toward reduplicative sounds—e.g.:

 - "Home of the state champion *pompom* squad and a General Motors Buick plant, it turned out to be a potential lodging industry jewel." Fred R. Bleakley, "On the Prowl," *Chicago Trib.*, 13 Aug. 1995, at C5.

 - "The viewing party clustered around a television and met \[Sarah\] Palin's speech at the Republican convention in Minnesota with raucous cheers, applause and waving of McCain posters and red *pompoms*." Noelene Clark, "It's About Time," *Seattle Times*, 4 Sept. 2008, at A8.

Today, the newer form, *pompom*, is sanctioned both by the *AP Stylebook* \(2008\) and by *The New York Times Manual of Style and Usage* \(1999\). And *pompom* outranks *pompon* in both modern journalism and in books. It has been the predominant form only since the 1990s.

*Pompom* \(with the variant spelling *pompon*\) is also a generic name for flowers with small blossoms that resemble the decorative tufts, such as dahlias and chrysanthemums. About as far from that pleasant mental picture as you can get is another definition for the hyphenated *pom-pom*: an automatic gun or cannon mounted as part of a set \(especially pairs\), as on a battleship.
