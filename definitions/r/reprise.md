# reprise

reprise.

**A. And *reprisal*.** *Reprise* = \(1\) /ri-**prīz**/ an annual deduction, duty, or payment out of a manor or estate, as an annuity or the like; or \(2\) /ri-**preez**/ \(in music\) a repetition of a theme or \(in the performing arts\) a repetition of a performance or role. In sense 2 the word is often metaphorical. *Reprisal* /ri-**prī**-zәl/ = an act of retaliation, usu. of one nation against another but short of war. *Reprisal* for *reprise* is a malapropism—e.g.: "Unless one party backs down, the battle could escalate into a *reprisal* \[read *reprise*\] of the partisan strife that paralyzed the Senate for several weeks over the summer." Jeremy W. Peters, "Between Democrats and a Push for Filibuster Change, One Nominee," *N.Y. Times*, 30 Oct. 2013, at A12. A clearer choice here would be *repeat*.

**B. For *reprieve*.** Another odd malapropism is the substitution of *reprise* for *reprieve*—e.g.: "It remains true that because of the acceptance of the trade dollar, and its subsequent circulation in the United States, the inflationary interests were granted a *reprise* \[read *reprieve*\] on the issue between 1873 and 1876, when the trade dollar's coinage was discontinued." Richard Hofstadter, *The Paranoid Style in American Politics, and Other Essays* 279–80 \(2012\).
