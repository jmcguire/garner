# rampant

rampant means "widespread, unrestrained," usually in a pejorative sense <a rampant epidemic>. Hence bad things become *rampant in* places; places don't become *\*rampant with* bad things. Yet some writers get this backward—e.g.: "Officials and residents in the area want to revive and showcase the glory that was Bronzeville before it becomes *rampant* \[read *rife*\] with large *tracks* \[read *tracts*\] of vacant land and dilapidated buildings." "Revival of ‘Black Metropolis' Blossoms," *Telegraph Herald* (Dubuque), 24 Sept. 1996, at A7. (For the second error in that sentence, see **track**.)

## Language-Change Index

 - *\*rampant with* for *rife with*: Stage 1
 - Current ratio in print (*corruption was rampant in* vs. \**was* *rampant with corruption*): 20:1
