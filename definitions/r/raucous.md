# raucous

raucous, adj.; **ruckus**, n. The adjective *raucous* \(= disagreeably loud, harsh, and boisterous; chaotically strident\) is sometimes confounded with the noun *ruckus* \(= a noisy, confused disturbance\). The word-swapping goes both ways, mostly *raucous* misused for *ruckus*—e.g.:

 - "Georgia Tech may need to cause a *raucous* \[read *ruckus*\] in the ACC tournament." Shannon Ryan, "Illini Perspiring More Than Inspiring Hope," *Chicago Trib.*, 9 Mar. 2010, Sports §, at 5.

 - "These three female kittens are making a *raucous* \[read *ruckus*\] in the cat room\!" "Animal Shelter," *Los Alamos Monitor*, 16 Nov. 2013, Features §.

 - "Attentive teachers . . . aren't pulling their hair out over Johnnie, who is causing a *raucous* \[read *ruckus*\] in the corner." Bianca Keegan, "Top Mark in Finding School Fit," *Cairns Post* \(Aus.\), 25 June 2016, Bus. §, at 2.

But the opposite error also occurs—e.g.:

 - "The audience was always with them—cheering on cue, making a *ruckus* \[read *raucous*\] noise that trailed off into the night sky." Robert S. Walters, "The Edge Birthday Bash Ushers in Summer," *Tulsa World*, 15 May 2001, at D4.

 - "Nita was fun-loving and was renowned for giving lively parties, including her annual Christmas Caroling Party, where singing and *ruckus* \[read *raucous*\] laughter were heard late into the night." Obituary, *Columbus Dispatch*, 12 Nov. 2021, at A16.

The errors appear about equally often in BrE and AmE. In either variety, they may well elicit a rictus.
