# rest on one's laurels

rest on one's laurels. The *laurel* in this phrase refers literally to the leaf of the bay laurel tree. It is used figuratively to denote honors or victories \(as, in antiquity, a laurel wreath awarded in athletic events or military campaigns\). To *rest on one's laurels* is to stop competing and bask in the glow of past successes. The phrase doesn't work as well with the verb *sit* \(that's no way to show off the symbol of one's triumphs\). Yet *\*sit on one's laurels* has become fairly common in AmE—e.g.:

 - "But American colleges cannot *sit* \[read *rest*\] on their laurels if they are to remain what they tend to consider themselves: the very best." Niall Ferguson, "Still the Head of the Class?," *L.A. Times*, 22 Aug. 2005, at B11.

 - "Our State Fair is the best state fair, but why *sit* \[read *rest*\] on our laurels?" Ken Fuson, "Big Bellies and Beer Men: Let's Spice Up Iowa State Fair," *Des Moines Register*, 18 Aug. 2006, News §, at A13.

 - "Not one to *sit on her laurels and rest* \[read *rest on her laurels*\], Shayna is also active with the local Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals, and is a singer and songwriter." Cindy Elavsky, "Stephanie Speeds Her Way into Salem," *Tulsa World*, 27 Aug. 2006, Television §, at 47.

Of course, people resting on their laurels might also be sitting on their hands.
