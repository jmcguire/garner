# roost

roost \(= to settle down for rest or sleep\), originally an Old English noun, was turned into a verb in the early 1500s. \(See functional shift \(d\).) Having retained both its noun uses and its verb uses, the word is most closely associated with domesticated fowl. It commonly appears in the metaphorical catchphrase *come home to roost* or *chickens have come home to roost*, meaning that someone's past misbehavior or indiscretions are now plaguing the person and causing problems <all the enmity aroused by his past philandering has finally come home to roost> <What can we say about these disciplinary proceedings? His chickens have come home to roost.>.

Occasionally in this phrase—sometimes as a pun, but equally often not—*roost* \(a comparatively uncommon word\) gets displaced by *roast*. But a vague etymological association has stumped etymologists: the phrase *to rule the roost* \(= to be dominant\), dating from the mid-1700s, is much newer than *to rule the roast* \(same sense\), dating from about 1500. But as the *OED* notes, "none of the early examples \[of *to rule the roast*\] throw any light on the precise origin of the expression."

 - Current ratio in print \(*come home to roost* vs. \**come home to roast*\): 263:1
