# woke

woke, adj. Traditionally, of course, this word is the simple-past form of *wake*. But it has acquired a new adjectival sense: "enlightened in matters of social justice." To many observers, it seemed to spring from nowhere in the 2010s and to spread rapidly.

It has a history, naturally. It began in the 1800s as a dialectal word, in African-American English, meaning "not asleep." By the early 1960s, it had come to be used metaphorically—mostly in African-American usage—to mean "fully informed," or more particularly "alert to racial discrimination and other social wrongs." In the 2000s, it became an exhortation: "Stay woke\!" It appeared as a refrain in the 2008 song "Master Teacher," by Erykah Badu. During the 2010s and 2020s, the term was popularized by the Black Lives Matter movement. It appeared in book titles and even served as the name of a 2020 television series. See **African-American English**.

Predictably, the term became controversial—in more ways than one. Pedants who objected that the standard form should be *woken* or *waked* (see **wake**) soon retreated and were heard no more. Some progressives were repelled by the "virtue-signaling" or perceived sanctimoniousness by which some tried to portray themselves as more "woke" than others. And some conservatives objected to the idea that anybody who didn't share the beliefs and attitudes of the "woke movement" were unenlightened. See political correctness.

Hence the term came to be used as either laudatory or disparaging, depending on the user's views. White males who were woke—such as the Archbishop of Canterbury—confessed their white privilege and denounced all the other privileges that inure to straight men of European descent. They especially decried institutional racism. But others came to use the term mockingly in reference to those who claim moral superiority to and greater enlightenment than everybody else: "woke" people became a target on Twitter and elsewhere. Hence there is little consensus about the word's connotations.

The cognate forms *wokeness* and *wokeism* are especially likely to bear negative connotations.
