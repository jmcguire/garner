# whereabout\(s\)

whereabout\(s\). In all uses, *whereabouts* is the predominant form in AmE and BrE alike—as a noun <I don't know her whereabouts>, as an adverb <whereabouts is the house?>, and as a conjunctive adverb <I don't know whereabouts he lives>. The use as a noun is most common—e.g.: "The silver BMW was discovered after authorities, checking a tip on the suspect's *whereabouts*, fanned out in the neighborhood." Robert Rudolph, "Suspect Surfaces in Search," *Star-Ledger* \(Newark\), 23 Sept. 1997, at 1.

The noun may take either a singular or a plural verb—e.g.:

 - "Vicky \(last name unknown\) is Tom's girlfriend; her *whereabouts is* unknown." J.R. Hunter, *Over the Edge* 202 \(2013\).

 - "The advantage of such a program is that the offender's *whereabouts are* known in a more real-time manner." Robert D. Hanser, *Introduction to Corrections* 147 \(2012\).

From 1850 to 1950 or so, the singular and plural uses vied closely for predominance, but beginning in the mid-20th century the plural uses took a decided advantage in both BrE \(before 1950\) and AmE \(beginning in the 1960s\). The plural use is now more common in print than the singular. Cf. **\*thereabouts**.

Current ratio in print \(*his whereabouts are* vs. *his whereabouts is*\): 3:1
