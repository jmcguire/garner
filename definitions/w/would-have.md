# would have

would have for *had*, in a conditional statement, is an example of a confused sequence of tenses. standard english calls for *if he had* or *if I had*, not *\*if he would have* \[\+ p.pl.\] or *\*if I would have* \[\+ p.pl.\]—e.g.:

 - "If the trial judge *would have* \[read *had*\] allowed impeachment with a limiting instruction . . . , Robinson would be before this court arguing that this alternative solution was error." *United States v. Robinson*, 783 F.2d 64, 68 (7th Cir. 1986).

 - " ‘It would have been more eventful today if we *would have* \[read *had*\] won.' " Mike Cochran, "Four's a Crowd: TCU Tops Tech, Shares in SWC Crown," *Austin Am.-Statesman*, 26 Nov. 1994, at C2 (quoting Spike Dikes, Texas Tech's coach).

*Would have* \[\+ p.pl.\] for *had* \[\+ p.pl.\] is especially common in the Southwest, probably from contamination by *could have* \[\+ p.pl.\]. See tenses (b).

## Language-Change Index

 - *\*if I would have* for *if I had*: Stage 2
 - Current ratio in print (*if I had gone* vs. *\*if I would have gone*): 23:1
