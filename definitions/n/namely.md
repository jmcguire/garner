# namely

namely is generally preferable to *viz.* or *to wit*. But often all three expressions can be avoided—e.g.: "There's a big, juicy, column-ready event hanging out there, *namely* the Microsoft CEO summit." Kevin Maney, "Computer Spins Technology Tale," *USA Today*, 15 May 1997, at B5. (A possible revision: *There's a big, juicy, column-ready event hanging out there: the Microsoft CEO summit*.) See **viz**. & **to(-)wit**.

*Namely* should usually be followed by a comma, as are equivalent terms such as *to wit*, *for example*, and *that is*. But writers increasingly skip the comma after this single-word introduction—e.g.: "\[Meyer\] Schapiro's understanding of Romanesque art is based on an essential belief about humankind, *namely* \[read *namely,*\] that individuality—the sense of it, the yearning for it, the struggle for it—is quite simply the core of the human." Jed Perl, "The Varieties of Artistic Experience," *New Republic*, 30 Oct. 2006, at 23.

This type of phrase or clause specifies, clarifies, or gives one or more examples of what preceded. Because it's nonrestrictive, it should be set off from the sentence. Not doing so usually results in a sentence that's tough to get through—e.g.: "That's tricky when some *signals namely equity markets* \[read *signals, namely, equity markets,*\] hitting new highs are bullish." Jon Birger, "Can the Economy Survive the Housing Bust?," *Fortune*, 13 Nov. 2006, at 189 (*namely, equity markets* might also be set off by em-dashes or parentheses).
