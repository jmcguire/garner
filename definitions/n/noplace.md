# \*noplace

\*noplace. This dialectal word, answering to the standard *anyplace*, was fairly common in the 1700s, but it became stigmatized by the mid-1800s. Although it was moderately on the rise in print sources during the 1980s and 1990s, it remains nonstandard for *nowhere* or *no place*—e.g.: "As the Joads' plight continues to worsen there's no work, no food, *noplace* \[read *nowhere* or *no place*\] decent to live." Doug Mason, "Power of Steinbeck's Book Evident on Stage," *Knoxville News-Sentinel*, 4 Feb. 1995, at B6. Cf. **\*anyplace** & **someplace**.

Language-Change Index

*\*noplace* for *nowhere*: Stage 2
Current ratio in print \(*nowhere* vs. \**noplace*\): 2,429:1
