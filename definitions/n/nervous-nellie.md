# nervous Nellie

nervous Nellie; \***nervous Nelly**. In 1879, in N. D'Anvers's novel *Parted*, the author writes that the sight of a large Newfoundland dog "made nervous Nelly tremble." That isolated use may or may not have influenced the alliterative catchphrase, which took hold in the 1920s and spread. More likely, the immediate influence was the playwright Edward Henry Peple, whose *Pair of Sixes* was novelized by Lilian Lauferty in 1914; in the novel version, a character fakes a telephone call and glances at "the evidently nervous Nellie with a smile." The predominant spelling has been *nervous Nellie* ever since: the phrase spread rapidly in the 1920s, when it was often applied to Senator and later Secretary of State Frank B. Kellogg. It denotes, of course, a fretful, timid, and unduly worried person.

 - Current ratio in print \(*nervous Nellie* vs. \**nervous Nelly*\): 3:1
