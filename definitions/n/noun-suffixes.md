# Noun Suffixes

Noun Suffixes. Although there are many other noun suffixes (*-age, -ance, -ence -hood, -ty, -ure*, etc.), five engender enough doubt or curiosity to occupy space in a usage guide: *-cy*, *-ion*, *-ity*, *-ment* & *-ness*. They are discussed below.

**A. -*cy*.** Deriving from French, Latin, and Greek, *-cy* appears primarily in five types of combined forms—those ending *-acy* (*delicacy*, *profligacy*), *-ancy* (*buoyancy*, *infancy*), *-ency* (*emergency*, *tendency*), *-cracy* (*democracy*, *plutocracy*), and *-mancy* (*chiromancy*, *hydromancy*). It also occurs in such words as *policy*, *prophecy*, and *secrecy*. Because abstract nouns ending in *-ency* correspond to so many adjectives and nouns ending in *-ent* (*agent*/*agency*, *fluent*/*fluency*, *proficient*/*proficiency*), the *-cy* suffix has also come to be used for similar-sounding words ending in *-n* (*chaplaincy*, *lieutenancy*) or in *-t* (*bankruptcy*, *baronetcy*).

For a discussion of *-ence* vs. *-ency*, as well as *-ance* vs. *-ancy*, see -ce/-cy. See also **normalcy**.

**B. *-ion*.** Deriving from French and Latin, *-ion* typically appears in noun loanwords of condition or action. Although a few such words appeared in Old English (450–1099), most date from the periods of Middle English (1100–1499) and Early Modern English (1500–1699). In most Latinate nouns, the suffix has been joined to the past-participial or supine stem of the Latin verb (*completio*, *damnatio*, *solutio*, etc.).

A few *-ion* nouns aren't of Romance origin at all: some are formed in English by analogy (*demotion* as a term to correspond with *promotion*), and sometimes *-ation* is added to native-English elements (e.g., *flirtation*, *scatteration*, *starvation*). See morphological deformities.

In fact, *-ation* has been a productive suffix in Modern English. It's the standard way of producing nouns from verbs ending in *-ate* (*dislocation*, *exaggeration*, *impersonation*, *infiltration*, etc.), *-ify* (*beautification*, *fortification*, *personification*, *verification*, etc.), and *-ize* (*authorization*, *categorization*, *personalization*, *realization*, etc.).

The English language contains many dual forms that vary by the choice between *-ion* and *-ness* (see (e)). Typically, the one is formed from a Latin verb stem (*abjection*), the other from an English adjective (*abjectness*). The *-ion* form tends to emphasize an action, condition, or process; the *-ness* form emphasizes a quality or state corresponding to the adjective to which it is added. Examples:

 - abstraction abstractness
 - concision conciseness (see **concision**)
 - consideration considerateness
 - correction correctness
 - evasion evasiveness (see **evasion**)
 - indirection indirectness
 - prohibition prohibitiveness
 - protection protectiveness
 - suggestion suggestiveness

Stylists sometimes say that nouns ending in *-ion* tend to be so cumbersome and so numerous that they need to be kept in check—preferably with no more than one and rarely two in a sentence. See noun plague & zombie nouns.

**C. *-ity*.** Deriving from Latin (*-itās*) through French (*-ité*), the suffix *-ity* expresses a state, condition, or quality primarily in words of Romance origin (*alacrity*, *iniquity*, *propinquity*). These words were typically formed with *-ity* as they were first imported into English; that is, Renaissance neologists tended to change words such as the Latin *veritas* or French *verité* into *verity* at the moment of creating the English version. The suffix typically appears in words in the more formal registers of English (*alkalinity*, *iniquity*, *jollity*, *theatricality*). If the suffix follows an *i*, it becomes *-ety* (*contrariety*, *society*). Words ending in *-ity* invariably have the stress on the preceding syllable (*bellicosity* /bel-i-**kos**-i-tee/, *fatuity* /fa-**tyoo**-i-tee/). Sometimes the result is a shift in the quality of the vowel (*chaste–chastity*, *sincere*–*sincerity*, *viscous–viscosity*). Many nouns ending in *-ity* derive from adjectives ending in *-able* or *-ible*, a few examples being *admissibility*, *collectibility*, *legibility*, *personability*, *reasonability*, *reprehensibility*.

As suggested with *obliqueness*/*obliquity*, the language contains many dual forms that vary by the choice between *-ness* and *-ity*. If there is a nuance, it might be that *-ness* denotes more saliently the quality denoted by the corresponding adjective, and *-ity* a condition or state of being. We also tend to regard the nouns ending in *-ity* as more formal and learned:

 - abstrusity abstruseness (see **abstruseness**)
 - barbarity barbarousness
 - capacity capaciousness
 - clarity clearness
 - commonality commonness (see **commonality**)
 - crudity crudeness
 - debility debilitation (see **debility**)
 - enormity enormousness (see **enormity**)
 - falsity falseness (see **falsehood**)
 - frivolity frivolousness (see **frivolity**)
 - gentility gentleness (see **gentility**)
 - humanity humanness
 - nobility nobleness
 - notoriety notoriousness (see **notorious**)
 - obscurity obscureness
 - perversity perverseness
 - precocity precociousness
 - sagacity sagaciousness
 - sensitivity sensitiveness
 - tenacity tenaciousness
 - turbidity turbidness (see **turbid**)
 - turgidity turgidness
 - voracity voraciousness

Some of these pairs (e.g., *enormousness*/*enormity*, *gentleness*/*gentility*, *notoriousness*/*notoriety*) have undergone differentiation. See **enormity**, **gentility** & **notorious**. See also (d) below.

**D. *-ment*.** Deriving from both French (*-ment*) and Latin (*-mentum*), the English suffix *-ment* forms nouns—often concrete nouns—from verbs in three ways (here listed in order of frequency): (1) to denote the result or product of the action denoted by the verb—hence the usual senses of *abridgment*, *accomplishment*, *advertisement*, *amusement*, *announcement*, *entanglement*, *establishment*; (2) to denote the action of the verb—hence the usual senses of *alignment*, *atonement*, *commencement*, *internment*; and (3) to denote the means or instrument of the action—hence *aliment*, *ornament*, *pigment*. In a few instances, nouns ending in *-ment* have been formed from adjectives, as with the unusual words *dreariment* (1500s) and *oddment* (1700s). Often the older the word in English, the less strongly the *-ment* seems to be a suffix, as opposed to an integral part of the word (as in *garment* \[1300s\] and *testament* \[1300s\]). Since the 1600s, the suffix has been especially productive in complementing words that begin with *be-*, *em-*, and *en-*, as with *bedazzlement*, *befuddlement*, *bewilderment*, *embodiment*, *enforcement*, *enhancement*, *ennoblement*, and *entrenchment*.

**E. *-ness*.** The ending *-ness* typically attaches to adjectives to form nouns. Some linguists call it a "deadjectival suffix" and the resulting words "deadjectival nouns."

Deriving from Old English, *-ness* expresses a state, condition, or quality when combined with adjectives (*fondness*, *weariness*), participles (*forgottenness*, *indebtedness*), and occasionally phrasal adjectives (*matter-of-factness*, *up-to-dateness* \[these are called "dephrasal compounds"\]). Anomalous but attested uses involve appending the suffix to nouns (*milkness*, *womanness*), verbs (*mendness*), pronouns (*me-ness*, *my-ness*, *nothingness*), and adverbs (*whyness*, *whatness*). A few such words—such as *business*, *wilderness*, and *witness*) have become concrete nouns that can take plurals. When combined with an adjective ending in *y*, the vowel changes to *i* (*pettiness*, *silliness*)—an exception being *busyness* (to differentiate it from its older cousin *business*). It is a living suffix that can be used with any adjectives or participles, whatever their origin. Nouns formed with *-ness* ordinarily bear a strong association with the adjective because they are quite noticeably derivative: *obliqueness*, for example, is more noticeably connected to *oblique* than is its counterpart *obliquity*. (See (b) above.) Note that the *-ness* words don't vary the pronunciation of the base adjective in either vowel quality or stress.
