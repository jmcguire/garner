# speed > sped > sped

speed > sped > sped. So inflected. The predominant past-tense and past-participial form is *sped*, not *\*speeded.* It has been so since the 1600s. There's one exception: the phrasal verb *speed up* \(= to accelerate\) has generally made *speeded up*. That was so throughout the English-speaking world during the 1900s. But in both AmE and BrE, *sped up* has greatly \(ahem\) accelerated since 1980. Shortly after 2000 in AmE and after 2011 in BrE, it surpassed *speeded up*. In short, in North America, *speeded up* has slowed down. See irregular verbs. See also **rate of speed**.

Language-Change Index

*\*speeded* for *sped* as past tense and past participle of *speed*: Stage 2
Current ratio in print \(*sped* vs. *\*speeded*\): 11:1
Current ratio in print \(*sped up* vs. *speeded up*\): 4:1
