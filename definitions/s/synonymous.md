# synonymous

synonymous (= equivalent in meaning) is sometimes, in a shocking example of word-swapping, confused with *antonymous* (= opposite in meaning)—e.g.: "The phrase ‘subsequent to' is a preposition, with the same meaning as ‘after' or ‘since.' It is *synonymous* \[read *antonymous*\] with ‘prior to,' which is also a preposition." Gertrude Block, "The Punctuated Lawyer," *Fed. Lawyer*, Jan. 1996, at 21. *\*Subsequent to* is in fact opposed to, not the same as, *prior to*. In tone, it is analogous to *prior to*. There would have been no confusion if the author had written, "It is the opposite of *prior to*." See **prior to**.

*Synonymous* is often misspelled *\*synonomous*—e.g.: "Woodrow Wilson ‘Woody' Hite, the man whose name was *synonomous* \[read *synonymous*\] with big band music in Portland for decades, died Tuesday, Nov. 4, 1997, in Camarillo, Calif." John Foyston, "Big Band Leader Woody Hite Dies at 82," *Oregonian* (Portland), 7 Nov. 1997, at D12.

 - Current ratio in print (*synonymous* vs. *\*synonomous*): 429:1
