# *Statements of necessity:

*Statements of necessity:*

 - "His entrance into the military made it necessary that he *use* the birth-certificate version \[of his name\]." Mike Elfland, "Outspoken Burke Says City Hall Needs Harder Sell to Lure Businesses," *Telegram & Gaz.* \(Worcester\), 19 Sept. 1997, at B1.

 - "It will be necessary that he or she *have* a solid understanding of school finances." Alyssa Roggie, "Candidates Target Budget, Openness," *Intelligencer J.* \(Lancaster, Pa.\), 30 Oct. 1997, at B1.

 - "When the position potentially involves exercising the power of life or death over citizens, it is essential that screening procedures *are* \[read *be*\] in place to keep from hiring people who are temperamentally unsuited for the work." "Is Screening Sufficient?," *Herald* \(Rock Hill, S.C.\), 15 Dec. 1997, at A9.

Formerly, writers used subjunctives with every type of condition, whether contrary to fact or not. Today most of these sound like not-so-quaint archaisms—e.g.:

 - "Its very existence is, therefore, a bulwark against oppression and tyranny, no matter who *be* the potential oppressor or tyrant." Leslie Scarman, *English Law—The New Dimension* 6 \(1974\).

 - "The word processor is a marvelous machine, and no sensible writer, *if such there be*, should scorn it." Stephen White, *The Written Word* 74 \(1984\).

This particular use of *be* in place of *is* fell into disuse quite rapidly during the 20th century. In 1850, the phrasing *If this be true* outnumbered *If this is true* at the start of a sentence by a 6:1 ratio; by 1950, the predominance had shifted: *If this is true* had become more common in print sources than *If this be true* by a 3:1 ratio.

Subjunctives persist in a few idiomatic phrases, such as *Long live the Queen*, *as it were*, *be that as it may*, *far be it from me*, *if need be*, and the literary *would \(that\) it were*. Another example is *be they*—e.g.: "In social situations, a conversation with Justice Brennan is likely as not to focus on the interests of those with whom he is speaking, *be they* judges, politicians and journalists, or waitresses, secretaries and gardeners." Martin Tolchin, "Brennan Described as Self-Effacing, Sociable Irish Pol," *Dallas Morning News*, 22 July 1990, at A12. They also endure in statements of fear or anxiety with the word *lest*. See **lest \(b\)**. See also tenses (a).

**B. Hypothetical Subjunctives About Past Events: *if you had*, etc.; *if you would have*, etc.** In a counterfactual hypothetical relating to a past event, standard english calls for the past-perfect tense in expressing the hypothetical or condition: *If I had been there, I would have . . .*—not \**If I would have been there, I would have . . . .* Hence: *If I had listened to people, I wouldn't have taken this job*. It's nonstandard to repeat the conditional *would*, as in \**If I would have listened to people, I wouldn't have taken this job*. The error occurs most commonly in speech, usually after an *if* or the expression of a wish. In speech it's often reduced to *If I'd 'a* or *I wish I'd 'a*—which some consider a casualism of conversation. But that means it inevitably finds its way into print in the unreduced form. It remains nonstandard—e.g.:

 - "A few years ago, *if you would have tried* \[read *if you had tried*\] to tell the gaming world they'd be using a remote control to play their favorite games, the audience would have laughed you into oblivion." Bill Hutchens, "Potentially Wand-erful," *Morning News Trib.* \(Tacoma\), 12 Sept. 2006, at E1.

 - "I think *if* *I would not have exercised* \[read *if I had not exercised*\], I would have gone crazy." Paris Goodnight, "Don't Try to Rebound Too Quickly," *Salisbury Post* \(N.C.\), 17 May 2020, Lifestyle §.

 - "The other word most used by people is ‘just.' "*If you would have just listened* \[read *If you had just listened*\] to me . . . ." D.D. Meighen, "If: Two Letters, One Big Word," *Times West Virginian*, 24 Sept. 2021, Community §.

 - "I think about them a lot and *wish I would have just asked* \[read *wish I had just asked*\] them how their family was doing or what was new in their life." Toby Moore, "You Can't Win an Argument," *Star-Herald* \(Scottsbluff, Neb.\), 13 Oct. 2021, Editorial §.

Of course, in mistranscribed speech and in tweets, online posts, etc., *would have* sometimes becomes the subliterate \**would of*, creating in this instance a double bobble—e.g.:

 - " ‘*If I would of* \[read *If I had*\] practiced a bit more I definitely would *of* \[read *have*\] won the other ones or at least had a good chance,' Schubert said." Kristi R. Garabrandt, "Steve Schubert Brings Home the Gold," *Daily Jeffersonian* \(Ohio\), 27 June 2021, Sports §.

 - "I *wish I would of* \[read *wish I had*\] seen it whilst I was pregnant\!" Natasha Meek, "Stories of Pregnancy and Motherhood in a Pandemic," *Bradford Telegraph & Argus* \(U.K.\), 28 Jan. 2021, Editorial §.
