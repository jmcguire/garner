# Serial Comma (aka Oxford Comma or Harvard Comma)

Serial Comma (aka Oxford Comma or Harvard Comma). This punctuation mark—the comma before the last item in a list, normally preceded by a conjunction—has been mystifyingly controversial. Social-media posts have long been full of acrimonious debates about it. In response to a Twitter poll that I posted in April 2021, one person said: "You will pry the serial comma from my cold, dead, and moldering hand." Somebody else responded: "No need to be strident, upset or even concerned about the serial comma. When it's necessary, use it. When it's redundant (which it is about 99% of the time), omit it." (Note the lack of a comma after *upset*.)

The results of the poll? Mind you, the 1,336 people who responded see my Twitter feed—so it's not exactly a random sample. They tend to be teachers and professionals of one kind or another, especially professional writers. The question was objectively posed: "What do you think about the serial comma (aka Oxford comma)?" There were four possible answers: favor its use (90%), disfavor its use (4%), don't know what it is (1%), and feel indifferent (5%). The results were much different from the neck-and-neck competition one might have expected.

Some years ago, there was a reference in the popular press to "Alyssha Curry, cookbook author, social-media maven, mother and wife to NBA superstar Stephen Curry." The mother-and-wife reference, with its miscue, caused a minor furor among defenders of the serial comma.

In BrE and in American journalism (influenced by the *AP Stylebook*), the comma is almost always omitted. In published AmE writing apart from journalism and advertising, it's generally included (especially through the influence of *The Chicago Manual of Style*).

Because people learn one convention or the other at a fairly impressionable age, they become indoctrinated. They hold their convictions almost as tenaciously as they might their religious beliefs. It's difficult to change such a deeply held tenet.

What follows might strike you as overdone. But the authorities on the question, to my knowledge, have never been thoroughly canvassed. And the issue is one of the most recurrent in prose composition—as well as one of the most controversial among amateurs. So read closely, if you like, or just browse through the authorities quoted. The examples are worth pondering.

What are the merits on each side of the argument?

**A. Favoring the Serial Comma.** Proponents say that the comma is both logical and helpful. They point out that including it *never* creates an ambiguity, whereas omitting it fairly often does. It's a preventive measure that requires no real thought. The serial comma precludes ambiguities and annoyances at a negligible cost.

Problems arise more and more when the items in the series become longer and more complex. Let's say it's as simple as a list of couples: "We'll invite Herb and Myra, Gary and Mariellen, John and Robert, and Bill and Les." Delete the comma after *Robert*, and you have uncertainty about the relationships. And those items in the series aren't even especially complex. Also, of course, there's a pause after *Robert*—and the couples are paired in a normal oral delivery to show relationships. The comma reflects the natural pause.

Among the authorities favoring the serial comma in all instances are these (in chronological order):

 - **1851:** Goold Brown, *The Grammar of English Grammars* 775 (1851) ("When more than two words or terms are connected in the same construction, or in a joint dependence on some other term, by conjunctions expressed or understood, the comma should be inserted after every one of them but the last . . . : *envy, scorn, remorse, or pride* . . . ; *head, hands, wings, or feet*.").

 - **1859:** Henry Beadnell, *Guide to Typography* 119 (1859) ("Three or more subjects, predicates, or complements, with or without adjuncts, . . . are separated by a comma . . . *industry, honesty, and temperance*.").

 - **1871:** John Wilson, *A Treatise on English Punctuation* 37 (21st ed. 1871) ("Some punctuators omit the comma between the last two particulars, when united by either of the conjunctions *and, or, nor*. But the propriety of using the comma will perhaps be obvious to any one who examines the nature of such sentences; for the last two words of a series are not more closely connected in sense and construction with each other than with the preceding words."). \[To the same effect with slightly different words: John Wilson, *The Elements of Punctuation* 16 (1856).\]

 - **1878:** W.J. Cocker, *Hand-book of Punctuation* 18 (1878) ("When a conjunction is used with the last word in the series, a comma should be placed before the conjunction and between the other words.").

 - **1892:** Paul Allardyce, *Punctuation* 34 (1892) ("When *and* occurs only between the last words of the series, the comma is usually inserted before it. *Trumpets, drums, and kettle-drums contended in noise with the shouts of a numerous rabble*. Many writers omit this comma. But it seems useful.").

 - **1893:** Edmund Shaftesbury, *One Hundred Lessons in Punctuation* 22 (1893) ("Where three or more similar elements are connected *all* should be separated by commas. This is of the greatest importance, and should be adhered to in all circumstances. . . . *We think, feel, and know*.").

 - **1901:** F. Horace Teall, *Punctuation* 4 (1901) ("It is a singular fact that many printers omit the comma before the last of such a series, though there is no plainer occasion for its use. Nearly all authorities prescribe such use of the comma, and the weight of custom, as well as common sense, favors it.").

 - **1914:** Lyle Spencer, *Practical English Punctuation* 36 (1914) ("If in a series of three or more words, phrases, or clauses only the last two members are joined by a conjunction, the comma is inserted before the conjunction just as if there were no connective. This is done to prevent the last two members from seeming to be a single unit in the series.").

 - **1926:** H.W. Fowler, *A Dictionary of Modern English Usage* 24 (1926)—elsewhere referred to in this book as *FMEU1* (calling its consistent use "the only rule that will obviate . . . uncertainties").

 - **1937:** H.A. Treble & G.H. Vallins, *An A.B.C. of English Usage* 56 (Am. ed. 1937) ("Where more than two words or phrases occur together in a sequence a comma should precede the final *and*; e.g. *A great, wise, and beneficent measure*.").

 - **1941:** Robert M. Gay, *Stops: A Handbook for Those Who Know Their Punctuation and for Those Who Aren't Quite Sure* 13 (1941) ("Present usage calls for a comma before *and* or *or* joining the last two members of a series.").

 - **1966:** Sheridan Baker, *The Complete Stylist* 124 (1966) ("The meaningful pause demands a comma. Items in a series are equal, and they silently wait for equal treatment: *words, phrases, or clauses in a series*; *to hunt, to fish, and to hike* . . . . By carefully separating all the elements in a series, you keep alive a fine distinction lost long ago in the daily press.").

 - **1966:** Wilson Follett, *Modern American Usage: A Guide* 398, 401 (Jacques Barzun ed., 1966) (noting that journalists omit it only to save space "in the narrow width of a newspaper column"—and that this is the only cogent argument for its omission).

 - **1972:** Maxwell Nurnburg, *Questions You Always Wanted to Ask About English* 181 (1972) ("When we say ‘The colors of our country are red, white, and blue,' there's a little pause after *white* that seems to ask for a comma to make *blue* just as important as *red* and *white*.").

 - **1974:** David W. Ewing, *Writing for Results in Business, Government, and the Professions* 358 (1974) ("While this rule is not observed by all publishers, it is valid and helpful.").

 - **1974:** *Words into Type* 187 (3d ed. 1974) ("The consistent use of this comma is recommended.").

 - **1980:** Roy H. Copperud, *American Usage and Style: The Consensus* 78–79 (1980) ("Opinion is divided on whether the comma should be used before *and* in a series; the parade, led by newspapers, is turning away from it. Meticulous writing and editing preserves this comma.").

 - **1990:** Richard Dowis, *How to Make Your Writing Reader-Friendly* 144 (1990) ("My feeling about the serial comma is simple: It can't do any harm, and it might do some good. I recommend it.").

 - **1990:** Lawrence H. Freeman & Terry R. Bacon, *Style Guide* 52 (2d ed. 1990) ("A comma separates the last two items in a series although these items are linked by a conjunction . . . . This comma was once considered optional, but the trend is to make it mandatory, especially in technical and business English. Leaving it out can cause confusion and misinterpretation.").

 - **1993:** Karen Elizabeth Gordon, *The New Well-Tempered Sentence* 46 (rev. ed. 1993) ("Three or more elements in a series are separated by commas. When the last two elements (words, phrases, or clauses) in a series are joined by a conjunction, a comma comes before the conjunction—unless you're a journalist.").

 - **1995:** Jane E. Aaron, *The Little, Brown Compact Handbook* 154 (2d ed. 1995) ("The final comma is never wrong, and it always helps the reader see the last two items as separate.").

 - **1996:** Patricia T. O'Conner, *Woe Is I* 137 (1996) ("My advice is to stick with using the final comma.").

 - **2000:** *U.S. Government Printing Office Style Manual* § 8.42, at 131 (2000) ("The comma is used after each member within a series.").

 - **2002:** R.M. Ritter, *The Oxford Guide to Style* 121 (2d ed. 2002) ("If the last item in a list has emphasis equal to the previous ones, it needs a comma to create a pause of equal weight to those that came before.").

 - **2005:** Ann Raimes, *Keys for Writers* 387 (4th ed. 2005) ("Use commas to separate three or more items in a series. Readers see the commas between items in a series and think, ‘This is a list.' " But: "Journalists and British writers often omit a comma before *and*.").

 - **2008:** Laurie G. Kirszner & Stephen R. Mandell, *The Pocket Wadsworth Handbook* 87 (4th ed. 2008) ("To avoid ambiguity, always use a comma before the *and* (or other coordinating conjunction) that separates the last two items in a series: *The downtown area includes a bakery, a florist, a small supermarket with an excellent butcher, and a bookstore.*").

 - **2011:** William A. Sabin, *The Gregg Reference Manual* § 163, at 44 (11th ed. 2011) ("It makes more sense to use a serial comma under all circumstances so as to maintain a consistent style.").

 - **2011:** John Trimble, *Writing with Style* 131 (3d ed. 2011) ("Believe it or not, among professional writers, few style questions are more contentious. Journalists, especially newswriters, reject that comma before *and*, insisting that it's usually superfluous and wastes space. Most usage experts and editors, meanwhile, defend it ardently. I'll side with the defenders here. . . . The beauty of the serial comma is that it instantly signals our arrival at the last item.").

 - **2017:** *The Chicago Manual of Style* § 6.19, at 371 (17th ed. 2017) ("Chicago strongly recommends this widely practiced usage, blessed by Fowler and other authorities.").

 - **2018:** Kate L. Turabian, *A Manual for Writers of Term Papers, Theses, and Dissertations* § 3.68, at 50 (9th ed. 2018) ("Always use a comma before the conjunction that introduces the last item.").

Of the many sources consulted, the authorities favored the serial comma by a 3:1 ratio. The listing above is only a sampling: it could readily be lengthened.

**B. Not Treating the Serial Comma as Mandatory.** The authorities not requiring the serial comma often say that it's redundant on either of two rationales: (1) because the conjunction at the end of a series (either *and* or *or*) signals the last item, so that a comma simply isn't necessary; or (2) because the commas earlier in the series simply represent the omission of a conjunction, which of course is present before the final item. But the authorities defending this view tend to be much less dogmatic than their disciples are. That is, they admit of exceptions, and they demand judgment calls in each instance. Curiously, though, the students who have learned the nonmandatory view, as shown in social-media posts, also tend to become staunchly averse to serial commas.

Among the authorities allowing the omission of the serial comma are these (in chronological order):

 - **1953:** Eric Partridge, *You Have a Point There* 16 (1953) ("To say that the \[serial comma\] is incorrect would be wrong. But the \[final commas in *Jack, Jill, and Tom* and *He, she, and I*\] are excessive, for they perform no useful work.").

 - **1958:** John E. Warriner & Francis Griffith, *English Grammar and Composition: Grade 10* 435 (1958) ("When the last two items in a series are joined by *and*, you may omit the comma before the *and* if the comma is not necessary to make the meaning clear. Some publishers, however, insist on this comma, whether or not it is necessary.").

 - **1965:** Theodore M. Bernstein, *The Careful Writer: A Modern Guide to English Usage* 362 (1965) ("Should it be ‘The colors are red, white and blue' or ‘The colors are red, white, and blue'? The answer is either; the authorities do not agree and never will agree on whether a comma should or should not precede the ‘and.' The only rule is that once you have made your choice you should follow it consistently.").

 - **1977:** Boyd B. Burnside & William R. Burnside, "A Writer's Handbook of Basic Composition," in *The Writer's Manual* 881, 896 (1977) ("There is a tendency today to leave out the comma before *and* if the series is only three items.").

 - **1994:** Colin Inman, *The Financial Times Style Guide* 121 (1994) ("In a sequence of items do not put a comma before *and* unless leaving it out would lead to ambiguity.").

 - **1994:** J.A. (Sandy) McFarlane & Warren Clements, *The Globe and Mail Style Book* 281 (1994) ("Note the absence of a comma before the *and*; this omission is Globe style.").

 - **1998:** Santi V. Buscemi, Albert Nicolai & Richard Strugala, *The Basics: A Rhetoric and Handbook* 345 (2d ed. 1998) ("You may leave out the comma before the final item in a series if you are sure readers will not misread the sentence.").

 - **1999:** Thomas H. Bivins, *Public Relations Writing* 325 (4th ed. 1999) ("Many people always include the final comma after \[read *before*\] the *and* in a coordinate series. . . . Others always leave it out. It's up to you, of course, but remember to leave it in if there is any chance of confusion.").

 - **1999:** Allan M. Siegal & William G. Connolly, *The New York Times Manual of Style and Usage* 74 (rev. ed. 1999) ("In general, do not use a comma before *and* or *or* in a series: *The snow stalled cars, buses and trains.* But do use a comma in sentences like this to avoid confusion: *A martini is made of gin and dry vermouth, and a chilled glass is essential*."). \[The example there doesn't even involve a serial comma: it's a "series" of two followed by an independent clause.\]

 - **2000:** Bill Walsh, *Lapsing into a Comma* 81 (2000) ("Serial Commas. Newspapers, by and large, don't use 'em. Other publications usually do. So it would be *red, white and blue* in the Washington Post but *red, white, and blue* in your doctoral thesis. But what a lot of newspaper editors don't know is that the serial comma is sometimes necessary even in newspaper style.").

 - **2001:** James Aitchison, *Cassell's Guide to Written English* 92 (rev. ed. 2001) ("A comma is usually optional between the second-last item in the list and the word *and*, but in some lists a final comma is an aid to the meaning.").

 - **2003:** Paula LaRocque, *The Book on Writing* 224 (2003) ("It is usually acceptable . . . to omit the serial comma if no confusion arises. But editors should refrain from automatically deleting it.").

 - **2003:** Laurie E. Rozakis, *The Complete Idiot's Guide to Grammar and Style* 210 (2d ed. 2003) ("The comma before *and* in a series of items is optional.").

 - **2005:** R.L. Trask, *Say What You Mean* 67 (2005) ("In most British usage, no comma is used before the word *and* or *or* \[in a series\], unless such a comma is required to avoid ambiguity: *My favorite opera composers are Mozart, Puccini, Gilbert and Sullivan, and Rossini*."). \[Consider the example, too, if *Gilbert and Sullivan* were last and the serial comma omitted\!\]

 - **2010:** Joseph M. Williams & Gregory G. Colomb, *Style: Lessons in Clarity and Grace* 227 (10th ed. 2010) ("There is the matter of punctuating a series of three or more coordinated elements. A few omit it, but most insist a comma must always precede the \[*and*\]. . . . Both are correct, but be consistent.").

 - **2013:** Bill Walsh, *Yes, I Could Care Less* 126 (2013) ("The serial comma, or Oxford comma, . . . is debatable today. I have no strong feelings about it—for every example in which it resolves ambiguity I could present a counterexample in which it creates ambiguity—except in a series containing an embedded conjunction. My background is in newspaper journalism, and so I generally omit the serial comma."). \[Yet he cites no counterexamples.\]

 - **2014:** Sir Ernest Gowers, *Plain Words: A Guide to the Use of English* 251 (Rebecca Gowers ed., rev. ed. 2014) ("*The company included ambassadors, ministers, bishops and judges*. In a sentence such as this one commas are always put after each item in the series up to the last but one, but practice varies about putting a comma between the last but one, and the *and* introducing the last.").

 - **2020:** *The Associated Press Stylebook* 336 (55th ed. 2020) ("Do not put a comma before the conjunction in most simple series: *The flag is red, white and blue. He would nominate Tom, Dick, Harry or Jeannette. She goes to school, plays league soccer and takes private dance lessons*.").

One interesting difference between the nonobligatory authorities is the question of consistency. Some say to use it only when necessary (e.g., Warriner & Griffith); others say to be consistent throughout (e.g., Williams & Colomb). Does the latter view mean that if it's *ever* necessary in any particular sentence, it's *always* necessary throughout the entire piece?

Another relevant point is that the nonobligatory authorities all concede that there are exceptions in which the comma is required: (1) if any item in the series contains an *and* or *or* (*He read nursery rhymes about Jack and Jill, Jack Sprat and his wife, and the owl and the pussycat*); and (2) if any item in the series contains an internal comma, the items must be separated with semicolons—and a semicolon must precede the *and*. But there are other exceptions: (3) if the last two items in the series might look like an appositive of the first (*For the outing, we needed to pack lunch, chocolates and sodas*—where the chocolates and sodas are actually in addition to the lunch, not lunch itself); and (4) if the last item looks as if it could be a continuation of the next-to-last (*Thomas bought a car, a new television, some antique books and furniture*—is the furniture antique? Or yet again: *The soldier recounted what he missed about being home: his dog, his little brother, the odor of his father's pipe and his girlfriend*—is the nostalgia about the girlfriend mainly olfactory?).

For those who say the *and* provides the pause before the last item, that's simply not true. It's a syncopated beat, not a beat equal to the other pauses. So the pause theory doesn't go far.

**C. Some Conundrums.** In popular commentary on the serial comma, advocates for one position or the other commonly cite two recurrent issues. The first involves the possibility of conjoined items in the next-to-last and last positions—conjoined items that aren't meant to be so. The second involves miscues relating to actual or supposed appositives.

As for ambiguous conjoinings, problems can arise in various ways. One occurs when a trailing modifier in the last member of the series might modify both the last item and the penultimate one. E.g.: *He is an employee at the Ministry of Magic, a husband and father of three school-age children*. (The *of*-genitive doesn't relate to *husband*—though it can certainly prompt a momentary distraction.) Or, with different syntax, the last item might be misread to be the direct object of a verb or participle in the next-to-last item: *Shown in this photo are Prince William, Prince George, Catherine holding her daughter and the Queen*. (Is the Queen competing for space on Catherine's lap?)

But sentences involving supposed appositives cause the funniest examples of confusion. Normally there will be a category term and specific names. Notice that the first three examples involve a plural category with the omission of the comma, while the last involves a singular category with the inclusion of the comma:

 - *They invited the strippers, JFK and Stalin*.

 - *We went for a walk with our dogs, grandma and grandpa.*

 - *Among those interviewed were Merle Haggard's two ex-wives, Kris Kristofferson and Robert Duvall.*

 - *I dedicate this book to my mother, Mother Theresa, and God.*

Such instances inevitably involve unusual juxtapositions. They are generally contrived.

Occasionally, a specific name comes first, followed by descriptors without names—descriptors that could be read as appositives. E.g.: *Highlights of his global tour include encounters with Nelson Mandela, an 800-year-old demigod and a dildo collector*. In that bizarre series, the possibility that two nouns following *Mandela* (namely, *demigod* and *collector*) are appositives creates humor, whether intentionally or not.

**D. Recommendation.** The first thing to realize is that publications have a "house style" that you'll be required to follow if you hope to appear in their pages. If the house style is to generally omit the serial comma, so be it. Accede to the demands of your editors.

But what should the house style ideally be? What should your own style be?

Suppose you had a rule in your jurisdiction that at any intersection with four stop signs, you must come to a complete halt only if there's other traffic at the intersection. If you know it's safe because no other vehicles are at the intersection, you may glide through slowly. Just use your judgment.

Or would you prefer a bright-line rule: whenever an intersection has four stop signs, you must come to a complete halt—no movement forward—before proceeding. It doesn't matter whether it's 3 a.m. and nobody else can be seen on the road.

My own preference is for bright-line rules that lead to consistency. The weight of authority, too, is on that side of this particular question. Yet one can understand why newspapers would prefer to omit these commas to squeeze more words onto lines (cumulatively, it matters in their narrow columns of type). So perhaps the best rule is this: unless you're a newspaper editor, prefer the serial comma. Some would expand the exception: unless you're a newspaper editor or a British writer, prefer the serial comma.

Why should writers—who are cognitively busy and often under pressure when composing—have to stop and consider whether anyone might misread a list they are composing or perhaps have just composed? As we know, people aren't necessarily good at seeing how others might misread their words. And so the default, for writers who tend to omit the serial comma, is to omit it even when it would be most helpful. But if they include it, they've suddenly created inconsistencies in their prose.

The convention of uniform inclusion obviates needless worries and in no way depletes a scarce resource: it's not as if we have only a finite supply of commas available. Even minimalists in punctuation typically don't see lists as a place for minimalism.
