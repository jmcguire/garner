# sere

sere; **sear**. Although both spellings have been used for the adjective meaning "dried and withered," *sere* is standard. In that sense, *sear* is a variant to be avoided. But *sear* has legitimate senses, first as a verb: \(1\) "to burn quickly with powerful heat"; \(2\) "to have an extremely strong sudden and unpleasant impact on, as by scorching"; or \(3\) "to briefly cook the surface of \(food\) at high temperature." *Sear* is also two nouns: first, referring to a scar or mark by the quick burns of searing; and second, the catch that holds the hammer of a gun's lock.

Current ratio in print \(*sere and yellow leaf* vs. \**sear and yellow leaf *\): 2.5:1 \[This is an allusion to *Macbeth*, and a slight misquotation.\]
