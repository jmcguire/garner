# slake

slake (= to quench or satisfy), an Old English word dating from about AD 1000, most often appears in the phrase *to slake (someone's) thirst*. The word is etymologically related to *slack* (= not tight or taut), and one of the long-obsolete functions of *slake* is as a synonym of *slacker*. But the words had mostly gone their separate ways by the 1600s—even though the *OED* attests five uses of \**slacking thirst* from the 1600s to the early 1900s.

In fact, big data will confirm that \**slacked his thirst* has occurred infrequently since the mid-1800s and even less frequently before. The better and far more prevalent usage is *slaked his thirst*.

 - Current ratio in print (*slaked his thirst* vs. \**slacked his thirst*): 108:1
