# sight unseen

sight unseen. From a strictly logical point of view, the phrase makes little sense. In practice, however, it has an accepted and useful meaning: "\(of an item\) bought without an inspection before the purchase." In this sense, it dates from late-19th-century AmE, but now it is used throughout the English-speaking world. \(See illogic \(a\).) The phrase has been common since the early 20th century and is now used throughout the English-speaking world.

Sometimes the phrase is erroneously written *\*site unseen*—e.g.: "Experts say the Web could be even more dangerous than the telephone because the medium will soon showcase virtual walk-throughs of property and homes for sale, in which purchases could be hustled *site unseen* \[read *sight unseen*\]." Bradley Inman, "Real Estate on the Web," *San Diego Union-Trib.*, 20 July 1997, at H3. See **site**.
