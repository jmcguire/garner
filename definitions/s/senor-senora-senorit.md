# señor, señora, señorita

señor, señora, señorita; **senhor, senhora, senhorita**; **signor, signora, signorina**. The first three are courtesy titles in Spanish: *señor* /say-**nyor**/ or /sen-**yor**/ \(for a man—equivalent to *Mr.*\); *señora* /say-**nyor**-ә/ or /sen-**yor**-ә/ \(for a married woman—equivalent to *Mrs.*\); *señorita* /say-nyә-**ree**-tә/ or /sen-yә-**ree**-tә/ \(for an unmarried woman—equivalent to *Miss*\).

The Portugese or Brazilian counterparts are *senhor* /sen-**yor**/, *senhora* /sen-**yaw**-rә/, and *senhorita* /sen-yә-**ree**-tә/.

The Italian counterparts are *signor* /seen-**yor**/ \(formerly often *\*signior*\), *signora* /seen-**yor**-ә/ \(formerly sometimes *\*signiora*\), and *signorina* /seen-yә-**reen**-ә/ \(no recorded instances of the alternative *\*signiorina*\).

For the Spanish forms, the tilde over the *n* is traditionally optional in English-language contexts, but the relative ease of producing it today on computer screens and handheld devices militates in its favor. See diacritical marks.

Like *miss* in English, the feminine titles are falling from favor. Using them in English-language writing seems to court stereotypes.
