# swear \> swore \> sworn

swear \> swore \> sworn. So inflected. In nonstandard English, the simple-past *swore* often displaces the past-participial *sworn*—e.g.:

 - "The soup also had carrot, cabbage, big wedges of yucca—which I could have *swore* \[read *sworn*\] were potatoes." Ryan DuVall, "Salvadoran Spot Shines with Flavor," *Ft. Wayne J.-Gaz.*, 27 Jan. 2013, at D1.

 - "I could have *swore* \[read *sworn*\] that he added, ‘nothing good comes from losing to Drake.' " Todd Golden, "Down in the Valley," *Trib.-Star* (Terre Haute, Ind.), 5 Jan. 2020.

 - "An officer said Mr Aitken had *swore* \[read *sworn*\] and struggled violently after being asked to stop." Stacey Mullen, "Taxi Driver Who Lashed Out Is Refused Licence," *Glasgow Times*, 24 Mar. 2021, at 11.

It's an irregular verb. Hence today you *swear*, yesterday you *swore*, and many times you have *sworn*.

Swear off using the weak form *\*sweared*, which most often appears in reported speech but sometimes in straight prose—e.g.:

 - "Goodwin *. . .* stormed out of McKeon's office, *sweared* \[read *swore*\] a bit, told teammates he was quitting, changed into street clothes and left the clubhouse." Dan Hoyt, "The Rundown," *Kansas City Star*, 8 Aug. 1997, at D4.

 - "Many of Jarvis' former Tribune colleagues *. . .* also once *sweared* \[read *swore*\] by the place." Charles Leroux, "An Italian Brigadoon," *Chicago Trib.*, 12 Jan. 2003, Mag. §, at 17.

 - "He said the ‘ringleader' had shouted and *sweared* \[read *sworn*\] at him, continually pestering him to be allowed to travel in the luggage compartment." "Yate Bus Driver Banned After Pupils Travel in Luggage Bay," *Gaz. Series* (U.K.), 9 June 2018.

For a discussion of the redundancy of *sworn affidavit*, see **affidavit**.
