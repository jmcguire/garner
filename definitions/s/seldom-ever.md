# \*seldom ever

\*seldom ever. In this phrase—which seems to be a collapsed form of *seldom if ever*—the word *ever* is superfluous. True, the usage dates back to the early 1600s, but it can hardly be recommended despite that history. E.g.: "And as everyone knows, Fleck, who *seldom ever* \[read *seldom*\] missed a meeting, will attend those sessions as long as he is able." Madeleine Mathias, " ‘Lafayette Treasure' Gets New Title at 98," *Morning Call* \(Allentown, Pa.\), 27 May 1997, at B1. Since the late 1700s, *seldom if ever* has steadily occurred more frequently in print. Occasionally the confused phrasing *\*seldom or ever* occurs. Cf. **\*rarely ever**.

Language-Change Index

*\*seldom ever* for *seldom*: Stage 2
Current ratio in print \(*seldom* vs. *\*seldom ever*\): 640:1
