# shambles

shambles. This word has borne several meanings since it was first used in Old English—in the 800s. Originally \(1\) "a table for exposing goods for sale," by the 900s it had acquired the more specialized sense \(2\) "a table or stall for the sale of meat." By the 1500s, it was common to use the word in the extended sense \(3\) "a slaughterhouse" and to construe it as either plural <the shambles were> or singular <this shambles> <a shambles>. Late in the 1500s, the word acquired the figurative sense \(4\) "a place of carnage or wholesale slaughter"—most often with the indefinite article <a shambles of dead bodies>. The word's meaning was stable for centuries. Then, in the 1920s, Americans started to use the word in the attenuated sense \(5\) "a ruin, mess, or scene of disorder" <they left the place in a shambles>. As that meaning caught on and spread across World Englishes, some usage commentators objected: they wanted bloodiness to be part of the word's essential meaning. They failed in that effort, and sense 5 became the word's primary meaning—as it remains today.

In terms of English usage, the principal question today is whether the phrase is preferably *in a shambles* or *in shambles*. All four illustrative quotations in the *OED* reflect *in a shambles*. Yet since 1980, the indefinite article has predominantly been dropped, doubtless because of the anomaly involved in using a singular determiner before a noun seemingly plural in form. The situation today is that highly literate people are likely to say *in a shambles* and all others to say *in shambles*—and the literary usage is in decline. Both must be accepted as standard, which is not to say that the word's modern senses or uses are at all in a shambles.
