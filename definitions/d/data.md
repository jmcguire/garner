# data

data \(/**day**-tә/ or /**dat**-ә/\) is in a small class of words: like *none*, it can correctly take either a singular or a plural verb. It can be seen as either a mass noun or a count noun. The change has come comparatively swiftly: in 1980, *data are* was three times as common as *data is*, but today—according to big data \(no less\!\)—the two are in equipoise.

If you're a proponent of *data are*, please read on. And hold on to your hat.

The word *data* wasn't common in English until the 1920s or so. By the 1940s, it had surged as a succinct way of saying "facts and figures." It was typically plural. By the 1960s, a historian of the English language could confidently write: "A student with one year of Latin \[knows\] that *data* and *phenomena* are plural." Albert C. Baugh, "The Gift of Style," 34 Pa. Bar Ass'n 101, 105–06 \(1962\). But things changed dramatically after that: it's not so simple anymore, and *data* can no longer be classified with *phenomena*. See **phenomenon**.

By 1980, the singular use had become common enough that the experts were divided. Roy H. Copperud, who tried to find consensus among usage critics, found that the singular use was "disapproved by four critics and considered acceptable by four." Copperud, *American Usage and Style: The Consensus* 99 \(1980\). About the same time, the *American Heritage Dictionary*'s usage panel was split half against and half in favor. The shift toward acceptability of the singular was well under way.

Formed as a Latinate plural, *data* has most traditionally been construed with a plural verb, especially in formal writing. In the following examples, we might speculate whether the plural constructions resulted from editorial intervention or authorial punctiliousness—e.g.:

 - "While recent U.S. Census *data show* that the average working woman's pay has declined in the '90s, highly educated, high-paid women keep gaining ground." Jeanhee Kim, "The New Way to Get Rich? Get Married\!," *Money*, Oct. 1996, at 141.

 - "The *data are* derived from tests performed on expectant mothers." "The Plague," *Economist*, 24 Mar. 2001, at 53.

 - "The states' *data do* affirm that vaccinated people are far less likely to become severely ill or to die from Covid-19." Apoorva Mandavilli, "Rethinking the Risks as the Cases Climb for Vaccinated Americans," *N.Y. Times*, 18 Aug. 2021, at A1, A14.

Today the singular uses are just as common, and not only in the phrase *big data*. With that phrase, in fact, the ratio in book sources now is 4:1 in favor of the singular. *Big data* tends to be viewed en bloc, and not as a collection of discrete items. But much the same can be said of *data* itself in the eyes of many contemporary writers—e.g.:

 - "There *isn't any data*." Graham Wilson, Foreword, *A Linguistics Reader* ix \(Graham Wilson ed., 1967\).

 - "No *data is* offered to suggest that women are being adversely hit by the dearth of articles." "A Woman's Place Is in the Law," *Globe & Mail* \(Toronto\), 24 Aug. 1993, at A14.

 - "Now NRG, hit by charges that its *data isn't* reliable, has some competition." "Gallup Goes to Hollywood," *Newsweek*, 28 Mar. 1994, at 43.

 - "Browsing the World Wide Web these days is less like surfing than like crawling: *data drips* like molasses onto your computer screen, sometimes taking several minutes to create a single page of text." Michael Krantz, "Wired for Speed," *Time*, 23 Sept. 1996, at 54.

 - "New census *data* *shows* how population shifts over the past decade in Texas . . . will strengthen big cities and their suburbs." Mimi Swartz, "Texas Needs New Leadership," *N.Y. Times*, 24 Aug. 2021, at A16.

It's likely that the singular uses were fueled by computerese and the language of science. But no matter. They're now standard english. *Data* in the singular sense is essentially synonymous with *information*. But if you try to replace *data* with *information* in all instances, you'll soon see that it often doesn't work. Many times it feels unidiomatic. \**Big information* is just silly.

Interestingly, too, *data* can't be plural in the same way as normal English count nouns. You can't say \**three data* or \**four data* the way you say *three birds* or *four trees*. That's a telltale indicator that it's a mass noun: nobody can quantify data. Yet the phrases *three criteria* and *three phenomena* are entirely natural.

In one particular use, *data* is overwhelmingly treated as plural: when it begins a clause and is not preceded by a determiner such as the definite article. E.g.:

 - "*Data* over the last two years *suggest* that the rate at which gay men get AIDS has finally begun to flatten out." Lawrence K. Altman, "Who's Stricken and How: AIDS Pattern Is Shifting," *N.Y. Times*, 5 Feb. 1989, at 1.

 - "Available *data suggest* otherwise." Ted Bennett, "Needling over Vaccine Should Be Put on Hold," *Portland Press Herald* \(Me.\), 29 Aug. 2021, at D.

With that type of construction, often at the beginning of a sentence, the plural is five times more common than the singular.

*Datum*, the singular count noun, is comparatively infrequent. It always needs a determiner before it. Hence you can't say \**Datum is suspect*—it invariably takes a *the* or *this* or other determiner. \(That's the crucial test for count nouns.\) *Datum* is unusual because it occurs only in the speech or writing of scientists and other highly educated people using a formal register—e.g.:

 - "We accept the law as a necessary *datum*, but that is not to say that we are required to accept it in abeyance of our critical faculties." F.R. Leavis, *The Common Pursuit* 166–67 \(1952\).

 - "The inclination of the orbital plane to our line of sight is another desirable *datum*." Philip A. Charles & R. Mark Wagner, "Black Holes in Binary Stars," *Sky & Telescope*, May 1996, at 38.

 - "Confident that my brain—assuming it had any blood left in it—would react hysterically to any syllables remotely sounding like ‘eject,' I took comfort in this *datum*." Christopher Buckley, "How I Went Nine Gs in an F-16 and Only Threw Up Five Times," *Forbes*, 23 Sept. 1996, at 116.

In nonscientific contexts, *datum* is likely to sound like a pretentious substitute for *fact*. See formal words.

Because *data* can function as either a plural or a singular noun, both *many data* and *much data* are arguably correct. In fact, though, *much data* became more frequent than *many data* in published books about 1990, and it remains more common today. Both forms have long appeared in print—e.g.:

 - "Numerous expert and representative interests are consulted, and *many data* assembled, often over a long period." Carleton K. Allen, *Law in the Making* 433 \(7th ed. 1964\). \(The verb *are* is understood after *data*. See understood words.\)

 - "But *much of the data* in present personnel files *is* highly subjective." William O. Douglas, *Points of Rebellion* 21 \(1970\).

 - "Finding out exactly how *much digital data makes* its way from smartphone apps or an Internet-connected PC to data brokers and digital advertisers is an uphill battle." Deborah Todd & Chris Potter, "Privacy Takes a Hit While Digital Data Is Scooped Up, Passed Around," *Pitt. Post Gaz*., 6 Sept. 2015, at A1.

 - "The transfer of soil data to digital format has allowed a wide range of interpretations, but *many data are* still not freely available as *they* are held by a number of different bodies." "Reports on General Science Findings from University of Ft. Hare Provide New Insights," *Science Letter*, 21 Aug. 2015, at 534.

Yet because *data* isn't countable, it's an easy call that the phrase *much data* is preferable to *many data*. See count nouns and mass nouns.

It's unusual to have a common English noun that can switch-hit as plural or singular. *Data* is among the select few. See plurals \(b\).
