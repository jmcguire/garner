# dilatory

dilatory \(= \[1\] tending to delay; or \[2\] tending to cause dilation\) is now occasionally misused for *deleterious* \(= harmful\)—e.g.:

• "Anti-takeover measures often have a *dilatory* \[read *deleterious*\] effect on a company's stock." Heather Harreld, "Stock Dips, Takeover Guard on Deck," *Triangle Bus. J.*, 21 Apr. 1995, at 1.

• "Pregnant substance-abusing women and their children have been vulnerable, not only because of the potential *dilatory* \[read *deleterious*\] effects of the drugs on them . . . but especially because of the blameful public attitude toward these women." David Lewis, "Pregnant Substance Abusers Need Our Help," *Brown Univ. Dig. of Addiction Theory and Application*, 1 Jan. 1998, at 12.

The pronunciation is /**dil**-ә-tor-ee/.
