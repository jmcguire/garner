# don't think

don't think. This phrasing has sometimes been criticized as illogical. After all, you *do* think: you simply think something negative. But the phrasing is perfectly idiomatic and centuries old—e.g.:

 - "I'd always wondered where I'd be when we found the aliens, but I *don't think* I ever imagined I'd see them on my computer screen." Wayne Rash Jr., "Rash's Judgment," *Communications Week*, 12 Aug. 1996, at 82.

 - "I *don't think* he's spent much time in New York or San Francisco, but I got his drift." Christopher Buckley, "How I Went Nine Gs in an F-16 and Only Threw Up Five Times," *Forbes*, 23 Sept. 1996, at 116.

 - "There are a number of countries that are friends and allies of the United States. With our power and friends, I *don't think* anyone would want to chance our retaliation." Sid Gordon, "Try to Keep the Peace Throughout the World," *Times Union* \(Albany\), 23 Aug. 2015, at D3.

For more on this phenomenon, which linguists call "raising," see **can't seem**.
