# delineate

delineate \(= \[1\] to sketch out or describe with some degree of specificity or vividness; or \[2\] to establish or clearly indicate \[a boundary, border, line, etc.\] in a way that separates one thing from another\), a Latin loanword dating from the 1500s, has several well-defined uses <the textiles are beautifully delineated> <a biography delineating Picasso's innovations> <elaborate moldings delineate the windows>. Since the 1950s, a new use has emerged out of sense 2, essentially making the word a loose synonym of *differentiate*. It manifests itself in the phrasing \**delineate between*. Another way of expressing the idea is that *delineate* is most traditionally a transitive verb, not an intransitive one. You delineate some kind of thing but not *between* two things. You might even delineate a line between two things. But avoid \**delineate between*—e.g.:

 - "But considering the individuals involved, it's difficult to *delineate* \[read *differentiate*\] between fact and fiction." Dave Luecking, "Bowman, Keenan 2-Man Head of Bureau of Minds," *St. Louis Post-Dispatch*, 5 May 1996, at F1.

 - "While it's not always easy to *delineate* \[read *differentiate*\] between legitimate communication to constituents and distribution of political material, taxpayers certainly have a right to be suspicious when material is distributed shortly before or during a political campaign season." "Identify Material Publicly Funded," *State-Times/Morning Advocate* \(Baton Rouge\), 24 Aug. 1996, at B6.

 - "In Burlington's testimony on Monday, he said he only brought up that comment to *delineate* \[read *differentiate*\] between the N-word being targeted at someone and the word being discussed in a ‘journalistic context.' " Dan Spinelli, "N-Word Lawsuit Intensifies as Colleague Testifies," *Phil. Daily News*, 10 June 2015, Local §, at 3.
