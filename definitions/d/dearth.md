# dearth

dearth = a deficient supply; a scarcity <there was a dearth of Americans at the festival>. E.g.: "A *dearth* of watchdog reporting has dire and quite specific results." Margaret Sullivan, *Ghosting the News* 22 (2020).

The word is commonly misunderstood as meaning a complete absence of something. This misunderstanding is revealed especially in the nonsensical phrase *\*complete dearth*, which has lurked in the linguistic shadows since the early 1800s. E.g.:

 - "Approximate number of calls logged by the ‘O.J. Hotline' before it was disconnected due to *a complete dearth* \[read *the lack* or *the absence*\] of concrete leads pointing to other suspects: 250,000." Richard Roeper, "The O.J. Trial . . . Again: This Time by Numbers," *Chicago Sun-Times*, 4 June 1995, Show §, at 2.

 - "The town's lack of a tourist sensibility can be deduced from its *complete dearth* \[read *its lack* or *its absence*\] of preening eateries." Tom Lappin, "Break for the Border," *Sunday Times* (London), 6 Feb. 2005, Features §, at 9.

 - "In many parts of the country, there *was a complete dearth of* \[read *were no*\] sales and little construction activity during the downturn." Cynthia M. Fraser, "True Value?," *Nat'l Real Estate Investor*, 1 Nov. 2014, at 44.

## Language-Change Index

 - *\*complete dearth* for *absence* or *lack*: Stage 1
