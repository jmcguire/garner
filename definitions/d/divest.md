# divest

divest = (1) to rid (oneself) of (something) by selling or donating <he divested himself of those holdings>; (2) to take away from or dispossess; to strip <the new leadership divested the former CEO of all power>; or (3) to sell (assets, investments, etc.) esp. for legal or political reasons <the company divested all foreign subsidiaries that had been using child labor>. In senses 1 and 2, *divest* invariably takes the preposition *of*.

Occasionally, through word-swapping, *divest* appears where the verb *withdraw* would be the right word—e.g.: "There's no law that would require either Trump or Clinton to *divest themselves* \[read *withdraw*\] from their organizations." Gregory Korte, "Clinton in Murky Territory on Charity," *USA Today*, 26–28 Aug. 2016, at 1A, 2A. One telltale clue for this misusage is *divest . . . from*.
