# dialectal

dialectal; **dialectical**; **dialectic**. These words are frequently confused. The adjective for *dialect* \(= a regional variety of language\) is *dialectal* /dī-ә-**lek**-tәl/. The adjective for *dialectics* \(= the art of argument\) is *dialectical* /dī-ә-**lek**-ti-kәl/. Broadly speaking, *dialectical* means "of, relating to, or involving logical argument, historical development, or the resolution of contradictory ideas." This term is usually confined to philosophical contexts.

But *dialectical* is often misused for *dialectal*—e.g.:

 - "Among the dozens of Russian-English dictionaries in her library, none specifically addressed idiom, that expressive, *dialectical* \[read *dialectal*\] language unique to a community or class of people." Paul Grondahl, "No Rushin' the Russian," *Times Union* \(Albany\), 5 June 1995, at C1.

 - " ‘Waiting on' is a *dialectical* \[read *dialectal*\] locution on the rise and splitting its meaning." William Safire, "On Language," *N.Y. Times*, 12 Nov. 1995, § 6, at 18.

 - " ‘Cretin' is normally thought to come from the Swiss French *dialectical* \[read *dialectal*\] term *crestin*, usually thought to be the local word for Christian." Letter of Donald F. McCabe, " ‘Cretin' from ‘Christian,' " *Wash. Post*, 9 May 1996, at A22.

*Dialectic*—a noun denoting the art of rigorously logical argumentation—is, when used as an adjective, a needless variant of *dialectical*.

Language-Change Index

*dialectical* misused for *dialectal*: Stage 1
