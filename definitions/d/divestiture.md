# divestiture

divestiture; **divesture**; **divestment**. The standard noun corresponding to the verb *divest* is *divestiture*. E.g.:

• "*Divestiture* of owned production facilities is common in electronics business here—Apple, Hewlett-Packard, IBM and National Semiconductor recently sold facilities with contracts to become their major customers." "Philips Plans Sale of U.S. TV Plant," *Consumer Electronics*, 2 Sept. 1996, at 9.

• "Completing *divestitures* already under way and those announced Friday will all but conclude Thomson's reorganizing process." "Takeover Target Loewen to Buy Cemetery Company," *Orlando Sentinel*, 21 Sept. 1996, at C10.

• "The forecast improvement over last year is primarily due to *divestiture* of the crop insurance business and growth in the average credit portfolio." Jennifer DeWitt, "Deere by Division," *Quad-City Times* \(Davenport, Iowa\), 22 Aug. 2015, at 8.

*Divesture*, when sharing the sense of *divestiture*, is a needless variant—e.g.: "Without the *divesture* \[read *divestiture*\], Otten would have controlled eight of the largest ski resorts serving skiers in eastern New England." Penny Parker, "Gillett Returns to Skiing," *Denver Post*, 11 Sept. 1996, at D1. The *OED* records *divesture* only in the sense of disrobing or unclothing.

*Divestment*, not at all uncommon, might seem to be another needless variant of *divestiture*. Yet it appears in a number of legal phrases, such as *vested interest subject to divestment*. If there is a nascent distinction, it seems to be that a *divestment* is involuntary \(a taking away\), while *divestitures* are often voluntary sales. Cf. **disinvestment**.
