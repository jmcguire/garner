# disenfranchise

disenfranchise; **disfranchise**. Though *disfranchise* was long favored—from the 1700s to the mid-1900s—*disenfranchise* is now far more common in print. It's the standard term meaning "to deprive of the right to exercise a franchise or privilege, esp. to vote." E.g.: "Part of this holding back was the result of white men, the persons in power, being unwilling or unable to reach out and bring in persons who traditionally had been *disenfranchised*." Jai Ghorpade, "California Civil Rights Initiative," *San Diego Union-Trib.*, 22 Sept. 1996, at G3.

## Language-Change Index

 - *disenfranchise* for *disfranchise*: Stage 5
 - Current ratio in print (*disenfranchised* vs. *disfranchised*): 7:1
