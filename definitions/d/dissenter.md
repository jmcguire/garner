# dissenter

dissenter; **dissentient**, n. Both nouns denote someone who dissents. *Dissenter* is standard for "someone who withholds assent or does not approve or agree." E.g.: "Reports suggest that one of the three *dissenters* \[on the jury\] was ‘scared to death' of precisely the kind of retaliation that put Nee in his grave." Peter Gelzinis, "Townies Know What Judge's Bias Refuses to Let Her See," *Boston Herald*, 8 Sept. 1996, at 10.

The formal variant *dissentient* occurs more often in BrE than AmE. E.g.: "The views of the 450 primary *dissentients* \[are\] . . . quite unrepresented." "Critical Day in the Coal Crisis," *Daily Telegraph*, 24 Sept. 2020, at 26. The reason for its prevalence in BrE may well be that the term *dissenter* (usually with an initial capital) has a special religious and social meaning in British history. In this specialized sense, *Dissenter* denotes someone who dissents from or refuses to conform—specifically, from the 1600s on—to the tenets and practices of the Church of England.
