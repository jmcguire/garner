# -ese

-ese. In English, this suffix \(deriving from Latin *ēnsem* "originating in \[a place\]"\) is one way of forming derivatives from the names of countries and major cities, as in *Chinese*, *Japanese*, *Lebanese*, *Milanese*, *Nepalese*, *Portuguese*, *Siamese*, *Veronese*, and *Viennese*. These derivatives may function either as adjectives <Lebanese food> or as nouns denoting either languages or people <speaking Portuguese> <the Portuguese sailed to Japan>. As demonyms, they formerly made regular plurals \(adding *s*\), but the language has long settled on using them as adjectives absolutely so that they function collectively <the Chinese haven't taken a position>. The *OED* remarks that in illiterate speech, these terms are mistakenly perceived as plurals, resulting in back-formations such as *Chinee*, *Japanee*, and *Portuguee*. \(See dialect.) But it's not just "illiterate" speech as we normally think of it; these back-formed singulars appear also in childish (preliterate) speech. Consider Althea Randolph Bedle Rusch's poem "My Jolly Little Japanee\!" \(1914\)—composed in a child's voice: "My mother gave a doll to me, / A jolly little Japanee\! \[etc.\]"

The most usual modern application of the suffix is to designate a certain type of language. At first, this extended use occurred with the habitual patterns of specific writers <Carlylese> <Johnsonese>, but by the late 1800s it was being used for specialties and genres <golfese> <legalese>. In these applications, the suffix typically denotes a jargonistic perversion of language, as in *cablese* \(= the cryptic jargon of cablegrams\), *educationese* \(= the obtusely complicated jargon of educationists\), *novelese* \(= the hackneyed style of inferior novels\), *sociologese* \(= the verbose jargon of sociologists\), etc. See airlinese, bureaucratese, commercialese, computerese, headlinese, legalese & officialese.
