# emulate

emulate \(= \[1\] to strive to equal or rival in quality or accomplishment; or \[2\] to copy or imitate with the goal of equaling or surpassing\), a Latin loanword from the late 1500s, acquired a computing sense in the 1960s ([3] to behave like another computer or software program by means of design modifications, additional programming, etc.). What all three senses have in common is that like emulates like: one person emulates another, or a computer program emulates a different computer program. Metaphorically, as in Shakespeare, an eye can emulate a diamond. (*Merry Wives of Windsor* 3.3.50.\) The person or thing being emulated is treated as being on the same level as the person or thing that is emulating.

This nuance is destroyed when a person is said to emulate not someone else but someone else's speech patterns or hair style or even toothbrush. In traditional idiom, a person doesn't emulate a habit or an implement. The better word for all such uses is *adopt, espouse, take on,* or *use*—e.g.:

 - "For his second directing job . . . , Avary *emulates* \[read *adopts*\] a novelist's style." Bob Ross, "Love Is in the Air," *Tampa Trib.*, 11 Oct. 2002, Friday Extra §, at 25.

 - "Success breeds imitation, and more authoritarian regimes \(plus some backsliding democracies\) across Eurasia, Latin America, the Middle East, and Africa are beginning to *emulate* \[read *adopt*\] these \[antidemocratic\] practices." Alexander Cooley, "The Rise of Global Counternorms," in *Regardless of Frontiers* 310 \(Lee C. Bollinger & Agnès Callamard eds., 2021\).
