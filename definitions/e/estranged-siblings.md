# Estranged Siblings

Estranged Siblings. The English language has several sets of cognate noun–adjective pairs that have diverged radically in meaning. Words in this category don't simply fall under the rubric of differentiation, which involves two or three terms in the same part of speech. The estranged-sibling category typically involves a corresponding noun–adjective pair that, despite their etymological relationship in Modern English, have gone separate semantic ways. Their predominant meanings are thought of as dissimilar:

 - *atrocity* (= a horrific crime) vs. *atrocious* (= really bad, esp. as a matter of taste) \<an atrocious pun\>.

 - *awe* (= a feeling of great respect) vs. *awful* (= very bad or unpleasant).

 - *enormity* (= monstrousness) vs. *enormous* (= immense, huge).

 - *fantasy* (= an imaginary idea or belief) vs. *fantastic* (= extremely good). See **fantastic**.

 - *gourmandise* (= \[traditionally\] connoisseurship in eating) vs. *gourmand* (= a glutton).

 - *phlegm* (= mucus, catarrh) vs. *phlegmatic* (= \[of a person\] slow-moving, stolid, not excitable). See **phlegmatic**.

 - *terror* (= extreme fear) vs. *terrific* (= very good).

Some pairs that might seem to fit the category don't quite qualify. *Noise* (= loud sound) and *noisome* (= extremely unpleasant, as with a smell) came into English as distinct words with separate meanings. Only folk etymology has made them seem closely related. (See etymology (d).) *River* (= a natural inland waterway) and *rival* (= a competitor \[originally someone living on the opposite bank of a stream\]) are etymologically related, but the pair is noun–noun—and there are many more instances of this kind of divergence. The essence of the estranged-sibling category is that a noun and its corresponding adjective, in modern usage, have little semantic overlap.

Among the pairs listed above, the only one that continually causes problems is *enormity*/*enormous*. The two words seem to want to end their estrangement, so that *enormity* might answer to *enormous* instead of being confined to heinous crimes and other iniquities. With this particular pairing, English speakers are reluctant to use *enormousness* in all references to mere size. Increasingly, people use *enormity* in this nonwicked sense. Traditionalists want the estrangement to continue; others want the old feuding to stop. In the meantime, *enormity* occupies a kind of linguistic limbo because many perceive it as an irretrievably derogatory term. For a full discussion, see **enormity**.
