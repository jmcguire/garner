# eurhythmics

eurhythmics; **eurythmics**; **eurythmy**; **eurhythmy**. *Eurythmics* \(= the practice of moving the body to rhythms, esp. those of music, as a method of teaching or interpretation\), a term dating from the early 20th century, is traditionally so spelled. It takes a singular verb <eurythmics is helpful in reinforcing language acquisition>. Although the standard spelling of the word \(ironically\) has no internal *rhythm*—because the Greek term on which it is based is *eurythmos*—there is a variant spelling *eurhythmics* \(a spelling that surged to predominance in the late 1980s\). E.g.: "The workshop she attended Thursday focused on *eurhythmics*—using body movements to interpret music." Kendra Martinez, "Music Teachers Use Bodies at BSU Workshop," *Idaho Statesman*, 25 July 1997, at B2. \(Cf. **rhythmic**.\) The rock band popular in the 1980s spelled their name the old way: *Eurythmics*.

Given the inevitable attraction of folk etymology \(see etymology \(d\)) and people's disinclination to learn irregular spellings (see spelling \(a\)), the two-*h* version seems likely to remain predominant. Today it is three times as common as the one-*h* version.

*Eurythmy* \(developed by Rudolf Steiner and Marie von Silvers, also in the 1920s\) is primarily about movement as therapy, and may or may not involve music. It is usually spelled \(as the *OED* notes\) in the irregular way—with one *h*—even though it is all about *rhythm*. Cf. **rhythmic**.

Confusingly enough, *eurhythmy* \(with two *h*s\) was once used both in architecture \(in reference to harmonious proportions of a building\) and in pathology \(in reference to regularity of the pulse\). This term qualifies at best as an archaism.

In sum, you might fairly conclude that these terms seem almost willfully calculated to induce, in lexicographers, heart arrhythmia.

Current ratio in print \(*eurhythmics* vs. *eurythmics*\): 3:1
