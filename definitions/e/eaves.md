# eaves

eaves. This noun, meaning "the overhanging lower edge of a roof," is—despite its appearance—historically singular. But since the 1800s, *eaves* has been misinterpreted as a plural form, and so the misbegotten singular *eave* was born (as a back-formation). Today it can hardly be criticized as nonstandard—e.g.:

 - "Paper wasps . . . build those familiar, often large papery nests either suspended from a branch or *eave*, or fitted inside an abandoned animal burrow." Allen M. Young, "In Praise of Wasps," *Chicago Trib.* (Mag.), 25 Aug. 1996, at 32.

 - "Snow hangs ominously over the *eave* of her home in Lake View as Diana Szczutkowski clears a path." "Weather Woes Deepen by a Foot or More," *Buffalo News*, 13 Jan. 1997, at A1 (photo caption).

 - "A row of rooms and a small office with a crisp US flag fluttering from an *eave* faced a narrow, too-steep beach." John Connor, "Hidalgo of Cuba Then, Part I," *Guns Mag.*, Sept. 2015, at 78.
