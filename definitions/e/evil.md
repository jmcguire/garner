# evil

evil, n. & adj., which appears frequently in the Bible and in theology, is out of fashion perhaps because of the supernatural associations it supposedly carries. It is often taken for "afflictions and punishments which God inflicts upon a person or people." John Hosking, *The Elements of Christian Theology, Philosophy, Morals, and History* 73 \(1894\). But it can also denote "injuries or wrongs done by one man to another." *Ibid*. One theologian defines *evil* as "the privation of good." Marion Le Roy Burton, *The Problem of Evil* 33 \(1909\). The *OED* defines it as "the antithesis of good . . . in all its principal senses."

Some writers have objected to the idea that the word is supernaturally loaded. The famous atheist Christopher Hitchens bristled at the idea that using the word is "naïve or morally too judgmental or simplistic." In a 2014 speech before the Commonwealth Club of California, he argued that the word is one "without which we cannot do." Using it to denote extreme hatred and cruelty, he defined it as "the surplus value that is generated by totalitarianism . . . \[given that\] you do more violence and cruelty than you absolutely have to to stay in power. . . . There must be special prisons just for rape \[i.e., to inflict it\], and there must be special mass graves just for children. . . . It's not a word that we can abolish from our vocabulary." As you can see, Hitchens wanted to reserve the word for true atrocities. He may well have been right—which means that people trivialize the word by calling trifling wrongs evil.
