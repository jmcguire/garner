# eyrie

eyrie \(= \[1\] the nest of a predatory bird, esp. one built on a crag or precipice; \[2\] the brood within such a nest; or \[3\] by extension, a human habitation, whether hovel or fortress, in a high place\) has long been the standard spelling in all varieties of English. In the 1700s, the spelling was *\*aery*, or sometimes *\*eyry*. In the 1800s and 1900s, *aerie* became fairly common, especially in AmE. Although *eyrie* has predominated almost universally since about 1850 in English-language print sources, American lexicographers have tended to list the main headword at *aerie*. But so does the OED, which notes that "the spelling *eyrie* seems to have been introduced by \[Henry\] Spelman" in a glossary of 1664 "to support his notion of its derivation from egg." Yet because no etymology for this word is certain—*W11* suggests a relation to the Vulgar Latin *agrum* "nest"—lexicographers have avoided the *eyrie* spelling. As the *OED* remarks under E, this is "now the commonest spelling." Regardless of the spelling, the word is pronounced /**er**-ee/, /**ay**-ree/, or /**eer**-ee/.

Current ratio in print \(*eyrie* vs. *aerie* vs. *\*aery* vs. *\*eyry*\): 17:9:3:1
