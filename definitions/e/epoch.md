# epoch

epoch /**ep**-әk/ or, in BrE, /**ee**-pok/ = (1) the date of an occurrence that starts things going under new conditions; or (2) a period of history. Some stylists object to sense 2 as an example of slipshod extension—an unnecessary word, since *era* fills that role nicely. But the extension occurred in the 1600s, and good writers today use the word in that sense—e.g.: "Scientists at the Smithsonian Institution . . . have challenged . . . the notion that our early ancestors were prodded into existence in response to abrupt environmental changes during the Pliocene *epoch*." Curt Suplee, "Team Challenges Theory Linking Climate Change, Evolutionary Surge," *Wash. Post*, 28 Nov. 1997, at A4.

In the taxonomy of geological time, an epoch is a division of a period, which in turn is a division of an era. For example, today we are in the Holocene Epoch of the Quaternary Period of the Cenozoic Era. Tomorrow—who knows?
