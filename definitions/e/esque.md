# -esque, Essay

-esque. This suffix—meaning "like, resembling"—almost always creates a solid word, as in *romanesque*, *Rubenesque*, *statuesque*. E.g.: "One could almost see the *Clintonesque* curling and biting of the lip for dramatic effect." "New Democrats and New Laborites," *Omaha World-Herald*, 20 Nov. 1997, at 28. Of course, given the suffix's meaning, it's wrong to add *-like* to the end of such a word—e.g.: "A man painted in white stands on a pedestal striking various *statuesque-like* \[read *statue-like* or *statuesque*\] poses." Alan Byrd, "Will the Real Key West Please Stand Up?," *Orlando Bus. J.*, 23 May 1997, at 1.
