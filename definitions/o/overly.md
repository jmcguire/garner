# overly

overly, adv. Many readers will be surprised to learn that there was ever a controversy about this word, which has now reached Stage 5 on the Language-Change Index: it should be considered unobjectionable.

The 19th- and 20th-century criticisms were that it is a "double adverb"—adding *ly* to *over*, which already functions as an adverb <move over>. \(See adverbs \(d\).) *Over* is itself a combining form when an adjective follows, as with *overambitious*, *overcautious*, *overoptimistic*, *overreliant*, etc. But for many people, this prefixing doesn't feel entirely right with many adjectives, such as *aggressive*, *concerned*, *dependent*, *large*, and *sensitive*. Although prefixing is possible with each of those adjectives, the predominant forms, with dates of predominance shown, are *overly aggressive* \(1957\), *overly concerned* \(1940\), *overly dependent* \(1971\), *overly large* \(1980\), and *overly sensitive* \(1989\). Not all writers and editors are fully comfortable creating a multisyllabic compound with *over*.

The primary objection to *overly*, apart from the double-adverb issue, is that the word sounded newfangled when it sprang to popularity beginning in the 1900s. \(In fact, it had been used in Old English 900 years before, and its modern use amounts to a "revival."\) A few 19th- and many 20th-century critics objected to it—e.g.:

 - **1881:** "**Overly.** This word is now used only by the unschooled." Alfred Ayres, *The Verbalist* 136 \(1881\).

 - **1927:** "**overly**, adv., colloquial in and dialectal for *very*, *extremely*, as in *I'm not overly fond of that fellow*." George Philip Krapp, *A Comprehensive Guide to Good English* 429 \(1927\).

 - **1943:** "**overly** . . . Don't use it for *over* in such expressions as *The roast is overdone* and *The case has been overstated*. You don't have to use it at all. It is a provincial or dialect word, and the adverbial field is already crowded without it." John B. Opdycke, *Don't Say It* 575 \(1943\).

 - **1966:** "It is hard to think of the reason why this adverb does not show itself up as superfluous and prove its right to be thrown into the discard. . . . If anyone argues that there is a shade of difference between *overly timid* and *overtimid*, he must be left to his own subtlety, for it is incommunicable." Wilson Follett, *MAU* at 243–44.

 - **1980:** "*Overly* . . . does nothing more than add an extra syllable to turn something into what it has been all along. Why, then, say *overly complicated*, *overly qualified*, or *overly fond*, et cetera, when *overcomplicated*, *overqualified*, *overfond*, and so on are shorter, neater, more euphonious? The argument that we need *overly* for the negative statement *not overly* is otiose; we have *overmuch*, *excessively*, *very*, and several others to choose from." John Simon, *Paradigms Lost* 3–4 \(1980\).

 - **1983:** "**overly**, excessively, too, is still regarded as an unassimilated Americanism, e.g. *Those overly rationalistic readers* \(TLS\). • Use *excessively*, *too*, or *over* instead; for *not overly*, *not very* and *none too* make satisfactory replacements." E.S.C. Weiner, *The Oxford Guide to English Usage* 121 \(1983\).

By 1996, Robert W. Burchfield noted that *overly*, though originally an Americanism in its revived currency, had become "widely used in BrE." \(*FMEU3* at 564.\) That same year, another eminent British writer, Sidney Greenbaum, was perhaps being a little too historical \(not to say overly historical\) when he wrote: "The adverb *overly* is an alternative to the prefix *over* in its ‘excessively' sense, particularly in American and Scottish usage. It premodifies adjectives: *overly eager*, *overly scrupulous.*" Greenbaum, *The Oxford English Grammar* § 9.11, at 449 \(1996\). It had already spread well beyond the U.S. and Scotland.

The objections to the word have always been insubstantial. For one reason or another, English speakers haven't warmed to either compounding words with *over* or to systematically replacing *overly* with *excessively*, *too*, *unduly*, etc. The once-criticized word appears with great frequency in well-edited English—e.g.:

 - "The UN inspectors were not *overly* skeptical when they started their work of scrutinizing Iraq's arsenal of weapons of mass destruction." "Saddam's UN Nemesis," *Boston Globe*, 8 June 1997, at D6.

 - "The harsher attacks create some risk for McCain, analysts say, because he may come off as *overly* negative." Jill Zuckman, "McCain Ad Fights Rival's Celebrity Head-On," *Chicago Trib.*, 31 July 2008, News §, at 1.

 - "The couple said they weren't *overly* concerned about the interest rate rise." Brittney Levinson, "Rate Rise Comes as No Surprise to Canberra Buyers," *Canberra Times*, 4 May 2022, at 5.

In the previous edition of this book, *overly* was pegged at Stage 4 on the Language-Change Index. On the basis of empirical evidence, it has now been upgraded to full acceptability. Some writers and editors, however, may be inclined in a given context to use a prefixed *over-* for reasons of euphony.
