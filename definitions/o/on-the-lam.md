# on the lam

on the lam \(= on the run from law-enforcement authorities\) began as American slang. Although the expression has been traced back to the late 1800s, its precise origin remains obscure. The phrase didn't become common till about 1930. It is fairly widespread today—e.g.:

 - "A former Bank of the Desert loan officer, who has been *on the lam* since May, was arrested in Las Vegas this week on federal bank fraud and money-laundering charges." Don McAuliffe, "Former Desert Bank Executive Arrested," *Press-Enterprise* \(Riverside, Cal.\), 28 Aug. 1999, at D3.

 - "Grauman was *on the lam* after escaping from both the South Dakota prison and a jail in Wyoming." "Escapee Charged with Murder," *Rocky Mountain News* \(Denver\), 15 June 2000, at A33.

 - "He fled after the conviction, living *on the lam* for six months before being arrested in Las Vegas and sentenced to 30 years in prison." James C. McKinley Jr., "Jamiel A. Chagra, 63, Drug King, Dies," *N.Y. Times*, 29 July 2008, at B6.

Occasionally the phrase is mistakenly made *\*on the lamb*—e.g.: "The owner of a local clothing company was arrested Tuesday and an employee of the company is *on the lamb* \[read *on the lam*\], in the wake of a raid by the state Department of Industrial Relations." Yomi S. Wronge, "Agents Stage Raid on an Unlicensed Garment Producer," *Orange County Register*, 16 Oct. 1997, at 2.

## Language-Change Index

*\*on the lamb* for *on the lam*: Stage 2
