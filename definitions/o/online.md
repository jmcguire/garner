# online

online; **on-line**. Although *on-line* (with the hyphen) remains common for references to being connected to the internet, the hyphen is probably doomed to disappear. The closed form is already predominant, whether the word is used as an adjective or an adverb—e.g.:

 - Adjective: "Besides, all these hip Web savants were smugly pronouncing that *online* journalism was the future." Rem Reider, "At the Crossroads," *Am. Journalism Rev.*, Mar. 2001, at 6.

 - Adverb: "Go *online*. Hundreds of restaurants are listed at www.opentable.com." Erica Marcus, "Advice for Making This a Valentine's Day Dinner to Remember," *Newsday* (N.Y.), 12 Feb. 2003, at B16.

In jargon, the phrase *on line* (unhyphenated) has been extended to mean "into service" <the new power plant will be coming on line in June>, and the corresponding adjective is solid <an online power plant>.

 - Current ratio in print (*online* vs. *on-line*): 47:1
