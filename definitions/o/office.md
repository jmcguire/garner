# office

office, vb., has become a commonplace expression in the American business world, but not among fastidious users of language. Although *office* as a verb is recorded from the 1500s, the new vogue began in the Southwest during the 1980s within the oil-and-gas industry. Gradually it has spread to other fields and started to overrun the country. No one seems to *have an office* anymore; instead, everyone *offices*. E.g.:

• "*Officing* in Irvine, Calif., Carlisle will be responsible for business development in the eleven western states." *Oil & Gas J.*, 25 July 1983, at 172.

• "The Uptown area is most attractive to prospective tenants wanting the lure of downtown without the high costs associated with *officing* there." Anne Belli, "Uptown Looking Up," *Dallas Bus. J.*, 2 May 1988, at A18.

• "A few months ago, Tali Blumrosen and Chris Pierini formed a new PR firm, B&P, *officing* out of their homes." Maxine Mesinger, "Life's a Party for Birthday Trio," *Houston Chron.*, 25 Sept. 1996, Houston §, at 1.

• "*Officed* at the corporate location today, she began her career across the street in bookkeeping using a crank adding machine." Rosemary Horvath, "Business Milestone," *Sentinel-Standard* \(Ionia, Mich.\), 29 Nov. 2014, Mich. News §, at 1.

This is a classic example of the issue discussed under functional shift \(a\), \(d\).
