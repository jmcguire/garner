# opera buffa

opera buffa /**boof**-ә/; **opera bouffe** /boof/. The first is the Italian phrase for "a farcical comic opera," first used in English in the mid-1700s; the second is its French equivalent, first used in English in the mid-1800s. Essentially, a farcical opera of Italian origin will be termed *opera buffa*; one of French origin will be termed *opera bouffe*.

As a matter of linguistic development, *opera buffa* was the sole term in English-language print sources from the 1770s to the 1860s, and it remained predominant until about 1890; *opera bouffe* predominated from then until the late 1930s; and then, beginning about 1937, *opera buffa* has steadily remained more than twice as common as *opera bouffe*.

Musicologists often distinguish the two based on historical development, noting (for example) that *opera buffa* most often has recitative dialogue. And some commentators confine *opera bouffe* to the works of Jacques Offenbach (1819–1880).

*Opera comique* is a different category still—one with a happy ending and usually with some dialogue spoken, not sung. The term is contrasted with *grand opera*, which denotes an opera with a serious and sometimes tragic theme—and in which all dialogue is sung.

Although some dictionaries record these terms with an acute accent (*opéra bouffe*, *opéra comique*), they appear far more often without one. See diacritical marks.
