# overflow \> overflowed \> overflowed

overflow \> overflowed \> overflowed. So inflected. Some writers mistakenly use *overflown* as the past participle of *overflow*—e.g.:

 - "But water has *overflown* \[read *overflowed*\] the levee at least four times since it was built." Missy Baxter, "Levee Project Halted After Flood Proves New Wall Would Have Failed," *Courier-J.* (Louisville), 14 Apr. 1997, at B2.

 - "Though waste was found in a lagoon at the farm, some waste had *overflown* \[read *overflowed*\] at a lift station designed to pump it from four hog houses to the lagoon." Hannah Mitchell, "Hog Waste Feared Leaking into River," *Morning Star* (Wilmington, N.C.), 10 July 1997, at B1.

See irregular verbs (d). For a short, interesting study of this term, see Ann Eljenholm Nichols, "The Past Participle of ‘Overflow': ‘Overflowed' or ‘Overflown,' " 41 *Am. Speech* 52 (Feb. 1966).

## Language-Change Index

 - *overflown* misused for *overflowed* as past participle of *overflow*: Stage 1
 - Current ratio in print (*overflowed its banks* vs. \**overflown its banks*): 55:1
