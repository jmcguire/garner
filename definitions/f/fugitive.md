# fugitive

fugitive, adj., is a formal word in the sense "elusive, fleeting." E.g.:

 - "The latter being dependent on the vagaries of light, it is *fugitive*, even evanescent." "The Picture of Excellent Photography," *Portland Press Herald*, 8 Oct. 1995, at E1.

 - "Such books would now be priceless reminders of dancers lost and dances forgotten, monumental little mementos of the fleeting times of a *fugitive* art." Clive Barnes, "Jack Mitchell: Photographer to the Dance," *Dance Mag.*, Jan. 1996, at 64.

 - "She moved every year of her childhood from school to school and her character itself is *fugitive*, preliminary." Sam Leith, "Review: The Rise and Fall of Great Powers," *Guardian*, 7 June 2014, at 10.

An even more learned equivalent is *fugacious*, which is a literary word as well as a botanical term describing plants that wither or drop their leaves early. On the use of this type of word, see sesquipedality.
