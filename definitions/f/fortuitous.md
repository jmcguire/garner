# fortuitous

fortuitous.

**A. And *fortunate*.** If words seem to have personalities, they also have fortunes. They may appreciate in meaning \(gaining better senses\) or depreciate in meaning \(gaining worse senses\). Their boundaries may be carefully observed or carelessly neglected. Although *fortuitous* has appreciated in meaning, its traditional boundaries have been muddled. You might debate whether its fate has been fortunate or unfortunate.

Strictly speaking, *fortuitous* means "occurring by chance; accidental or casual." This traditional sense, dating back to the adjective's first use in the 1600s, remains fairly strong—e.g.:

• "Whether the victim lives or dies might be entirely *fortuitous*." Jerome H. Skolnick, "A Capital Offense Spared by Luck?," *L.A. Times*, 27 Aug. 1993, at B7.

• "He began his career with a *fortuitous* meeting and handshake with his brother, William C. Liedtke Jr., whom he encountered by chance on the South Pacific island of Saipan during the war." "J. Hugh Liedtke" \(obit.\), *Tulsa World*, 1 Apr. 2003, at A12.

• "Restricted to such *fortuitous* discoveries, paleontologists learned very little about the brains of extinct species such as dinosaurs for a long time." Daniel T. Ksepka, Bird Brain Evolution," *Am. Scientist*, 1 Nov. 2021, at 352.

In the first sentence above, the chance involved is possibly unpleasant \(dying\); in the second, it is pleasant \(a happy reunion\); in the third, the discoveries are unpleasantly random but fortunate when they occur. But the significant feature in each example is the presence of chance or coincidence. See **aleatory**.

Through word-swapping, the word has been widely misused for *fortunate* \(= favored by fortune\) or *lucky* \(= having or resulting from good luck\). Those words also involve chance, but always of an unexpectedly positive kind. The gradual progression, or degradation, of *fortuitous* toward synonymy with *fortunate* or *lucky* is traceable to AmE of the 1920s. *Fortuitous* resembles *fortunate*, of course, in its first five letters. The word gradually became more closely associated with happy than with unhappy occurrences. Some have suggested that it also sounds like *felicitous*, so that it might be thought of as a portmanteau word combining *fortunate* and *felicitous*. Perhaps that's far-fetched, but the misuses aren't—e.g.:

• "My choice of Leeds University was quite *fortuitous* \[read *lucky* or *fortunate*\]. A few weeks before the university session was scheduled to begin, I was given a scholarship to study in England." Wole Soyinka, "Yoruban Astrophysics," *Wash. Post*, 9 Apr. 1995, at C3.

• "Perhaps the *most fortuitous* \[read *most fortunate* or *best*\] feature of the house is a full-length staircase between the second and third floors, which physically separates the living and work areas." Alvin Rosenbaum, "The Balancing Act," *Home Office Computing*, Dec. 1996, at 62.

• "The project was helped by *fortuitous* \[read *good*\] weather." Michael Gagne, "Airport Reopens After Runway Work," *Record-J.* \(Meriden, Conn.\), 23 Oct. 2021, at A1, A3.

As suggested by the bracketed replacements inserted in those examples, editors have tended to replace wordings of the kind just illustrated. After all, weather \(third example\) is always fortuitous; the award of a scholarship \(first example\) and the architectural plans \(second example\) both involve the human will. But the happy accidents in the first and second examples were unplanned or unexpected by the person being written about. Notice that we're still talking about *things*—usually occurrences of some kind—as being *fortuitous*.

A further effacement of the word is underway—involving hypallage. We're now beginning to see instances in which the adjective is applied to people, not events. So lucky people are said to be *fortuitous*—e.g.:

• "That Smoltz would be so *fortuitous* \[read *fortunate* or *lucky*\] is something of a good howl in itself, given all the cruel twists in his career." Tom Verducci, "Eye Opener," *Sports Illustrated*, 10 June 1996, at 46.

• "The Cardinals were *fortuitous* \[read *fortunate*\] winners against the Villagers." "Cleeve Boss: We're a Jekyll and Hyde Team," *Gloucestershire Echo* \(U.K.\), 17 Nov. 2010, at 39.

• "Baumritter still felt *fortuitous* \[read *lucky* or *fortunate*\] that his team was able to compete this season." Jared Keene, "Abrupt Ending to Memorable Season," *Beacon* \(Acton, Mass.\), 19 Nov. 2020, at B1.

Lest you suspect that only sportswriters use the word this way, be assured that the facts are otherwise—e.g.: "No, it wasn't a Jackie Chan stunt, just a very *fortuitous* \[read *fortunate*\] woman who accidentally fell 60 feet from a department store balcony . . . straight onto a bed that cushioned her fall." "Woman Falls 60 Feet in Department Store, Lands on Bed," *Catch It Kansas* \(Wichita\), 11 Feb. 2016, News §.

What has befallen this word? In 1987, the Oxford lexicographer Robert W. Burchfield received a letter that said: "How sad it will be to lose *fortuitous* to the Visigoths." \(*FMEU3* at 310.\) So widespread had the misuses become, Burchfield wrote in 1996, that "the Visigoths \[were\] at work among standard speakers." \(*Ibid.*\) What H.W. Fowler had seen in 1926 as a malapropism was threatening by the 1980s and 1990s to become standard english. Burchfield urged resistance: "When an intrusive meaning contains the seeds of ambiguity, it is advisable to stay with the older ones." \(*Ibid.* at 311.\)

How do things stand in the 2020s? The erosion of the verbal coastline, if we may change the metaphor, continues. But there has been no massive landslide. Careful writers and speakers will continue to observe the old but fragile differentiation between the terms, perhaps with a sense of nostalgia. Even if the objectionable use will someday be considered, by almost all reasonable people, to be entirely unobjectionable, that day probably won't come during this century. Reaching Stage 5 will take some time.

**B. \**Fortuitous accident* & \**fortuitous coincidence*.** In these phrases, the word *fortuitous* bears the right sense but amounts to a redundancy: every accident or coincidence is fortuitous. Writers using those phrases, though, almost invariably mean "fortunate" or "lucky"—e.g.:

• "Without that *fortuitous accident* \[read *lucky accident*\] of layout, even Conran's space might have stayed empty." Claudia H. Deutsch, "The Shops at Citicorp Center," *N.Y. Times*, 16 June 1996, § 9, at 10.

• "By a *fortuitous coincidence* \[read *fortunate coincidence*\] that allows a unique movement-by-movement comparison, Pittsburgh Symphony's incoming music director, Mariss Jansons, has recorded a Sibelius disc released simultaneously with Maazel's completion of his third Sibelius cycle." Robert Croan, "Two Perspectives on Sibelius," *Pitt. Post-Gaz.*, 1 Sept. 1996, at F9.

• "Without meaning to, I ordered several items twice. It was a *fortuitous accident* \[read *lucky accident*\]; each dish on the tiny menu . . . is worth revisiting." Hanna Goldfield, "Vegan Chinese, Both Innovative and Traditional," *New Yorker*, 22 Mar. 2021, at 9.

**C. Corresponding Adverb: *fortuitously*.** In the early 1980s, a *Time* magazine journalist wrote a piece about American aircraft that, having been ordered by the Shah of Iran, "*fortuitously* were never delivered." Both William Safire and Jacques Barzun called the journalist to task for misusing *fortuitously* in place of *fortunately*. See Safire, *I Stand Corrected* 205–06, 315 \(1984\). The journalist, George Church, then defended himself by saying that the failure of delivery was an instance of "sheer dumb luck." He posited that while *fortunately* would have emphasized the happiness of the outcome, *fortuitously* emphasized that it resulted from chance. *Ibid.* at 205. No dice, said Safire and Barzun.

In short, as could be amply shown with supernumerary examples, the adverb has the same potential pitfalls as the adjective. See \(a).

Perhaps the more interesting development—maybe it was inevitable—is that *fortuitously* has come to be used as a sentence adverb. \(See adverbs \(f\).) These uses are almost inevitably treading on the do-not-trespass domain of *fortunately*—e.g.:

• " 'Tis the season to be jolly . . . so it's a ho ho here and a pinot grigio there . . . . *Fortuitously* \[read *Luckily*\], West Cape Howe Wines has just the drop to ensure a very Merry Christmas indeed." Vanessa Jones, "Perfect Drop for a Merry Time," *Gold Coast Sun*, 10 Dec. 2015, at 16.

• "*Fortuitously* \[read *Fortunately*\] for locked-down fans, on March 5, 2020, the band also dropped Live at Joshua Tree, a 45-minute concert film shot in the desert with no audience." Annabel Ross, "Dance from the Heart," *Sydney Morning Herald* \(Aus.\), 16 Oct. 2021, at 10.

• " ‘Now people are cooking at home more,' Sewall told me a few months ago. *Fortuitously* \[read *Fortunately*\], then, he released \[a cookbook with recipes\] . . . simple enough for the home cook to pull off." Kara Baskin, "Sewall's ‘Row 34 Cookbook' Not Just Another Fish Story," *Boston Globe*, 20 Oct. 2021, Living Arts §.

You might argue, surely enough, that in these examples, the writer wanted to emphasize the chancy nature of the occurrence more than the propitiousness of the outcome. But this argument overlooks the very nature of fortune, which always involves chance. Literature is rife with references to the wheel of fortune \(now popularized as a game show\), which betokens the vicissitudes of outcomes. If *fortunately* suggests *bon chance*, why make *fortuitously* suggest the very same thing? We keep returning to that idea.

**D. Corresponding Noun: *fortuity*.** The noun *fortuity* is the relatively unusual noun corresponding to *fortuitous* \(usually in the strict sense\)—e.g.:

• "Voters tend to be more straightforward, rewarding presidents who, whether by *fortuity* or by design, happen to be in office when the public's pocketful swells." John Liscio, "History, and Hard Numbers, Favor Bill Clinton," *Commercial Appeal* \(Memphis\), 18 Aug. 1996, at C1.

• "Dreams can be like eggs and steel—dependent on the fragile *fortuity* of fate and achieved by unflinching determination, unbendable confidence in one's desire to compete and unyielding effort." Mike Tupa, *Bartlesville Examiner-Enterprise* \(Okla.\), 26 Aug. 2021, Sports §.

*Fortuitousness*, which emphasizes the quality as opposed to the state of being fortuitous, is even rarer.

Once again, though, we cheapen *fortuity* if we use it as a mere synonym of *good fortune*—e.g.:

• "Wingfield had the enviable opportunity to give away money. Even with that *fortuity* \[read *good fortune*\], there was something that set him apart." Loran Smith, "Terry Wingfield, a Man Blessed with a Good and Loyal Heart," *Athens Banner-Herald* \(Ga.\), 5 Sept. 2021, at C1.

• "I've had the *fortuity* \[read *good fortune*\] to plant my bum onto the superbly upholstered seat of a private aircraft once or twice and I'm here to tell you they're not all they're cracked up to be." Robert Crampton, "The Last Word," *Australian*, 18 Sept. 2021, at 27.

All in all, despite their more and more positive connotations, you might bemoan the fortunes of *fortuitous, fortuitously,* and *fortuity.* Their gain has been the language's loss.
