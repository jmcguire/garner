# fundament

fundament = \(1\) basis; or \(2\) anus or buttocks. Sense 2 is more common in BrE than in AmE—e.g.: "There is even a 12-step group for people addicted to 12-step groups—which is very Fight Club, but surely only a small step away from one's head disappearing entirely up one's own *fundament*." Mimi Spencer, "Let's Talk About Me," *Sunday Times* \(London\), 19 Jan. 2003, Features §, at 12. But because sense 2 is current in AmE as well, it typically can't be used without creating a double entendre. One hardly knows what to think about sentences such as this: "This is the best of the best, and like it or not, it's the *fundament* of our private culture and public life." Kyrie O'Connor, " ‘Great Books,' with a Classic Male Slant," *Hartford Courant*, 22 Sept. 1996, at G3. To avoid trouble, try *foundation*.

Sometimes the word is misused in the plural as an inelegant variation of *fundamentals*—e.g.: "\[Kyle\] Korver is like a golfer, always tinkering, always looking for the perfect swing. He'll watch other players, work on his *fundaments* \[read *fundamentals*\], tweak his own shot." Tom Shatel, "Korver Puts in the Hours to Become Jays' Big Shot," *Omaha World-Herald*, 18 Jan. 2003, at C1. \(He wouldn't get far by working on his fundament.\)
