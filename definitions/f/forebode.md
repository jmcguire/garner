# forebode

forebode \(= \[1\] to predict \[usu. something bad\]; foretell; or \[2\] to have a strong inward warning or intuition of \[some imminent misfortune or disaster\]\), a verb dating from the early 1600s, has always been predominantly so spelled. \**Forbode* is an etymologically inaccurate variant that occasionally appears—e.g.:

 - "If the personalities of the principals on both sides of the table don't mesh, it *forbodes* \[read *forebodes*\] what well could be a bad marriage between the firms." Ursula Miller, "Investment Banker Manages Mergers," *Cincinnati Enquirer*, 4 Mar. 1996, at D1. \(On the odd syntax of that sentence, in which the unidiomatic phrase *well could be* appears in place of the more natural *could well be*, see adverbs \(b\).)

 - "\[T\]he track is purportedly built atop an ancient Indian burial ground and the place seems to ooze a sense of morbid *forboding* \[read *foreboding*\]." Brant James, "In Junior's Words," *St. Petersburg Times* \(Fla.\), 28 Apr. 2007, Sports §, at C12.

See for-.
