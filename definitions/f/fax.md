# fax

fax, n. & vb. This term is now all but universal, in the face of which *facsimile transmission* is an instant archaism—and a pompous one at that. *Fax*, which is now perfectly appropriate even in formal contexts, first appeared in the mid-1970s—e.g.: "In the past two years, *fax* installations have more than doubled from fewer than 50,000 to more than 100,000 units." "The Office of the Future," *BusinessWeek*, 30 June 1975, at 48. The verb use dates from the mid-1980s—e.g.: "The prints are then *faxed* from the regional centers." "Data Detectives Use a New Technique to Match Fingerprints," *Data Communications*, Feb. 1984, at 56.

The noun plural is *faxes*.

Some writers mistakenly put the word in all capitals, as if it were an acronym. It isn't. It's just a clipped form with a slight change in spelling: write *fax*, not *\*FAX*.
