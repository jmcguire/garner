# forefathers

forefathers; **founding fathers**. Both may denote the founders of a country, but the two shouldn't be combined—e.g.: "What would Washington, Jefferson, Madison and the other *founding forefathers* \[read *founding fathers*\] say about us today?" Charlie Rea, "The Moral Implications of Tax Cutting," *Roanoke Times*, 30 Jan. 2001, at A11. *Forefathers* is an old-fashioned synonym for *ancestors*; some writers avoid it because of its sexist overtones. \(See sexism.) But *founding fathers* seems unobjectionable and historically accurate in reference to those who participated directly in establishing the U.S. Constitution and the American government. Meanwhile, the term *founders* \(often capitalized\) is common nowadays; it may denote the founders of anything, such as a town or a corporation.
