# facilitate

facilitate; **facilitator**. *Facilitate* is a formal word to be used sparingly because it is often jargonistic. So is its agent noun *facilitator*, a vogue term that often denotes a helper, teacher, seminar instructor, and the like—e.g.:

 - "Everett Clark, chairman of the Education 2000 Task Force, mentioned the meeting after a presentation to council members from the group's new ‘*facilitator*,' Andrea Tannenbaum. The council hired Tannenbaum for $10,000 through the end of the year to guide the task force." Lizabeth Hall, "Citizen Task Force, School Board Plan Talks in Bloomfield," *Hartford Courant*, 29 Oct. 1996, at B1.

 - "Poking fun at a system that calls teachers ‘*facilitators*' and uses the euphemism ‘extensions' for homework is just too easy." Mark Pino, "Celebration's a Spot for Fun in a Fishbowl," *Orlando Sentinel*, 20 Dec. 1996, Osceola §, at 1.

 - "The seminar's two ‘*facilitators*'—doctors who are present only to nudge the deliberations in certain directions—ease the students through the various options, trying to get them to understand that it is not enough for a doctor to simply diagnose DKA caused by patient noncompliance." Jim Atkinson, "So Much to Learn, So Little Time," *Texas Monthly*, Jan. 1997, at 118.

As H.W. Fowler and others have noted, it's better to write that an *action* rather than the *actor* is facilitated \(*FMEU1* at 164\). Hence *The safe passage of refugees must be facilitated by the international community*, not *\*Refugees must be facilitated in their safe passage by the international community*. Of course, using active voice and plain language facilitates framing a better sentence: *The international community must ensure the safe passage of refugees*. See passive voice.
