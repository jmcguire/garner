# Tartar, Tater

Tartar; **Tatar**. These are historically the same word, the first borrowed into Middle English from medieval Latin, the second reintroduced into English from Persian or Turkish in the mid-1800s. The term refers to (1) a member of the Mongolian and Turkic peoples who, led by Genghis Khan (1202–1227), invaded central and western Asia, as well as eastern Europe, in the Middle Ages; (2) a descendant of those people, esp. someone whose family comes from European Russia, Crimea, or parts of Asia; or (3) any of the Turkic languages of these people. Although *Tartar* /**tahr**-tәr/ has predominated in print sources since Middle English, *Tatar* /**taht**-әr/ has become almost equally frequent since the mid-1900s, and it is considered the preferable term for ethnological references.

One reason for the decline of *Tartar* in technical references is that the word took on a derogatory sense beginning in the 1600s (often capitalized also in this sense): "a savage; a violent, tempestuous, uncontrollable person." Another reason is the etymological spuriousness of *Tartar*: in the 1300s, the Persian word *Tatar* was corrupted through false association with the Latin word *Tartarus*, meaning "hell." Hence the scholarly trend in recent decades has been to prefer *Tatar*.
