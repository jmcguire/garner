# \*these kind of

\*these kind of; **\*these type of**; **\*these sort of**. These illogical forms were not uncommon in the 1600s and early 1700s, but by the mid-1700s they had been stigmatized. Today they brand the speaker or writer as slovenly. They appear most commonly in reported speech, but sometimes not—e.g.:

 - "\[The\] Lebanon team . . . felt it was built for *these kind of challenges* \[read *this kind of challenge*\]." Mike Gross, "Berks Power Chops Down Cedars," *Patriot & Evening News* \(Harrisburg\), 6 Sept. 1997, at C1.

 - "It's just that *these sort of things* \[read *this sort of thing*\] always seem\[s\] to happen to the Angels." J.A. Adande, "Shedding His Wings," *L.A. Times*, 17 Sept. 1997, at C1.

 - "Media reporters persistently ask *these type of questions* \[read *questions of this type* or *questions like this*, etc.\]." Johnny E. Williams & David G. Embrick, "Misinterpretations of Media on the Movement," *Hartford Courant*, 14 June 2020, at 2.

Of course, it's perfectly acceptable to write *these kinds* or *these types* or *these sorts*, as many writers conscientiously do—e.g.:

 - "It's *these kinds* of stories that are scary." David Horowitz, "Fight Back: What to Do When It's Time for New Doctor," *Daily News* \(L.A.\), 26 Apr. 1997, at L16.

 - "I told my sister that setting up her own Website would allow her to propagate *these kinds* of activities." Stewart Alsop, "Alsop to Publishers: Wake Up\!," *Fortune*, 29 Sept. 1997, at 257.

See **sort of**.

Language-Change Index

*these* followed by *kind*, *type*, or *sort* in the singular: Stage 2
Current ratio in print \(*these kinds of* vs. *\*these kind of *\): 11:1
Current ratio in print \(*these types of* vs. *\*these type of *\): 32:1
