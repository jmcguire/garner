# tsar

tsar; **czar**; \***tzar**. The variable spellings of this word historically depended on the system used for transliterating the Russian to the Roman alphabet. Interestingly, some differentiation has emerged: the spelling *tsar* tends to be used in reference to a pre-1917 Russian emperor \<Tsar Nicholas II\>. It is uniformly capitalized before a proper name. The female counterpart—for either a Russian empress or the wife of a tsar—is *tsarina* \<Tsarina Elizabeth\> \<Tsarina Alexandra Feodorovna\>. Scholars in Slavic studies certainly prefer these spellings. The Russian word for *tsarina* is *tsaritsa*—which is sometimes seen in English.

For metaphorical uses originating in the late 1800s, *czar* is the usual spelling. Originally in this sense, *czar* denoted a tyrant or authoritarian ruler, but in recent decades it has become usual, especially in AmE, in reference to someone responsible for coordinating policy and overseeing its implementation \<drug czar\> \<energy czar\>. In BrE, the spelling *tsar* is often attached to this extended sense; even so, *drug czar* is more common in BrE than *\*drug tsar*.
