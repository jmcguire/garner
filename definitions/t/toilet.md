# toilet

toilet; **toilette**; **toiletry**. In old-fashioned usage, dating back to the 1600s, *toilet* denoted either the act of washing and dressing oneself <she finished her toilet> or a formal dressing table fitted with drawers and a mirror <sitting at her toilet, she combed her hair>. By the 1700s, the term referred to the reception of visitors by a lady during the last stages of her dressing. Into the 1800s, the word often denoted a woman's "get-up"—that is, her gown or costume. At the same time (1800s) especially in AmE, the term came to denote a dressing room furnished with bathing facilities—and by extension a flushable commode. In short, the word has undergone such a transformation in meaning that today it is mostly associated with excretory functions. It now generally refers either to the room in which people urinate and defecate, or to the bowl in which they do it.

As *toilet* underwent a depreciation in meaning, the gallicism *toilette* /twah-**let**/ (as far back as the late 1600s) took over the functions of referring to modes of fashionable attire or dress, or the process of dressing oneself. Meanwhile, in reference to the room, AmE speakers and writers tend to resort to a euphemism such as *restroom* (at a public place) or *bathroom* (in a house). In reference to the basin, *toilet* is more likely to appear in both AmE and BrE than *commode*; and *commode* is more likely to appear among older AmE speakers than younger ones (and highly unlikely in BrE).

*Toiletries*, meanwhile, are soaps, lotions, perfumes, hair gels, mouthwashes, toothpastes, shaving creams, and other articles for grooming and cleaning oneself. In other words, *toiletries* are associated with the room known as the *toilet* but more nearly with the older senses of grooming and dressing.

Today it's inconceivable in most varieties of English that a guest would approach a hostess and declare admiration for her *toilet*.
