# marquis

marquis /**mahr**-kwis/ (= a man who, in the British hierarchy of noble titles, has a rank between a duke and an earl), a French loanword dating in English from the 1300s, has long predominated over the variant *marquess*. Hence we have the *Marquis of Queensbury rules* in boxing, dating from the mid-1860s and essentially establishing the first modern rules of boxing (using gloves, dividing a match into rounds, etc.). \**Marquess of Queensbury rules* is a variant that occurs much less often.

Yet as the *OED* notes, *marquess* (dating from the late 1300s) remains the official term. So if you're writing to the Queen and mentioning someone bearing this title . . .

The feminine equivalent, denoting either the wife or widow of a marquis, or else a woman who holds the rank of marquis in her own right, is *marchioness* or *marquise*—the former being the usual term.

 - Current ratio in print (*Marquis of Queensbury rules* vs. \**Marquess of Queensbury rules*): 4:1
 - Current ratio in print (*Marquis of Rockingham* vs. *Marquess of Rockingham*): 1.8:1
 - Current ratio in print (*Marquis of Argyll* vs. *Marquess of Argyll*): 2:1
 - Current ratio in print (*created marquis* vs. *created marquess*): 1.1:1
 - Current ratio in print (*created Marchioness* vs. *created Marquise*): 1.9:1
