# muckety-muck

muckety-muck; **muck-a-muck**; \***mucky-muck**; **\*muckamuck**; **\*muckymuck**. The original phrase, a mid-1800s Americanism adapted through hobson-jobsonism from the Chinook phrase *hayo makamak* "plenty to eat," was *high muck-a-muck*. Today the *high* is usually dropped, although it admittedly adds something, by an accident of sound, to the sense "an important person who is also self-important." The predominant term was *muck-a-muck* \(also spelled \**muckamuck*\) through most of the 20th century. But an epenthetic syllable, perhaps on the model of *clickety-clack*, arose in the 1950s, and by the mid-1990s *muckety-muck* had gained preeminence in print sources.

 - Current ratio in print \(*muckety-muck* vs. \**mucky-muck* vs. *muck-a-muck* vs. \**muckamuck* vs. \**muckymuck*\): 13:9:7:4:1
