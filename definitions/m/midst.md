# midst

midst; **mist**. To be *in the midst of* an event or situation is to be right in the middle of it <the President has been in the midst of a firestorm>; to be *in the midst of* a place, a group of things, or a group of people is to be surrounded <in the midst of ornate buildings> <in the midst of such a distinguished group of honorees>. For more, see **amid \(b\)**.

To be *in the mist* is to be covered in a foggy, watery cloud <the dales were shrouded in the mist>. Figuratively, *mist* appears in the phrase *in the mists of time*—idiomatically in the plural \(not \**in the mist of time*\)—meaning "so long ago as to be irretrievably forgotten."

As you can see, the words *midst* and *mist* are quite distinct. Yet people sometimes misuse *mist* for *midst*, as by writing \**in the mist of this crisis*.

Current ratio in print *\(in the midst of this* vs. \**in the mist of this*\): 533:1
Current ratio in print \(*mists of time* vs. \**mist of time*\): 13:1
