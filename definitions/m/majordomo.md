# majordomo

majordomo (= \[1\] someone in charge of the servants in a large house; or \[2\] someone who takes charge in representing another by making arrangements, commenting publicly, etc.), imported into English in the 1500s, derives from either Spanish (*mayerdomo*) or Italian (*magiordomo*). Until the 1950s, the term was usually hyphenated in all strains of English, but since then it has been predominantly solid in AmE. In BrE, the term is more frequently hyphenated (*major-domo*), but the solid form has been in close contention since the 1920s. The plural ends in *-os*, not *-oes*. See plurals (d).

 - Current ratio in print (*majordomo* vs. *major-domo* in AmE): 4:1
 - Current ratio in print (*major-domo* vs. *majordomo* in BrE): 1:1.2
