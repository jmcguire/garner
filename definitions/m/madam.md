# madam

madam; **madame**. The word *madam* /**mad**-әm/ is a curious one with meanings both high and low. As a formal word, it is used to address a woman politely, especially a customer in a shop \<may I help you, Madam?\>. In AmE, the tendency is to use the syncopated form *ma'am* /mam/ in such a context, especially in the South. The term is also used as a form of address for a woman who holds an important official position \<Madam Secretary\> \<Madam Ambassador\>. In BrE, the term can bear a sarcastic sense in reference to a presumptuous young girl who expects to be pampered \<isn't *she* a proper little madam?\>. Then there is the really low sense: *madam* can also denote a brothel-keeper.

In Francophone countries, *Madame* /mә-**dahm**/ is traditionally used as a form of address for a married woman—*mademoiselle* being reserved for unmarried women. (See **mademoiselle**.) *Madame* is also used in place of all equivalent foreignisms (e.g., the German *Frau* or the Dutch *Mevrouw*) if the speaker or writer doesn't know the appropriate term in a given language. Again, however, since the late 1800s *madame* has also been used in the low sense of a brothel-keeper.

The plurals of the two words are *madams* /**mad**-әmz/ and *mesdames* /may-**dahm**/. Since the late 1700s, *Mesdames* has been the predominant plural of the title *Mrs.* \<Mesdames Grogan, Lee, and Green hosted the tea\>. That is, the gallicism *mesdames* has tended to serve as the plural for the formal or high senses of *madam*. The plural *madams* has tended to be associated with the prostitution-related sense \<three madams were arrested and convicted of keeping houses of ill fame\>. *Madams* is rarely used (in fact, would be incorrect) as a title: \**Madams Baird, Durden, and Eason* would almost never appear in preference to *Mesdames Baird, Durden, and Eason*.

Giacomo Puccini's 1903 opera *Madama Butterfly* (the Italian title based on John Luther Long's short story *Madame Butterfly*) is predominantly rendered *Madame Butterfly* in English, not \**Madam Butterfly*. Many productions in English-speaking countries retain the Italian title.
