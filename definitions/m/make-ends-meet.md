# make ends meet

make ends meet (= to have enough money to provide for one's necessities of life, esp. just enough to avoid ruin), a phrasal verb dating from the 1600s, in the early days was a reference to making both ends of the year meet—that is, to survive into the next year. Soon after the phrase rose to widespread popularity, in the mid-1800s, the calendar metaphor was lost on most people, even if they understood that it had something to do with joining one thing (a bank balance?) with another (actual expenditures?). In the early 1970s, some writers began mincing the phrase into \**make ends meat*—a mistake that has gradually spread (though not too far) since then.

 - Current ratio in print (*to make ends meet* vs. \**to make ends meat*): 1,414:1
