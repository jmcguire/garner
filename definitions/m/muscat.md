# muscat

muscat; **muscatel**; **muscadelle**; **muscadine**; **muscadet**. All but the last of these terms refer to grapes or wine or both. *Muscat* may refer to (1) a family of grapes that have a musky scent, including many varieties used to make wine and raisins, or (2) a wine made from a muscat grape, esp. a sweet or fortified white wine. A *muscatel* is (1) a white muscat grape cultivated in Mediterranean countries and used to make wine, (2) a wine made from such grapes, (3) in the U.S., a fortified wine made from such grapes, or (4) raisins made from muscat grapes of any color. This term is sometimes written as \**muscatal*. A *muscadelle* is a white wine grape that is not in the muscat family. It is grown primarily in France and has a sweet fragrance. A *muscadine* is a grape that is native to the U.S., sometimes cultivated but often growing wild (especially in the southern states) and used to make many food products, from wine to jellies. Only the term *muscadet* (/**mәs**-kә-day/ or /mәs-kә-**day**/) does not refer to the grape from which the wine is made; it is unrelated to muscat grapes. Instead, the French noun refers to the musky or melon taste that the white wine is reputed to have (though many find no such flavors). Muscadet? It's for you to say.
