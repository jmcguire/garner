# mislead > misled > misled

mislead > misled > misled. So inflected. As with the verb *lead*, the past forms of *mislead* are sometimes mistakenly rendered—e.g.:

 - "Don't be *mislead* \[read *misled*\] by the inflated score." Jason Cohn, "Joseph Weathers Attack Until Toronto Storms to 7–4 Victory," *Toronto Star*, 14 Dec. 2000, Sports §, at 1.

 - "It is important that the voting public not be *mislead* \[read *misled*\]." Rosalie Pedalino Porter, "Debate on Bilingual Education Should Correct Misconceptions," *Telegram & Gaz.* \(Worcester\), 2 Sept. 2001, at C2.

 - "Hay said many small business customers have complained that they were *mislead* \[read *misled*\] by telemarketers for NOS." "Hay Plans to Contest Company's Actions," *Las Vegas Rev.-J.*, 11 July 2002, at D2.

Cf. **lead**.

## Language-Change Index

*mislead* misused for the past-tense *misled*: Stage 1
Current ratio in print \(*were misled by* vs. *\*were mislead by*\): 86:1
