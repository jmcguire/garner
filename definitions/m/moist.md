# moist

moist (= moderately wet, often with warmth or humidity), an Anglo-Norman word dating from the 1300s, was originally, in Middle English (1100–1499) and Early Modern English (1500–1699), a near-synonym of *damp*. Gradually, though, it appreciated in meaning to become more neutral, suggesting a lesser degree of wetness and greater warmth. But today it is widely disliked because of its associations with bodily fluids and because some people have been socialized to feel disgust at it. In April 2016, a paper published by a cognitive psychologist at Oberlin College found that fully 20% of Americans have an aversion to it. (See **word aversion** \[p. 1248\].) So if the word underwent melioration during the 1700s and 1800s, it underwent pejoration again in the 1900s and 2000s. (See **melioration** \[p. 1221\] & **pejoration** \[p. 1230\].)
