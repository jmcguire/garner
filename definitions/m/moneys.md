# moneys

moneys; \***monies**. The first is the more logical and the editorially preferred form, but *\*monies* is a frequent variant. Their history is interesting. *\*Monies* (an archaic plural similar to *\*attornies*) was prevalent from 1750 to 1830. *Moneys* swelled into predominance from 1830 until 1975—at one point during that period achieving a 7-to-1 advantage in comparative word frequency. Although 20th-century usage authorities almost unanimously recommended *moneys* (and thought the question settled), in 1980 or so the two forms once again became close contenders—to the dismay of many traditionalists. See plurals (e).

But why do we need a plural at all? Why doesn't the collective noun *money* suffice? The answer lies in idiom. While *money* generally functions in collective senses <we made a lot of money on that deal>, *moneys* is frequently used, especially in financial and legal contexts, to denote "discrete sums of money" or "funds" <many federal and state moneys were budgeted for the disaster relief>.

 - Current ratio in print (*moneys* vs. \**monies*): 1:2.1
