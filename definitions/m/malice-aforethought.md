# malice aforethought

malice aforethought. This phrase, denoting evil intent or extreme recklessness in disregard of human life, dates from the 1600s as an anglicization of the old legal gallicism *malice prepense* or *malice purpense*. *Aforethought* here functions as a postpositive adjective that originally described premeditation. It's the same *afore-* that's found in such legalisms as *aforementioned* and *aforesaid*.

Because *aforethought* is such an unusual word—almost never appearing outside the phrase *malice aforethought*—some writers have erred with it. Since the mid-1800s, writers have occasionally written \**malice and forethought* or \**malice of forethought*. These are essentially mondegreens.

 - Current ratio in print (*malice aforethought* vs. \**malice and forethought* vs. \**malice of forethought*): 79:1:1
