# manuscript

manuscript. The etymology of this term refers to a handwritten document \[L. *manu-* "hand" \+ *script* "written"\]. But today the term most frequently refers to a word-processed (or typewritten) document. Although Eric Partridge urged a bright-line distinction between a *manuscript* and a *typescript* (see *U&A* at 179), scholars and antiquarian booksellers are the only people who observe it, and today *typescript* is probably obsolescent. The phrase *typed manuscript* (technically an oxymoron) arose in BrE in the 1890s, was adopted in AmE by 1905, and spread throughout World Englishes over the course of the 20th century.

The abbreviation *ms*. shouldn't be written *Ms.*; nor should it be italicized. Pl. *mss*.
