# mentee

mentee (= protégé; a person who has a mentor) arose in the mid-1960s to correspond to *mentor* (which itself dates from the mid-1700s). The main oddity about the pair is that unlike most pairs ending in *-or* and *-ee*, these are not from a verb stem. That is, almost every other pair derives from a verb—*grantor–grantee*, *licensor–licensee*, *mortgagor–mortgagee*, and so on. But there is no verb *\*to ment*. Rather, *mentor* derives from the eponym *Mentor*, the name of a guide or adviser in Homer's *Odyssey*. Yet the *mentor*–*mentee* correlatives have become established as vogue terms, especially in education and business—e.g.:

 - "One major candidate from a major university had an extremely distinguished sponsor who was apparently intent on emphasizing his special closeness to the *mentee*." David Damrosch, "Mentors and Tormentors in Doctoral Education," *Chron. of Higher Educ.*, 17 Nov. 2000, at B24.

 - "But Spane has managed to fashion a flip-flop in their mentor–*mentee* relationship." Linda Helser, "Client Turns Teacher for Mentor," *Ariz. Republic*, 13 Dec. 2000, at 1.

 - "Mentors and *mentees* should talk on the phone at least once a week and get together at least once a month." "Mentors Needed for Teens in Phillipsburg," *Star-Ledger* (Newark), 11 Jan. 2001, at 3.

The alternative term, *protégé*, may sound affected, but *mentee* is hardly an improvement.
