# more perfect

more perfect. This phrase appears in the preamble to the U.S. Constitution: "We the People of the United States, in Order to form a *more perfect* Union." Some critics object that *perfect*, as an absolute quality, should not take a comparative adjective. The answer to those critics is an old one: "It is pedantic to object to the colloquial use of such expressions as ‘more universal' \[and\] ‘*more perfect*' . . . . Of course, superficially viewed, these expressions are incorrect, as there cannot be degrees of universality or of perfection . . . ; yet what is really meant by ‘more perfect,' for example, is ‘more nearly perfect'." Harry T. Peck, "What Is Good English?" in *What Is Good English? and Other Essays* 3, 16–17 (1899). See adjectives (b).

Although we tend to think of the phrase as having been coined for the Constitution, in fact it was in general currency during the 1600s. Its idiomatic standing was never questioned until the 1800s.
