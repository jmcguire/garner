# influence-peddlers

influence-peddlers. This term should refer to the politicians who sell their influence, not to the people who try to buy it. Yet many politicians get it backward—e.g.: "He \[Patrick J. Buchanan\] even updated his stump graphics with a giant mock check at his side made out for hundreds of millions of dollars to the major parties and signed *Influence Peddlers* \[read, perhaps, *Special Interests*\]." Francis X. Clines, "Buchanan Wraps Himself in McCain's Flag of Reform," *N.Y. Times*, 17 Mar. 2000, at A17.

The distinction is hardly a fine line, though. Officeholders and government officials often avail themselves of the "revolving door" option to make more money as private-sector lobbyists. But they still retain some influence through friendships and political connections and could be seen as peddling that advantage.
