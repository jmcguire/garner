# indict

indict; **indite**. Both words are pronounced /in-**dīt**/. The first means "to charge formally with a crime"; the second, "to write, compose, dictate." A literary term, *indite* has largely fallen into disuse since the mid-20th century. But it isn't unknown—e.g.:

 - "Its principles hold for drafting a policy memorandum to the head of a business firm or for *inditing* a letter to a friend." Gorham Munson, *The Written Word* 9 \(rev. ed. 1949\).

 - "I would take it to one of my dupes who would *indite* a blackmail demand to the young man's father." Donald Thomas, "The Case of the Talking Corpse," in *Sherlock Holmes and the Voice from the Crypt* 223, 318 \(2002\).

 - "Max, who supposedly *indites* these confessions in the year 1930, . . . has survived a number of the young century's crises." John Updike, "Mind/Body Problem," *New Yorker*, 26 Jan. 2004, at 90.

Cf. **extradite**.
