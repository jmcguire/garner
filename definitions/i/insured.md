# insured

insured, n., like *deceased* and *accused*, forms an awkward plural \<several insureds\> and possessive \<the insured's liability\>. This infelicity can be avoided in one of two ways: (1) by using *insured* as an adjective \<both insured representatives\> or (2) by using an equivalent term \<both insurants\> \<the policyholder's claim\>. If you are using *insured* as a noun, though, try an *of*-genitive instead of a possessive \<the liability of the insured\>. See possessives (j).

Understand, too, that the plural or possessive of *insured* is less jolting than that of *accused* or *deceased*, both of which have an *s* more nearby. That's probably why *insureds* (pl.) and *insured's* (possessive) have been much more common than their counterparts since the early 20th century.
