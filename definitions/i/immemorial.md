# immemorial

immemorial (= beginning longer ago than anyone can remember or than anyone recorded), a Latin loanword dating in English (as the records show) from the early 1600s, occurs typically in the phrase *time immemorial*. Since about 1980, the phrase has occasionally been transmuted into the mondegreen \**time in memorial*.

In legal terminology, *time immemorial* refers to a point in time so far back that no living person has knowledge or proof contradicting the right or custom alleged to have existed since then. At common law, this time was fixed at 1189, the year when Henry II of England died. For many centuries, English-speaking lawyers have also expressed the idea by saying "the memory of man runneth not to the contrary."

 - Current ratio in print (*time immemorial* vs. \**time in memorial*): 943:1
