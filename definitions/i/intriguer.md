# intriguer

intriguer; **intrigant**; **intrigante**; \***intriguant**; **\*intriguante**. These words have been the source of much linguistic, well, intrigue. *Intrigant* /in-tree-**gahnt**/ (= a man given to secret plots or machinations to harm others), a French loanword dating in English from the mid-1700s, was most commonly spelled **intriguant* until the 1910s. Since then, *intrigant* has been standard. Interestingly, though, since 1830 the feminine form *intrigante* (for a woman who similarly plots), or its earlier variant \**intriguante*, has occurred in print with even greater frequency than its masculine equivalent. Even so, an erudite British correspondent swears that you could walk back and forth several times from Land's End to the northernmost outposts of Scotland without hearing the word. The fact is that *intriguer* has always been the more common English word—so forget about the gallicism *intrigant* and its variants.

 - Current ratio in print (*intriguer* vs. *intrigante* vs. *intrigant* vs. \**intriguant* vs. \**intriguante*): 38:4:1.5:1.4:1
