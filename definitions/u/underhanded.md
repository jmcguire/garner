# underhanded

underhanded; **underhand**, adj. The shorter form is much older <underhand dealings>—having become established in the early 1700s—but *underhanded* spread in the 1800s and is now about twice as common. It must be accepted as standard—e.g.: "Partisans accused each other of unnecessary delay and *underhanded* negotiating tactics." Jeff Mayers & Mike Flaherty, "Senate GOP Could OK Budget Deal," *Wis. State J.*, 20 Sept. 1997, at B1. Increasingly, *underhand* is confined to literal senses <because he hurt his shoulder, the tennis champion is temporarily having to use an underhand serve>, where there is no suggestion of unsportsmanlike deceit.

*underhanded* for *underhand*: Stage 5
