# \*used to could

\*used to could is dialectal for *used to be able to* or *could formerly*. It appears mostly in reported speech—e.g.:

 - " ‘I was a lot stronger back then,' Webb said. ‘I *used to could* \[read *once could* or *used to be able to*\] take a 100-pound bag and lift it over my head.' " Melissa Devaughn, "It Was a Grind, but Floyd County Miller Loved His Work," *Roanoke Times*, 23 Oct. 1994, at 19.

 - " ‘You *used to could* \[read *used to be able to*\] read all the liner notes and see who wrote the songs and who played on whose album,' he said." Lori Buttars, "Silver Tongues Joe Flint," *Salt Lake Trib.*, 23 June 1996, at J1.

 - "‘I *used to could* \[read *used to be able to*\] rattle it off, knew the answers like the back of my hand,' he said." Ed Grisamore, "Longtime High School Football Coach ‘Coming to Grips' with Dementia," *Macon Telegraph*, 3 Aug. 2014, at 965.

Dating from the early 1800s in AmE, the phrase had spread to BrE by the late 1830s. It has never been standard english. See dialect & double modals.
