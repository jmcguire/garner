# annoyance

annoyance; **\*annoyment**. The first has always been the standard term in Modern English. The second, when used with a straight face, is worse than a needless variant—it's a nonword that is itself what it denotes. E.g.: "Even into July, Howe had not stirred. Nor had Washington been active, to Adams's considerable *annoyment* \[read *annoyance*\]." John E. Ferling, *John Adams: A Life* 178–79 \(1996\). Occasionally it appears as a jocular antonym that echoes *enjoyment*—e.g.: "So here, for your *enjoyment/annoyment*: What new director found his preteen daughter's pot stash moments before his big film's premiere party, and what critic took the rap for it?" Michael Musto, "La Dolce Musto," *Village Voice*, 4 Feb. 1997, at 30.
