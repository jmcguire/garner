# amid

amid.

**A. And *among*.** *Amid* usually connotes position—e.g.: "*Amid* the public tributes, the one from Felix Frankfurter stood out." Barry Siegel, *Claim of Privilege* 186 \(2008\). *Among* often connotes a mingling—e.g.: "To see the true \[Tom\] Coughlin, watch him *among* friends and family, away from football." John Branch, "Coughlin's Playful Side Has Deep Roots in Florida," *N.Y. Times*, 6 May 2008, at D1.

**B. And *amidst*; *in the midst of*; *mid*; *'mid*.** *Amid* and *amidst* are slightly quaint words, especially the latter. Often the word *in* or *among* serves better. \(But see **among \(b\)**.) Since the mid-1800s, *amid* has predominated in AmE and BrE alike. Today it is about twice as common as *amidst*.

*In the midst of*, a wordy equivalent, has always been more common than *amid*. It often lends a better cadence, as in these titles:

• David Freeman Hawke, *In the Midst of a Revolution* \(1961\).

• Louis Breger, *Freud: Darkness in the Midst of Vision* \(2000\).

• Geoffrey Warner, *In the Midst of Events: The Foreign Office Diaries and Papers of Kenneth Younger* \(2005\).

See **midst**.

The preposition *mid* is poetic in all uses except traditional compounds \(e.g., *mid-1800s*, *midnight*, *midstream*\) or scientific uses; if the word is appropriate, however, *mid* is better than *'mid*.
