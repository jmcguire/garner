# about

about.

A. And *approximately*. When possible, use *about* instead of *approximately*, a formal word often intended to lend a scientific air to prose. But it sounds pseudoscientific and pretentious when it appears in ordinary contexts.

**B. And *around*.** When there is a choice between *about* and *around*—as in *beat around* \(or *about*\) *the bush*, *strewn around* \(or *about*\) *the garden*, or *all around* \(or *about*\) *the city*—the word *around* greatly predominates in AmE. *About* is more commonly found in BrE.

**C. *About the head*.** Theodore M. Bernstein called this phrase "police-blotter lingo" \(*The Careful Writer* at 5\) when used in the sense "on" <the victim was pounded several times about the head>. The phrase might still be common in police blotters, but in print it appears only occasionally—e.g.: "A Malaysian companion, 15, suffered a punctured eardrum from the interrogator's blows *about the head*." William Safire, "Singapore Adds Insult to Injury," *Star Trib.* \(Minneapolis\), 24 May 1994, at A15.

**D. *At about*.** This phrase is sometimes criticized as a redundancy, the argument being that *about* can often do the work by itself. It often can, but in many contexts, especially those involving expressions of time, the phrase *at about* is common, idiomatic, and unimpeachable <we'll arrive at about 9:00 tonight>.
