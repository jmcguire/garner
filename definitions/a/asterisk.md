# asterisk

asterisk /**as**-tә-risk/. The mispronunciation of this word, as if it had no second *s*, is well known. \(See pronunciation \(b\).\) Not so well known are the resulting misspellings of the word. The most common one is *\*asterick*—e.g.:

 - "Speaking of Khannouchi, when he and Belgian Eddy Hellebuyck become U.S. citizens this fall, will the critics of American distance running put *astericks* \[read *asterisks*\] next to their performances . . . ?" Steve Nearman, "Chicago, New York Battle for Top Marathon Runners," *Wash. Times*, 9 Aug. 1998, at C8.

 - "Entries marked with the *asterick* \[read *asterisk*\] \(\*\) did not run in the Sebastian River edition." "Crimewatch," *Fla. Today*, 4 May 1999, at B4.

 - "*Astericks* \[read *Asterisks*\] appear for schools that are new and did not have test results from last year." "Reading the Charts," *San Diego Union-Trib.*, 28 July 1999, at A20.

Another misspelling, *\*asterik*, is nearly as common—e.g.: "The most memorable score in the 80-year history of the NFL—Chicago 73, Washington 0 in the 1940 championship game—always has deserved an exclamation point. Now it has got an *asterik* \[read *asterisk*\]." Bob Broeg, "59 Years Later, Baugh Casts Doubt on Bears' Rout," *St. Louis Post-Dispatch*, 5 Dec. 1999, at D1.
