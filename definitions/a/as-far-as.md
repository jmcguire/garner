# as far as

as far as. In its figurative uses, this phrase must be followed by some complement such as *that's concerned*, *that goes*, or *I know*—e.g.: "*As far as* they're concerned, January 1, 2000, will bring enough of a hangover." Neil Randall, "Welcome to the Millennium," *PC Mag.*, 23 Sept. 1997, at 229.

When the complement is omitted, idiom is violated. This seems to happen most often in reported speech—e.g.: " ‘*As far as* Ron Lynn \[insert *is concerned*\], I have no idea what the inner workings of \[his\] team are.' " Lee Shappell, "Miracle Win over Redskins Revived Cardinals in '96," *Ariz. Republic*, 12 Sept. 1997, at C1 \(quoting Vince Tobin, a coach\). Having first been noted by Robert L. Chapman in "Trouble with the Pointing Phrase," 44 *Am. Speech* 305 \(1969\), this error is becoming fairly widespread in spoken English.

Idiom aside, however, *as far as* usually signals verbosity. So instead of *As far as he is concerned,* it's possible to save half the words by writing *As for him, . . . .* See **insofar as**.
