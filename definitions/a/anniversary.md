# anniversary

anniversary \(= the day of the year on which an event occurred in a previous year\) is today used informally to denote a milestone in months or even weeks. That usage has become increasingly common since the late 1930s, perhaps because there is no convenient equivalent for terms shorter than a year \(*milestone* is close, but it doesn't connote observance and recurrence the way *anniversary* does\). Given the word's tight association with "year," however, this loose usage is subject to criticism and should be avoided if possible—e.g.:

 - "So, how's he doing *at the one-month anniversary of his arrival* \[read *a month after arriving*\] in Richmond?" Margaret Edds, "Shucet Steers Troubled Roads Department on a Straight Course," *Virginian-Pilot* \(Norfolk\), 19 May 2002, at J5.

 - "The only other week in 2002 when terrorism was the top evening news story came during the six-month *anniversary* \[read *remembrance*\] of the New York and Washington attacks." Mark Jurkowitz, "News Media Try to Give Public Fair Warnings," *Boston Globe*, 30 May 2002, at D6.
