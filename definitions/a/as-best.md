# as best

as best; \***as** best as. The traditional idiom is *as best she can*, meaning "in the best way that she can." The *as* isn't part of a comparative *as . . . as* construction, which takes the positive form of the adjective \(*as good as*\), not the superlative \(*best*\). So adding a second *as* is incorrect—e.g.:

 - "But in the meantime, Michelle combed his coat *as best as she* \[read *as best she*\] could and adorned him yesterday with a pink ribbon and a yellow bandanna around his neck." Celeste Tarricone, "Lost Six Weeks, Sebastian Is Back in the Family Fold," *Providence J.-Bull.*, 10 June 1998, at C1.

 - "Good dog owners try to discipline their dogs *as best as they* \[read *as best they*\] know how, but I don't think people give us a chance." D. Andrich, "Dog Bias," *Chicago Trib.*, 26 Sept. 1998, at 20.

 - "He raised himself *as best as he* \[read *as best he*\] could on his hands and knees and crawled out of the store." Bruce Von Deylen, "Store Owner Felt Remorse, Fear After Shooting," *South Bend Trib.*, 31 Jan. 1999, at A1.

## Language-Change Index

 - *\*as best as he can* for *as best he can*: Stage 1
 - Current ratio in print \(*as best he can* vs. *\*as best as he can*\): 9:1
