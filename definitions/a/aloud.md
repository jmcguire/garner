# aloud

aloud; **out loud**. The latter is colloquial when used in place of the former in expressions such as *read out loud*. Because of this—and because *read aloud* is much more common than *read out loud* in print—*read aloud* should be preferred in edited prose. E.g.:

• "McGuffey's fifth and sixth readers had an abundance of the kind of poetry that demands to be read *out loud* \[read *aloud*\], like ‘The Raven' by Edgar Allan Poe." Diane Ravitch, "Children's Books," *N.Y. Times*, 17 May 1987, § 7, at 46.

• "Oprah loves writing that begs to be read *out loud* \[read *aloud*\]." Marilyn Johnson, "Oprah Winfrey: A Life in Books," *Life*, Sept. 1997, at 44.

Current ratio in print \(*read aloud* vs. *read out loud*\): 8:1
