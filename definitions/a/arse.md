# arse

arse; **ass**. *Arse* is the spelling of the BrE slang term—in the anatomical sense, that is, not in the horse sense. In AmE, *ass* is the spelling for both meanings.

There's a story behind this. Today *ass* means both \(1\) "donkey," and \(2\) "a person's bottom." Sense 1 is the historical one; sense 2 originated in the mid-1700s as the result of a phonological change, as *arse* and *ass* became homophones. By the early 1800s, it was possible to engage in wordplay between the words—as in the 1802 cartoon in which one female rider says to another, "I'll show my Ass against any Lady's at Wells" \("Neddy Paces at Tunbridge Wells," Kent\). See Tony Fairman, "How the Ass Became a Donkey," 10 *English Today* 29–35 \(1994\).
