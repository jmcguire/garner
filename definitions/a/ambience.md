# ambience

ambience; ambiance. These words denote the atmosphere of a place. *Ambience* /**am**-bee-әn\[t\]s/ is an anglicized form that entered the language in the late 1800s \(with a lone outlier from 1648\). It's preferable to *ambiance* /**ahm**-bee-ahn\[t\]s/, a Frenchified affectation that, since its proliferation in the mid-20th century, has become a vogue term.

In modern print, *ambience* is used more than twice as often as *ambiance*. Though the *New York Times* style manual specifies *ambience*, its editors \(like all other editors\) have occasionally stumbled—e.g.: "Ratings reflect the reviewer's reaction to food, *ambiance* \[read *ambience*\] and service with price taken into consideration." "What Lies Beneath: Serious Mexican Food," *N.Y. Times*, 11 Sept. 2002, at F6.
