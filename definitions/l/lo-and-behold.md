# lo and behold

lo and behold. The word *lo* derives from Old English \(450–1099\) as an interjection that directs attention to the presence or approach of something <Lo\! Here come the clouds.>. The jocular phrase *lo and behold*, according to the *OED*, dates back to at least the first decade of the 1800s. It caught on quickly and has been common ever since. Given that *lo* is essentially synonymous with an imperative *behold*, the phrase qualifies as an instance of synonymous doubling \(*day and age*, *fit and proper*, etc.\). Hence *lo and behold* is something of a popular redundancy—though arguably the second part \(*behold*\) adds meaning by exhorting the listener not just to take note but to gaze upon the thing mentioned, to give it some thought.

Since the 1880s, some have mistakenly written \**low and behold*, a blunder that began in AmE and spread \(some 40 years later\) to BrE. To *low* is to moo like a cow. Not until the turn of the 21st century, however, did this howler spread much. E.g.:

 - "*Low and behold* \[read *Lo and behold*\], she's back." Taylor Ferber, "Cancel Culture Won't Change Cyberbully Teigen," *News-Star* \(Monroe, La.\), 20 June 2021, at B3.

 - "The Ewart Library acquired a box of old papers that had belonged to Primrose and Gordon, and *low and behold* \[read *lo and behold*\] it contained the whole story." "Mystery of Dumfries Boy Who Inherited a Fortune," *Dumfries & Galoway Standard* \(U.K.\), 6 Aug. 2021, at 16.

 - "*Low and behold* \[read *Lo and behold*\] we canned 28 quarts in two days." Melodie Davis, "August Bounty," *Daily American* \(Somerset, Pa.\), 20 Aug. 2021.

Some might assert that these regional newspapers can hardly signal much of anything, but that's wrong. There's a *reason* for including so many far-flung sources in this book: they truly show what's happening in the written language. Besides, it was a mistranscription in *The New York Times* \(in prominent type\) that, in August 2021, alerted me to this error. Lo and behold, it turns out that the error grew much more pronounced in print sources internationally after 2000.
