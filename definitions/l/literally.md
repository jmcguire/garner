# literally

literally \(= \[1\] with truth to the letter, or in a literal way; \[2\] exactly; according to the strict sense of the word or words; \[3\] truly, really, virtually, completely, or absolutely; or \[4\] not really, but isn't it fun to imagine it?\) has become a linguistic hot spot in senses 3 and 4. The literal \(ahem\) uses of the word \(senses 1 and 2\) cause no problem, as when a contortionist is said to have "literally tied herself up in a knot," a grief-stricken survivor whose aorta suddenly burst is said to have "literally died of a broken heart," or a reader is said to "interpret texts literally." It's also unobjectionable to say that an NBA player is "literally seven feet tall" or that there were "literally hundreds of people" at a wedding attended by more than 200 guests. These are the most traditional uses of the term.

Sense 3 expresses emphasis, as we might use *really* or *truly*: "he was literally huge," or "the problem is literally tiny." When the word is so used, its use isn't subject to serious criticism because the meaning conveyed by the word is still within its semantic ambit. But it is often superfluous in this sense—e.g.: "With *literally* \[delete *literally*\] hundreds of shops, from humble food trucks to exquisitely highbrow dining concepts, Phoenix holds its own as a culinary contender." Kaely Monahan, "Is the Best Mexican Food in Phoenix or Tucson?," *Ariz. Republic*, 13 Apr. 2022, at Z8.

Sense 4, the problematic use, introduces a rank overstatement that is immediately recognizable as a nonliteral exaggeration: \**he is literally the size of a house*, \**there are literally millions of reasons you shouldn't attend that college*, \**they were literally glued to their seats* \(when they were merely interested and no adhesive was involved\), or *\*he stole the bike literally from under our noses*. These uses are self-evidently nonliteral. The potential humor of the self-contradiction was captured in a 1977 *New Yorker* cartoon by Lee Lorenz, in which one character says to another, "Confound it, Hawkins, when I said I meant that *literally*, it was just a figure of speech."

Unless there is shoving, a callous landlord isn't *literally pushing tenants out of their homes*; without a rocket launch, a baseball isn't *literally projected into the stratosphere*; and without proof, an oil spill doesn't *literally contaminate the universe*.

In sense 4—highly objectionable to so many—the word is sometimes said to mean *figuratively*. But it certainly isn't displacing *figuratively*: nobody would say *there are figuratively millions of reasons you shouldn't attend that college*. English speakers don't introduce a figure of speech with the adverb *figuratively*. Hence "correcting" *literally* to *figuratively* in such contexts is pedantic nonsense.

But that's not to say that the sense-4 crowd stands on firm ground. In addition to distracting literalists, a hyperbolic statement "strengthened" by *literally* is often actually weakened by it. Whether it's a senator saying that the President is \**literally gaslighting the American people* or some other exaggeration, the intensifier that instantly belies itself is rarely helpful—e.g.:

 - "He *literally* bombed the Cougars for 52 points." Tom Krasovic, "Aztecs Thinking Aerial Rout Is the Way to Go," *San Diego Union-Trib.*, 22 Sept. 1992, at C1. \(Delete *literally*.\)

 - "For Chip Sullivan, former club professional turned PGA Tour pro, life *literally* has been turned upside down." Randy King, "PGA Life Different Than Being Home on the Range," *Roanoke Times*, 15 Jan. 1997, at B1. \(Delete *literally*. On the use of *different than* in that headline, see **different \(a\)**.)

 - "Lives are *literally* at stake." "Baltimore Gets Serious About Helping Homeless People," *Baltimore Sun*, 28 Feb. 2022, Editorial §. \(Delete *literally*.\)

 - "The best overall smartphone right now . . . checks *literally* every box out there." Alexander Maxham, "Top Ten Mother's Day Gifts–2022," *Android Headlines* \(blog\), 13 Apr. 2022. \(Delete *literally*.\)

These uses might be characterized as amateurish.

As if the oxymoron inherent in the hyperbolic *literally* weren't enough, sometimes writers double the contradiction by context—e.g.: "Empty bathtubs were *literally* overflowing with pending paperwork." Paul Morton Ganeles, "Why Universal Health Care Makes Sense," *Ariz. Daily Star*, 31 Dec. 2021, at A6. If an empty bathtub can't be overflowing with any material substance, it especially cannot be *literally overflowing*.

Sometimes the term appears in the pairing *literally and figuratively* \(or with words reversed\), a self-conscious phrasing that rarely helps matters—e.g.: "Rudy Giuliani melted down *figuratively and literally* on screen." Joe Patrice, "Lin Wood Is Having Himself a Day on Twitter," *Above the Law* \(blog\), 31 Dec. 2020. Undoubtedly, he melted down figuratively. But he didn't do so literally, unless you consider a trickling of hair dye to be a literal melting down.

Finally, the adverb *literally* is about words, not feelings. It makes no sense to say, as many have, \**This literally feels awesome*. Or *\*This literally changed the trajectory of my life*. Or \**This literally seems unreal.* In uses such as these, the word has essentially become meaningless. Literally meaningless.
