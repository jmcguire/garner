# lead

lead, n.; **lede**. The noun *lead*—not the heavy metal but the first position—dates back to Middle English \(1100–1499\). During the Modern English period \(1500 to date\), this versatile word developed many uses relating to leadership <taking the lead in the tournament> <I'll follow your lead> <the film's romantic lead> <the guitarist playing the lead> <keeping your dog on a lead>. It also developed senses relating to investigation <the witness gave us several leads>. Beginning in the 1920s, journalists used the term to denote the evocative summary at the outset of a story or the most important item in a bulletin.

In the early 1950s, or perhaps a little earlier, American journalists began using *lede*—an altered spelling supposedly to distinguish the word from its seeming homophone *lead* \(properly /led/\), used in instructions to printers \("Hey, we need a little more leading between those lines\!"\). Although this account has been disputed, the *OED* accepts it. The first known use of *lede* to denote the opening of a journalistic piece is 1951—in a newspaper in the Texas Panhandle \(*Pampa Daily News*\). By the 1970s, it was flourishing in newsrooms, but it didn't make the *OED2* \(1989\) and wasn't recorded in Merriam-Webster dictionaries until 2008. The current *OED* labels it "chiefly U.S."

The most common occurrence is in the formulation *don't bury the lede* or the accusation *you buried your lede\!* These phrases took off in the 1990s and became extremely common in the early 21st century. To bury the lede is to fail to state the main point at the outset of a piece of writing—for example, not to disclose the upshot of your message until the middle of the second paragraph. That's a journalistic sin.

And *lede* is a kind of journalistic jargon. Defenders will say it's a term of art that immediately conveys an idea that the word *lead*, on its own, doesn't. Critics will say that it's just an insider spelling that conveys nothing more than, "Hey, I'm a bona fide journalist\!" In any event, *bury the lead* remains more common than *bury the lede* in modern print—because, remember, the spelling *lead* is also used by journalists in the specialized sense.

Now for the upshot: use *lede* if you like, but understand that some among the uninitiated become irritated when they encounter it. But there's a countervailing consideration: insiders are known to get irritated when they encounter the more conventional spelling. How's that for burying the lede in this entry?
