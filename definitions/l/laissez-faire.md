# laissez-faire

laissez-faire /les-ay-**fair**/ \(= the idea that the government should allow the economy and private businesses to carry on with minimal state control or influence\), a French loanword dating in English from the early 1800s, has been predominantly so spelled since about 1870. \**Laissez faire*, \**laisser-faire*, and \**laisser faire* are variants that occur sometimes in British publications—e.g.:

 - "Should Hongkong's *laisser-faire* \[read *laissez-faire*\] government do an about-face to build Hongkong Inc?" "Farewell to Adam Smith," *Economist*, 30 Sept.–6 Oct. 1989, at 71.

 - "This is bonkers, though par for the course for a Bush administration that is all for *laisser faire* \[read *laissez-faire*\] except when US companies whine about foreign competition." Robert Peston, "The Stock Exchange Drops Its American Dream," *Sunday Telegraph*, 13 Oct. 2002, at 3.

The first word in the phrase is pronounced /les-ay/—not /lah-zay/ or /lay-zay/. See gallicisms.

Current ratio in print \(*laissez-faire* vs. \**laissez faire* vs. \**laisser-faire* vs. \**laisser faire*\): 95:18:1.2:1
