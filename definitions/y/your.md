# your

your, the possessive form of the second person, is sometimes misused for *you're*, the contraction of *you are*. Often, as in the second example below, the error occurs in a quotation:

 - "Just saying *your* \[read *you're*\] going to get fit this year doesn't mean you will." Lareta M. Tabor, "Have You Already Given Up Your New Year's Resolutions?," *Kansas City Star*, 15 Jan. 1994, at 19.

 - "Gallagher said: ‘They can accept it if *your* \[read *you're*\] older and time goes by.' " Malcolm Moran, "Some Final Goodbyes for a Fallen Fordham Player," *N.Y. Times*, 18 Oct. 1996, at B24.

 - "If *your* \[read *you're*\] hoping your early federal tax filing may lead to a quick refund, you might have to wait there, too." David P. Willis, "State Delays Early Tax Refunds," *Asbury Park Press* \(Neptune, N.J.\), 8 Jan. 2019, at A5.

The opposite error also occurs, somewhat less commonly, most often in a quotation—e.g.:

 - " ‘Sometimes the kids taunt them and say, "*You're* \[read *Your*\] father has the ninja," ' he said. ‘That hurts.' Ninja is slang for AIDS." Richard M. Nangle, "AIDS Rate High Behind Bars," *Sunday Telegram* \(Worcester\), 4 Sept. 1994, at A1 \(quoting Keith Williamson\).

 - " ‘In boxing you don't have that kind of luxury or time. If you mess up in a fight or two, *you're* \[read *your*\] career could be over.' " Maureen Landis, "Arroyo Wins Gold Gloves Title," *Lancaster New Era*, 29 May 1996, at 9 \(quoting Ernie Arroyo\).

 - "I used to say to them, which really worked, ‘Now *you're* \[read *your*\] mother didn't teach you to talk like that.' " Doug Fernandes, "Groundbreaking Career," *Sarasota Herald-Trib.*, 19 Oct. 2017, at C1 \(quoting Lesley Visser\).

See spelling \(a\).
