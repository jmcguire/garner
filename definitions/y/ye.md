# ye

ye.

**A. As Article Meaning "the.**" In phrases such as *Ye Olde Cheshire Cheese* \(reportedly Samuel Johnson's favorite pub in London\) and *Ye Olde Curiosity Shoppe*, the word *ye* is simply a variant spelling of *the*. The Middle English thorn \(þ\) represented the sound now represented in English by *th*. By 1500 or so, its form was identical to that of *y*. Hence the "proper" pronunciation of *ye* as a definite article is /thee/—not /yee/.

**B. As Archaic Second-Person Pronoun.** The personal pronoun *ye* \(= you\) was originally used, in the 900s, only as a nominative plural <ye \[you two\] have my blessing> or in apposition with a vocative noun <ye gods>. By the 1200s it had come to be used as a singular equivalent to *thou*, at first as a way of showing respect to a superior <ye have my loyalty> and as an objective pronoun <I love ye> <strange news to tell ye\!>. Quaint by the 1600s, it now survives mostly in dialect, *you* having taken over all second-person responsibilities in standard english.
