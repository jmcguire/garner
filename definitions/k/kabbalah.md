# kabbalah

kabbalah; **cabala**; **kabbala**; **kabala**; **qabalah**; **cabbala**; **qabbalah**; **qabala**; **cabbalah**; **qabbala**; **cabalah**. Mysteries abound here. How does one spell the term denoting the mystical, esoteric, originally Jewish method of interpreting the Torah and other materials held to be sacred? Although the medieval Latin spelling of the Hebrew word is *cabbala*, the historically predominant spelling in AmE and BrE alike is *cabala*. But in the 1950s, the spelling *kabbalah* sprang to prominence and, by the mid-1980s, had established itself as the standard form. Whatever the spelling, the standard pronunciation is /kә-**bahl**-ә/.

There are said to be more than 20 spellings of the term. In literature on the subject, writers suggest that these different spellings signal sectarian differences. Broadly speaking, Jewish commentators have traditionally preferred the *K*-spellings, Christians the *C*-spellings, and occultists the *Q*-spellings. See Alan Jones, *The Fools Journey* 146 (2016) (calling this distinction "a fairly quick guideline, and not always true"); Alan Silver, *Jews, Myth and History* 214 n.33 (2008) (noting that "this \[distinction\] seems to be less used" today).

The rationale for a given spelling sometimes has nothing to do with etymology or transliteration, but rather with personal whim, as illustrated here: "Although the established convention as put forth by the rabbis has Kabbalah spelled with a *K*—the rabbinic kosher stamp—I prefer Cabala because it is more to the point. The Cabala is the most feminine of the Jewish teachings; even among the esoteric works, Cabala is the ultimate hidden secret. Therefore, in my opinion, Cabala should be written with a curve, denoting the feminine, and not defined by the line of the rabbis." David Krafchow, *Kabbalistic Tarot* x n. (2005).

Another point. Is the term capitalized? Generally, practitioners of the art do so: *Kabbalah* outnumbers *kabbalah* in print sources by a 10-to-1 margin. But if the term is used in its extended sense ("any esoteric or secret doctrine"), it is most commonly lowercase—and undoubtedly the spelling tends to drift toward the traditional *cabala* in extended senses.

Adherents tend to capitalize the noun *kabbalist*, while skeptics tend not to. For the difference between *kabbalist* and *cabalist*, see **cabalist**.

As for the adjective, the traditional spelling—predominant for about 350 years, from the early 1600s through the mid-1970s—is *cabalistic*. In the broad sense "having an esoterically mysterious meaning," that spelling doubtless remains standard. But if the sense is nonmetaphorical and refers instead to the actual doctrines of *kabbalists*, the adjective *kabbalistic* tends to appear today: in print sources, it predominates over *cabalistic* by a 2:1 ratio.
